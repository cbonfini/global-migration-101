Teaching Guide

How the course is structured, how to adapt it across levels and disciplines, and how to use each component in your classroom.

Course philosophy

Global Migration 101 treats migration as a normal, historical, and global human phenomenon rather than a crisis to be managed. It is interdisciplinary by design, moving deliberately from foundations (concepts, data, drivers) through historical and structural context, systems and governance, the human experience of migration, narratives and ethics, and finally future challenges and solutions. The arc lets students build conceptual vocabulary before engaging contested policy and ethical terrain.

Teaching across levels

Each module pairs undergraduate outcomes (3–5, emphasizing definition, description, and application) with graduate outcomes (1–3, emphasizing critique, synthesis, modeling, and design). A single module can therefore anchor both an introductory survey and a graduate seminar: assign the same readings and videos, but differentiate the signature learning experience and assessment by outcome tier.

Using each component

Readings. Each module includes peer-reviewed scholarship with DOIs. For undergraduates, assign one or two anchor texts; for graduate students, assign the full set and use the annotations to scaffold a critical reading seminar.

Videos. Curated lectures, documentaries, and explainers work well as pre-class priming or in-class discussion prompts. Pair a short explainer with a primary reading to surface gaps between public narratives and scholarship.

Narrated lecture decks. Each module has a self-contained, co-branded HTML lecture deck with synthesized teaching content, instructor speaker notes, and optional AI voice narration. Open a deck and press ▶ Narrate (or the N key) for audio that advances slides automatically; press S for the speaker-notes view with instructor guidance and the full narration script; press F for fullscreen. Browse them all from the Lectures library.

Handouts. One-page faculty handouts summarize outcomes, key concepts, the signature experience, and readings — useful as a syllabus insert or a teaching prep sheet.

Knowledge checks. Each module ends with a short self-graded knowledge check students can use for retrieval practice — accessible, mobile-friendly, and usable in any LMS or browser.

Ethical teaching note

Several modules engage trauma, violence, and lived experiences of displacement. Where lived-experience or interview material is used (notably Modules 5 and 9), adopt a trauma-informed and ethical engagement stance: secure informed consent, protect anonymity where needed, avoid extractive storytelling, and give students framing before exposure to difficult material.

Suggested pacing

The course is built for a standard 14-week semester (one module per week). For a shorter term, the six arcs can be compressed into six thematic units. For a quarter system, pair Weeks 1–2, 4–5, and 13–14.
