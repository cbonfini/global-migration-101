7 Module overview

Examines how governments regulate migration through borders, immigration systems, and legal frameworks, including detention, deportation, and the tension between domestic and international law.

Skill emphasis: Legal + Analytical

◎ Learning outcomes

Undergraduate

  1. Explain the role of borders in regulating migration
  2. Identify legal frameworks and immigration policy tools
  3. Describe enforcement practices and rights issues
  4. Compare migration policies across countries

Graduate

  1. Critically analyze migration governance systems
  2. Evaluate legal tensions and policy approaches to border management
  3. Interpret real-world migration law cases

State sovereigntyborder enforcementlegal frameworks (1951 Convention, non-refoulement)detention and deportationdomestic vs international lawimmigration policy toolscomparing policies across countries

▤ Peer-reviewed readings

Show 6 readings6
  • DOIExternal Border Control Techniques in the EU as a Challenge to the Principle of Non-Refoulement
    Nagy, Boldizsár & Goldner Lang, Iris · 2021 · European Constitutional Law Review, 17(3), 476–509 · 10.1017/S1574019621000249

    Systematically analyzes how EU border practices—pushbacks, 'safe country' designations, deterrence policies—undermine non-refoulement under EU and international law. Essential for teaching the tension between state sovereignty, domestic law, and international refugee law obligations in a comparative EU context.

    Access article →
  • DOINon-Refoulement in a World of Cooperative Deterrence
    Hathaway, James C. & Gammeltoft-Hansen, Thomas · 2015 · Columbia Journal of Transnational Law, 53(2), 235–284 · 10.2139/SSRN.2479511

    Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen argue that cooperative interdiction schemes between states allow systematic non-refoulement violations by diffusing legal responsibility. A landmark article in international refugee law that illustrates how states use inter-state cooperation to evade obligations, fundamental for teaching deportation and detention policy.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Emerging Migration State
    Hollifield, James F. · 2004 · International Migration Review, 38(3), 885–912 · 10.1111/j.1747-7379.2004.tb00223.x

    Hollifield introduces the concept of the 'liberal paradox'—the tension between states' desires to control immigration and the rights-based constraints that liberal democracies impose on themselves. A foundational piece for teaching the interaction between sovereignty, domestic law, and international norms in migration governance.

    Access article →
  • DOIA European Narrative of Border Externalisation: The European Trust Fund for Africa Story
    Pacciardi, Agnese · 2024 · European Security, 33(1), 91–110 · 10.1080/09662839.2024.2304723

    Critically examines the EU Trust Fund for Africa as a case study in how development aid is weaponized as a migration-control tool, blurring the line between humanitarian and security objectives. Provides concrete policy material for teaching the 'migration-development nexus' and border externalization strategies.

    Access article →
  • DOIUnderstanding the Crisis of Refugee Law: Legal Scholarship and the EU Asylum System
    Noll, Gregor; Vedsted-Hansen, Jens; & Byrne, Rosemary · 2020 · Leiden Journal of International Law, 33(4), 871–891 · 10.1017/S0922156520000382

    Examines the widening gap between international refugee law ideals and the political-institutional realities of EU border governance. Useful for a comparative policy analysis of how sovereignty pressures have progressively restricted asylum access through legal mechanisms rather than overt refusal.

    Access article →
  • DOIContainment Instead of Refoulement: Shifting State Responsibility in the Age of Cooperative Migration Control
    Pijnenburg, Annick · 2020 · Human Rights Law Review, 20(2), 306–334 · 10.2139/ssrn.3418365

    Argues that destination states are increasingly using transit states as proxies to contain migrants, shifting—and often evading—responsibility for non-refoulement violations. Directly addresses the contemporary form of border enforcement through cooperative deterrence and complicity under international law.

    Access article →

YouTube Understanding contemporary migration and border controls in the context of colonial histories
Universitat Pompeu Fabra – Barcelona (GRITIM-UPF Seminar Series) · 2021 · 1:38:03

Dr. Lucy Mayblin (University of Sheffield) delivers an academic seminar connecting contemporary border controls to colonial histories of migration governance, arguing that current enforcement frameworks reproduce colonial logics. A theoretically sophisticated lecture for advanced Week 7 discussion.

YouTube Deportation Law and Practice: A Global Perspective
Harvard Law School Human Rights Program · 2021 · 56:30

Harvard Law School panel with scholars from multiple countries examines deportation law, practice, and human rights implications across jurisdictions. Directly relevant to Week 7 topics of enforcement, detention, and deportation as policy instruments.

YouTube Governing the Border: A Conversation on Immigration Policy and Governance in the United States
Institute of Politics, Harvard Kennedy School · 2022 · 1:17:06

Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham and other policymakers discuss US border governance in a Harvard Kennedy School forum, covering federal–state tensions, enforcement, and policy reform debates. Brings practitioner and political perspectives to complement the more theoretical content.

YouTube The racist history of US immigration policy | Vox
Vox · 2019 · 2:38

Vox traces the racial origins of US immigration restriction from the Chinese Exclusion Act through national origin quotas to contemporary policy, providing essential historical context for understanding the politics of borders. A concise, data-backed explainer for setting historical context in Week 7.

YouTube Webinar: Restoring Credibility and Humanity to Immigration Enforcement | American Immigration Council
American Immigration Council · 2021 · 1:01:20

Immigration policy experts examine how enforcement practices (detention, deportation, family separation) undermine both public safety and human rights, and what a reformed system could look like. Useful for exposing students to policy reform debates beyond critique — offering constructive alternative frameworks.
