1 Module overview

Introduces key concepts, terminology, and theoretical frameworks used to understand migration, displacement, refugees, and mobility. Establishes migration as a global system and engages the tension between the right to migrate and the right not to migrate, separating myths from realities.

Skill emphasis: Conceptual + Reflective

◎ Learning outcomes

Undergraduate

  1. Define key migration terms
  2. Distinguish voluntary and forced migration types
  3. Explain why migration is a global and historical phenomenon
  4. Reflect on personal assumptions and narratives about migration

Graduate

  1. Critically evaluate major migration frameworks and theories
  2. Synthesize competing definitions across disciplines
  3. Apply theory to contemporary migration cases

▤ Peer-reviewed readings

Show 6 readings6
  • DOITheories of International Migration: A Review and Appraisal
    Massey, D. S., Arango, J., Hugo, G., Kouaouci, A., Pellegrino, A., & Taylor, J. E. · 1993 · Population and Development Review, 19(3), 431–466 · 10.2307/2938462

    The foundational canonical review article that systematically evaluates neoclassical economics, new economics of labor migration, dual labor market theory, world systems theory, network theory, and institutional theory. Indispensable as a starting point for any migration theory unit because it maps the entire theoretical landscape in one place.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration and Development: A Theoretical Perspective
    de Haas, Hein · 2010 · International Migration Review, 44(1), 227–264 · 10.1111/j.1747-7379.2009.00804.x

    De Haas synthesizes optimistic and pessimistic views of migration's development impact by integrating the new economics of labor migration, livelihood approaches, and transnational perspectives. A key theoretical piece that shows why migration outcomes are heterogeneous and context-dependent, and demonstrates how paradigm shifts in social theory shape migration debates.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration in the Age of Involuntary Immobility: Theoretical Reflections and Cape Verdean Experiences
    Carling, Jørgen · 2002 · Journal of Ethnic and Migration Studies, 28(1), 5–42 · 10.1080/13691830120103912

    Carling introduces the aspiration/ability framework, distinguishing between migrants' desire to move and their capacity to do so. This article is essential for teaching the voluntary–forced migration continuum and the structural constraints that produce 'involuntary immobility', a concept now central to migration theory.

    Access article →
  • DOIWho is a Migrant? Abandoning the Nation-state Point of View in the Study of Migration
    Tazzioli, Martina & Scheel, Stephan · 2022 · Migration Politics, 1(1), 002 · 10.21468/migpol.1.1.002

    Critically examines how nation-state-centred definitions of 'migrant' embed methodological nationalism and create stigmatizing labels. Faculty teaching definitional debates (refugee vs. migrant vs. IDP) will find this article provocative and useful for introducing students to the politics of categorization.

    Access article →
  • DOIWhy Do People Migrate? Fresh Takes on the Foundational Question of Migration Studies
    Carling, Jørgen · 2024 · International Migration Review, 58(4), 1639–1662 · 10.1177/01979183241269445

    Carling revisits migration theory's central question from multiple angles including push–pull, capabilities, volition, and opportunity frameworks. This recent article provides an accessible yet theoretically rigorous entry point that debunks simplistic explanations while introducing students to multi-causal thinking.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration Policies Are Problematic – Because They Are About Migration
    Castles, Stephen · 2017 · Ethnic and Racial Studies, 40(9), 1538–1542 · 10.1080/01419870.2017.1308532

    Castles argues that migration policies fail because states try to manage what are inherently social transformation processes, not controllable flows. This piece is ideal for introducing the 'myths vs. realities' theme and for contextualizing why restrictive policies rarely achieve their stated goals.

    Access article →
