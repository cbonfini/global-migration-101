6 Module overview

Introduces refugee law, international protection frameworks, and contemporary refugee crises, comparing camp and urban settings and examining the limits of protection systems.

Skill emphasis: Conceptual + Policy

◎ Learning outcomes

Undergraduate

  1. Define refugee, asylum seeker, and internally displaced person
  2. Explain international refugee protection systems and asylum processes
  3. Identify major refugee crises
  4. Compare migrant categories

Graduate

  1. Critically evaluate refugee protection frameworks and their limits
  2. Analyze effectiveness of asylum systems across regions
  3. Analyze policy implications of protection gaps

▤ Peer-reviewed readings

Show 6 readings6
  • DOIRefugee Policy Amidst Global Shocks: Encampment, Resettlement Barriers and the Search for 'Durable Solutions'
    Spiegel, Paul B. & Mhlanga, Jabulani · 2022 · Global Policy, 13(3), 386–399 · 10.1111/1758-5899.13084

    Analyzes the tensions between encampment, resettlement barriers, and local integration in light of the 1951 Convention and Global Compact on Refugees. Uses Zimbabwe as a case study to illustrate protection gaps and the constraints on all three durable solutions, making it ideal for classroom discussion of camp vs. urban policy trade-offs.

    Access article →
  • DOIInternal Displacement (Chapter 47 in Oxford Handbook of International Refugee Law)
    Kälin, Walter · 2021 · In C. Costello, M. Foster & J. McAdam (Eds.), The Oxford Handbook of International Refugee Law, pp. 787–803. Oxford University Press · 10.1093/law/9780198848639.003.0048

    Kälin's authoritative chapter explains the fundamental legal distinction between IDPs and refugees, analyzes the Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement and the Kampala Convention, and discusses how IDP durable solutions frameworks can inform refugee law. Essential for teaching the IDP protection gap.

    Access article →
  • DOIUnderstanding the Crisis of Refugee Law: Legal Scholarship and the EU Asylum System
    Noll, Gregor; Vedsted-Hansen, Jens; & Byrne, Rosemary · 2020 · Leiden Journal of International Law, 33(4), 871–891 · 10.1017/S0922156520000382

    Examines the constitutional and structural crisis in EU refugee law, illuminating how the asylum system has eroded protection principles since the 1951 Convention. Particularly valuable for teaching the gap between the Convention's protective intent and contemporary asylum processes in practice.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Right to the City for Urban Refugees? Living in the Shadow of the Camp in Nairobi, Amman and Addis Ababa
    Earle, Lucy · 2024 · Urban Studies, 61(14), 2831–2847 · 10.1177/09562478241277077

    Compares urban refugee experiences in three major Global South cities, showing how refugees face the choice between humanitarian assistance in camps and informal self-reliance in cities. Directly addresses the 'camps vs. urban refugees' debate and illustrates the real consequences of protection gaps for urban refugees.

    Access article →
  • DOIDurable Displacement and the Protracted Search for Solutions: Promising Programs and Strategies
    Ferris, Elizabeth & Kerwin, Donald M. · 2023 · Journal on Migration and Human Security, 11(1), 1–25 · 10.1177/23315024231160454

    Synthesizes 10 cases of protracted forced displacement and identifies cross-cutting barriers to durable solutions (resettlement, local integration, voluntary return). Provides a comprehensive, empirically grounded framework for teaching why durable solutions remain elusive for the world's 100+ million displaced people.

    Access article →
  • DOIPalestinian Refugees and the Syrian Uprising: Filling the Protection Gap During Secondary Forced Displacement
    Erakat, Noura · 2014 · International Journal of Refugee Law, 26(4), 581–621 · 10.1093/IJRL/EEU047

    Analyzes the specific protection gap facing Palestinian refugees doubly displaced by the Syrian war—falling between the mandates of UNRWA and UNHCR. An excellent case study of how the 1951 Convention's exclusion clauses create protection gaps for particular refugee populations.

    Access article →

▷ Video resources

Show 6 videos6
YouTube What does it mean to be a refugee? – Benedetta Berti and Evelien Borgman | TED-Ed
TED-Ed · 2015 · 5:43

TED-Ed's animated explainer clearly defines the legal and practical distinctions between refugees, asylum seekers, and migrants. An excellent short introductory video to establish shared vocabulary at the start of Week 6.

YouTube The 1951 UN Refugee Convention: its origins and significance | 10-Minute Talks
The British Academy · 2021 · 11:07

Historian Peter Gatrell (University of Manchester) explains the origins, drafting, and enduring significance of the 1951 Refugee Convention in a concise British Academy lecture. Essential viewing for understanding the legal architecture of the international refugee regime.

TED.com Our refugee system is failing. Here's how we can fix it | Alexander Betts | TED
TED · 2016 · 17:59

Oxford refugee studies scholar Alexander Betts argues the current international refugee system is fundamentally broken and proposes a new development-based model. A landmark TED Talk for stimulating debate on the effectiveness of existing asylum frameworks.

YouTube Professor Jane McAdam – The Refugee Convention
UNSW Sydney · 2013 · 6:46

UNSW Professor Jane McAdam (leading international refugee law scholar) explains Australia's obligations under the Refugee Convention and addresses common misconceptions about asylum seekers. Useful for introducing refugee law from a legal-academic perspective.

YouTube What happens when people are forced to flee? | UNHCR
UNHCR – UN Refugee Agency · 2021 · 7:33

UNHCR's own explainer video walks through the global forced displacement process — from the decision to flee through camps, urban displacement, and durable solutions. Provides an authoritative primary-source institutional perspective on the refugee experience.

YouTube Immigration 101: Refugees, Migrants, Asylum Seekers – What's the Difference? | PBS SoCal
PBS SoCal · 2018 · 1:08

A brief PBS explainer distinguishing the key legal categories used in forced displacement contexts. Ideal as a 60-second classroom warm-up before deeper legal definitions, particularly effective with students new to the topic.
