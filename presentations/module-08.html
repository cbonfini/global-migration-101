Migration, Labor, and the Global Economy

Who powers the global economy — and at what cost?

Narration transcript

When we think about the global economy, we rarely think about the millions of migrants whose labor underpins it. From construction sites in Dubai to care homes in London to harvest fields in California, migrant workers do work that economies depend on — yet they often labor in conditions of precarity, low wages, and limited rights. This module examines the structural relationship between migration and labor markets, explores remittances as a development tool, and confronts the exploitation that too many migrant workers face.

Narration transcript

The scale of migrant labor in the global economy is staggering. According to the International Labour Organization's 2022 Global Estimates, approximately 169 million international migrant workers contribute to economies around the world — nearly one in every twenty workers globally. These workers are not evenly distributed. High-income regions, including the Gulf States, North America, and Northern and Western Europe, host the largest concentrations. Within this workforce, there is important variation by gender: while men make up roughly 57 percent of migrant workers overall, women are heavily overrepresented in domestic service, caregiving, and the informal sector — precisely the sectors where formal labor protections are weakest or entirely absent. To understand why this pattern exists, the dual labor market theory, articulated by Massey and colleagues in their 1993 review, is essential.

Key pointDe Haas (2010): 'Migration is neither the solution to underdevelopment nor its inevitable product — it is a complex outcome of development processes themselves.'

Full slide detail

  • Neoclassical economics (Todaro, 1969; Massey et al., 1993): individuals migrate when expected income differentials between origin and destination exceed the costs of moving — treats migration as a rational individual investment decision.
  • New economics of labor migration (Stark & Bloom, 1985): migration is a household risk-diversification strategy — families send one member abroad to hedge against local income shocks and accumulate capital unavailable at home.
  • Dual labor market theory (Piore, 1979): structural segmentation of labor markets in advanced economies creates a persistent, institutionalized demand for migrant workers in 'secondary' sector jobs — migration is supply responding to structural pull.
  • World systems theory (Wallerstein; Sassen): penetration of capitalist market relations into peripheral societies disrupts traditional livelihoods, generating mobile labor that flows toward global cores — migration is an artifact of global capitalism.
  • Hein de Haas's aspiration-capability framework (2010, International Migration Review): migration decisions require both the aspiration to move AND material, social, and human capital to do so — the very poorest rarely migrate internationally.

Instructor notes

This slide is important for graduate students especially. Push back on the default neoclassical framing that treats migration as purely individual rational choice. Highlight how world systems and dual labor market theories situate migration within capitalist structures. The de Haas aspiration-capability point is often counterintuitive: the poorest people in the world do NOT generally have the resources to migrate internationally — this challenges 'poverty causes immigration' narratives.

Narration transcript

Scholars have offered multiple competing frameworks for why labor migration happens, and each carries different policy implications. The neoclassical approach, developed by economists like Todaro and reviewed comprehensively by Massey and colleagues in their canonical 1993 Population and Development Review article, treats migration as a rational individual calculation: people move when the expected wage gain at the destination, adjusted for the probability of finding work, exceeds the costs of moving. This is intuitive, but it misses much. The new economics of labor migration, associated with Stark and Bloom, shifts the unit of analysis from the individual to the household: families send members abroad as a risk-diversification strategy, not merely for individual gain.

Narration transcript

Migrant labor is not randomly distributed across sectors — it is concentrated in specific industries and occupations that wealthy economies depend on structurally. Agriculture remains heavily reliant on migrant workers in virtually every major food-producing country: from the H-2A agricultural visa program in the United States, which brings in hundreds of thousands of seasonal workers each year, to seasonal labor programs in the United Kingdom, Spain, and Australia. Construction in the Gulf Cooperation Council states presents a particularly stark case: the airports, stadiums, hotels, and highways of the UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and neighboring countries were built almost entirely by migrant workers from South and Southeast Asia. Domestic and care work represents another critical sector — the ILO estimates 67 million domestic workers worldwide, the majority of them women and many of them migrants.

Instructor notes

The sheer scale of remittances often surprises students and faculty alike. Emphasize the comparison to ODA and FDI — remittances are the largest source of external financing for many developing countries. Mannan and Farhana's Bangladesh study is a good contemporary case for the digital financial inclusion dimension. Note that these figures are OFFICIAL remittances — informal flows through hawala and other channels mean the true total is likely higher.

Narration transcript

Remittances — the money that migrants send home to their families — represent one of the most significant economic dimensions of global migration. According to the World Bank's 2024 data, global remittance flows reached approximately 905 billion US dollars in 2024, with 685 billion of that going to low- and middle-income countries. To put that in perspective: this sum exceeds the combined total of foreign direct investment and official development assistance flowing to those same countries. Remittances are not just large — they are remarkably stable. Unlike FDI, which collapses during global financial crises, remittances often increase when origin countries face shocks, because migrants send more money home when their families are in greater need.

Debate

Key pointKey contested question: Do remittances fuel development — or dependency?

Full slide detail

  • Optimistic view (World Bank 1990s-2000s): remittances reduce poverty, smooth consumption, fund small-enterprise investment, and substitute for absent public services — migration as grassroots development.
  • Pessimistic / dependency critique: remittances foster consumption rather than productive investment; they can increase inequality between remittance-receiving and non-receiving households; they may reduce recipients' labor force participation (the 'moral hazard' concern).
  • Hein de Haas (2010) argues the 'migration-development nexus' debate is a 'pendulum' between optimism and pessimism — neither position captures the conditional, context-specific nature of migration's developmental effects.
  • Wagle (2024, Comparative Migration Studies) finds that in the Gulf region, mass labor emigration has in some cases slowed labor productivity growth in origin economies despite large remittance volumes — complicating simple positive narratives.
  • Brain drain vs. brain gain debate: emigration of skilled workers may deprive origin countries of human capital, but return migrants bring skills, capital, and networks; 'brain circulation' may be more accurate than 'drain' for many contexts.

Instructor notes

This is a genuine scholarly debate without a settled answer. Present both positions fairly. De Haas's 2010 framework is the best contemporary synthesis — his aspiration-capability model shows that migration's developmental effects are conditioned by structural factors in both origin and destination societies. For grad students, push them to think about: under what conditions does migration produce development vs. dependency?

Narration transcript

The relationship between remittances and development has been one of the most contested questions in migration scholarship. Through the 1990s and 2000s, the World Bank and development agencies were broadly optimistic: remittances, they argued, reduced poverty, smoothed consumption, funded housing and education, and allowed migrant households to invest in small businesses. This view positioned migration as a form of grassroots development that official aid programs could not replicate. But critics pushed back. If remittances primarily fund consumption rather than investment, they argued, they may foster dependency rather than structural development. Inequality may increase between households that receive remittances and those that don't. And the emigration of skilled workers creates a 'brain drain' that deprives origin countries of the human capital they need most.

Migrant Labor Exploitation: Mechanisms and Vulnerability

  1. Recruitment debt bondage migrants pay high fees to recruitment agencies in origin countries, creating debt that employers and traffickers …
  2. Document confiscation employers seize passports and identity documents to prevent workers from leaving.
  3. Contract substitution workers are promised one contract, then presented with a different, inferior one on arrival.
  4. Legal precarity irregular immigration status gives employers significant power over workers, who fear deportation if they report abuse.
  5. Siyuan Yin situates platform gig economy work within a 'continuum of exploitation' for transnational migrant domestic workers.2023, Capital and Class
Key pointStructural vulnerability: exploitation is not merely a product of 'bad employers' but is embedded in immigration systems that deliberately tie workers to single employers and deny them labor rights.

Full slide detail

  • Recruitment debt bondage: migrants pay high fees to recruitment agencies in origin countries, creating debt that employers and traffickers exploit to extract labor through threats and wage theft.
  • Document confiscation: employers seize passports and identity documents to prevent workers from leaving — illegal under international law but common in domestic and construction sectors.
  • Contract substitution: workers are promised one contract, then presented with a different, inferior one on arrival — a documented practice in the GCC and other regions.
  • Legal precarity: irregular immigration status gives employers significant power over workers, who fear deportation if they report abuse — creating structural conditions for exploitation regardless of employer intent.
  • Siyuan Yin (2023, Capital and Class) situates platform gig economy work within a 'continuum of exploitation' for transnational migrant domestic workers — digital platforms extend and intensify precarity rather than disrupting it.

Instructor notes

The critical point here is structural: vulnerability to exploitation is built into immigration systems. Students often frame exploitation as individual moral failure (bad employers). Help them see how immigration status, debt bondage mechanisms, and policy architecture systematically disempower workers. Yin's 2023 article on platform gig economy is particularly timely for discussions of digital economy and migrant domestic work.

Narration transcript

When we talk about migrant labor exploitation, we need to resist the temptation to see it primarily as a problem of bad individuals — unscrupulous employers, criminal recruiters. Exploitation is often structured into immigration and employment systems in ways that systematically disempower workers. The recruitment debt bondage cycle is one of the most pervasive mechanisms: migrants in Bangladesh, Nepal, the Philippines, or Ethiopia pay recruitment agencies fees that may equal several months of expected wages, creating debt that they must work off before they can accumulate any savings. On arrival, employers and recruiters may leverage that debt to extract compliance. Document confiscation — seizure of passports and identity cards — is illegal under international law but remains common in domestic and construction sectors across the Gulf, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe.

Full slide detail

  • The kafala (sponsorship) system governs labor migration in GCC countries: Qatar, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman. An estimated 25-30 million migrant workers live under kafala arrangements.
  • Under kafala, a migrant worker's legal status is tied to a single sponsor-employer, who controls the worker's ability to change jobs, obtain an exit visa, and in some contexts leave the country — creating a power imbalance ripe for abuse.
  • Qatar's 2022 FIFA World Cup brought international scrutiny: an estimated 6,500 migrant construction workers from South Asia died in Qatar between 2010-2020 (The Guardian, 2021), primarily from cardiovascular causes linked to extreme heat and labor conditions.
  • Several GCC states have announced kafala reforms since 2020-2021, including abolishing exit visa requirements in Qatar and allowing job mobility; however, implementation and enforcement gaps remain significant.
  • Rodríguez-Modroño et al. (2022, Work, Employment and Society) show how care platforms operating in Europe similarly exploit intersecting inequalities of gender, race, and immigration status among migrant women workers.

Instructor notes

Qatar and the FIFA World Cup provide a powerful contemporary case that most students will have heard about. Use it to ground the abstract concept of structural exploitation in a specific, well-documented context. Be careful to present the kafala system accurately: it is a regional legal architecture, not simply employer misconduct. Note that reforms have been announced but enforcement is uneven. The comparison to platform care work in Europe (Rodríguez-Modroño et al.) is useful for showing how similar dynamics emerge in different regulatory contexts.

Narration transcript

The kafala, or sponsorship, system operating across the Gulf Cooperation Council states provides one of the most extensively documented case studies in structural migrant labor vulnerability. Under kafala, a migrant worker's legal status is tied directly to a single employer-sponsor. The worker cannot change jobs, renew a residence visa, or in many cases leave the country without the sponsor's permission. This creates a power imbalance that is not incidental — it is built into the legal architecture. Globally, an estimated 25 to 30 million workers live under kafala arrangements. When Qatar was awarded the 2022 FIFA World Cup, international scrutiny of these conditions intensified dramatically.

Full slide detail

  • The informal economy — economic activity that is not registered, regulated, or taxed — employs a disproportionate share of migrant workers globally, particularly undocumented migrants.
  • Sectors: street vending, domestic work, construction day labor, agriculture, small-scale manufacturing. Workers in the informal economy lack access to labor protections, social security, healthcare, and recourse for wage theft.
  • Informality is not simply a feature of the Global South: in the United States, an estimated 8 million undocumented workers participate in the formal and informal economy; in Southern European countries, agricultural and domestic sectors rely heavily on unregistered migrant labor.
  • The 'triple vulnerability' of undocumented, informal, and female migrant workers: immigration precarity, absence of legal employment protections, and gendered power dynamics compound risk of exploitation and abuse.
  • Migrant entrepreneurship: despite barriers, migrants are more likely than native populations to start businesses in host countries — a form of economic agency and livelihood strategy that official systems often impede through regulatory barriers.

Instructor notes

Challenge the common assumption that informal migrant labor is 'underground' and separate from the formal economy. In fact, formal supply chains in agriculture, construction, and food processing routinely rely on undocumented subcontracted labor. The entrepreneurship point is important for balance: despite enormous barriers, migrants show high rates of business formation — a form of agency and resilience that the 'victim' framing can obscure.

Narration transcript

Not all migrant workers labor within formal, regulated employment relationships. A substantial portion of the global migrant labor force works in informal economies — economic activity that is unregistered, unregulated, and untaxed. This is not simply a feature of lower-income countries. In the United States, an estimated 8 million undocumented workers contribute to agriculture, construction, food service, domestic work, and manufacturing. In Spain, Italy, and Greece, informal agricultural and domestic work is structurally reliant on migrant labor, often from Africa, Eastern Europe, and Latin America. Workers in the informal economy lack access to minimum wage guarantees, occupational safety protections, workers' compensation, and legal recourse when wages are stolen. For undocumented workers, the fear of deportation creates a powerful silencing effect that prevents reporting of abuse.

Instructor notes

This slide connects well to development finance and financial inclusion policy. Narration transcript

The economics of sending money home are not as simple as they appear. According to the World Bank's Remittance Prices Worldwide database, the average global cost of sending 200 US dollars in remittances stood at approximately 6.4 percent in early 2024 — more than double the 3 percent target set in the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals. At 6 percent, that cost represents nearly two weeks' wages for a low-income migrant worker. The choice of transfer channel matters enormously: banks charge an average of around 11 percent, while mobile money operators average just 3.5 percent, making fintech adoption critically important for reducing costs for low-income workers.

Narration transcript

The feminization of migration is one of the most significant structural shifts of the past four decades. Women now make up nearly half of all international migrants globally, and in many major migration corridors they are the majority. But the sectors where women migrants are concentrated — domestic work, care, nursing, hospitality — are precisely those where labor protections are weakest and exploitation risk is highest. Rodríguez-Modroño and colleagues, writing in Work, Employment and Society in 2022, applied feminist political economy to care platforms in Europe and found that digital platforms have created new mechanisms of invisibility for women migrant care workers: the app-based consumer interface obscures labor relations, externalizing labor risk onto individual workers while platform companies profit.

Full slide detail

  • The traditional 'migration hump': development economists once predicted that as countries industrialize, emigration first increases (as more people acquire the aspiration and capability to migrate) before eventually declining — arguing aid and investment can reduce migration long-term.
  • De Haas's critique (2010): the aspiration-capability framework predicts that development increases migration in the medium term — making the 'address root causes to stop migration' argument empirically problematic.
  • Circular migration and brain circulation: labor migration need not be permanent; circular programs allow migrants to contribute to both origin and destination countries — but poorly designed systems create more exploitation than development.
  • The 3 Ds — migration and development in origin societies can be understood through three mechanisms: direct (remittances, investment), indirect (social remittances — ideas, skills, values migrants bring back), and demonstration effects (migration aspirations in communities).
  • Structural critique: without reforming the global trading system, intellectual property regimes, and debt structures that disadvantage low-income countries, migration cannot substitute for the systemic development changes needed.

Instructor notes

This is where the module connects to broader development theory. The 'addressing root causes to stop migration' framing is extremely common in policy discourse — and de Haas's work shows why it is empirically oversimplified. Peggy Levitt's concept of social remittances is important for expanding the analysis beyond money. The structural critique at the end is important for graduate students: development-migration linkages cannot be fixed by migration policy alone.

Narration transcript

One of the most persistent framings in migration policy discourse is that if wealthy countries invest in development in poor countries, migration will decrease. This 'addressing root causes' narrative has structured billions of dollars of development aid. But the empirical record is more complicated. Development economists once projected a 'migration hump': as countries industrialize and people acquire resources, emigration first increases before eventually declining. Hein de Haas's aspiration-capability framework explains why: development gives more people the resources and aspirations to migrate, meaning development investments in the medium term may actually increase emigration, not reduce it. This has important policy implications: arguing that aid substitutes for migration governance is empirically questionable.

Full slide detail

  • Core international instruments: ILO Conventions 97 and 143 on migrant workers; ILO Convention 189 on domestic workers; the International Convention on the Protection of Rights of All Migrant Workers (ICMW, 1990) — ratified only by major emigration states, not major destination states.
  • The ILO 'decent work' agenda for migrants: five principles — employment, social protection, fundamental rights at work, social dialogue, and gender equality — but application is uneven and often depends on bilateral labor agreements.
  • Bilateral labor agreements (BLAs): country-to-country agreements governing terms of labor migration; examples include Philippines-Qatar, Philippines-Saudi Arabia, Moldova-Germany; vary enormously in quality and enforcement.
  • Trade union access: migrant workers, especially undocumented workers, face legal and practical barriers to trade union membership in most destination countries — undermining collective voice and bargaining power.
  • Foley and Piper (2021, Global Public Policy and Governance) examine the ILO-IOM relationship, revealing tensions between the ILO's rights-based 'decent work' mandate and IOM's more state-centric 'migration management' approach.

Instructor notes

The gap between the existence of international standards and their ratification/enforcement is a critical pattern for students to internalize. The ICMW ratification map is a powerful visual: ask students why destination countries have not ratified. The ILO-IOM tension from Foley and Piper is relevant for Module 14 on governance, but introduce it here to show how institutional architecture shapes worker protection outcomes.

Narration transcript

The international labor rights framework for migrant workers is extensive on paper but limited in practice. The ILO Conventions 97 and 143 on migrant workers established baseline protections in the 1940s and 1970s. The International Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families, adopted by the UN General Assembly in 1990, is the most comprehensive instrument — but as of today, only approximately 58 states have ratified it, and almost none of them are major destination countries. The same countries that most depend on migrant labor have largely declined to bind themselves to comprehensive protection standards for those workers. The ILO's 'decent work' agenda provides another framework: five pillars of employment opportunity, social protection, fundamental rights at work, social dialogue, and gender equality.

Key pointKey insight: migrant economic inclusion is not just about job placement. It requires legal status, credential recognition, social network access, financial inclusion, and freedom from exploitation — simultaneously.

Full slide detail

  • Livelihood frameworks (Chambers and Conway, 1992; DFID Sustainable Livelihoods Framework): analyze assets (human, social, natural, physical, financial capital), vulnerability context, transforming structures and processes, and livelihood outcomes.
  • For migrant populations, sustainable livelihoods must account for: legal status and its implications for labor market access; social capital and diaspora networks; recognition of foreign credentials and prior experience; language and cultural barriers.
  • Key interventions: labor market integration programs (language training, credential recognition, vocational training); social protection portability; micro-finance and migrant entrepreneurship support; financial literacy and remittance cost reduction.
  • Refugee-specific livelihood programs (UNHCR, ILO): challenges include legal right to work (absent in many countries), geographic constraints (camps), credential non-recognition, employer discrimination — success requires multi-level reform.
  • The 'livelihood design challenge' for faculty: ask students to design a realistic, constraint-aware livelihood support program for a specific migrant population — integrating economic, legal, social, and structural dimensions.

Instructor notes

This slide prepares students for the module's signature experience — a livelihood design challenge for a specific migrant population. The DFID Sustainable Livelihoods Framework is a useful teaching scaffold. Ground the design challenge in a real population: Syrian refugees in Germany, Venezuelan migrants in Colombia, or domestic workers in Hong Kong. Emphasize that livelihood design must be constraint-aware — working with actual legal, financial, and social conditions rather than imagining an ideal world.

Narration transcript

How should societies support the economic inclusion of migrant and refugee populations? The sustainable livelihoods framework, originally developed by Chambers and Conway and later formalized by the UK's Department for International Development, provides a useful analytical scaffold. It asks us to inventory the human capital, social capital, financial assets, physical resources, and natural resources available to a household or community, and then trace how policy structures and social institutions either support or undermine people's ability to turn those assets into sustainable outcomes.

Full slide detail

  • Discussion prompt 1: A wealthy country has a structural shortage of care workers. It recruits nurses from a country that also faces a nursing shortage. What ethical obligations does the recruiting country have, and to whom?
  • Discussion prompt 2: Is the kafala system fundamentally incompatible with international human rights standards, or can it be reformed? What would genuine reform look like — and what political obstacles prevent it?
  • Discussion prompt 3: Should remittances be considered a substitute for development aid, or does framing migration as a development tool shift moral responsibility for global inequality from states to individual migrants?
  • Discussion prompt 4: De Haas argues that development increases migration in the medium term. What does this imply for the 'addressing root causes to stop migration' policy argument? Is that argument ever honest?

Instructor notes

These prompts are designed to be productively uncomfortable. The ethical recruitment debate (prompt 1) has no clean answer and surfaces real tensions between state interests and individual rights and global equity. The remittances-as-development-substitute critique (prompt 3) often provokes strong reactions from students with diaspora backgrounds — create space for that personal connection while maintaining analytical rigor. For prompt 4, have students think about what political actors gain by framing migration policy as development policy.

Narration transcript

This module raises questions that don't have clean answers — and that is precisely why they are worth debating carefully in class. Consider the ethical recruitment question: a wealthy country like the United Kingdom or the United States faces a critical shortage of nurses. It runs recruitment campaigns in the Philippines or Ghana, countries that also face nursing shortages. Who owes what to whom in that situation? Does the recruiting country have obligations to the source country's healthcare system? To the individual nurses who benefit from migration? The kafala discussion asks whether institutional architecture can truly be reformed, or whether the system's exploitation is baked into its design.

Key takeaways

Module 8 Takeaways

  1. Migrant labor is not peripheral to the global economy. ILO, 2022
  2. Remittances of approximately USD 685 billion annually to low- and middle-income countries exceed FDI and ODA combined. World Bank, 2024
  3. Exploitation is built into immigration systems, not just individual conduct: debt bondage, document confiscation, kafala, and immigration precarity are structural …
  4. Gender matters: the feminization of migration and the concentration of women in domestic and care sectors.
  5. Effective economic inclusion requires simultaneous action on legal status, credential recognition, labor rights, financial access, and social protection.
Key pointCore tension to carry forward: Migration sustains the global economy — but the global economy often fails to sustain migrants.

Full slide detail

  • Migrant labor is not peripheral to the global economy — it is structural: approximately 167.7 million migrant workers power the sectors that wealthy economies depend on and cannot fill from domestic labor alone (ILO, 2022).
  • Remittances of approximately USD 685 billion annually to low- and middle-income countries (World Bank, 2024) exceed FDI and ODA combined — but their developmental impact is conditional and context-dependent, not automatically transformative (de Haas, 2010).
  • Exploitation is built into immigration systems, not just individual conduct: debt bondage, document confiscation, kafala, and immigration precarity are structural features that require policy reform, not just better employers.
  • Gender matters: the feminization of migration and the concentration of women in domestic and care sectors — combined with weak protection frameworks — creates compounded vulnerability requiring targeted policy responses.
  • Effective economic inclusion requires simultaneous action on legal status, credential recognition, labor rights, financial access, and social protection — no single intervention is sufficient without the others.

Instructor notes

Close the module by returning to the opening tension: migrant labor is indispensable to wealthy economies, yet those economies routinely fail to extend full rights and protections to the workers they depend on. Invite students to name one concrete policy change — at any governance level — that would most improve the situation of migrant workers. This bridges to the governance module later in the course.

Narration transcript

This module has covered a lot of ground. Let us pull together the key threads. First, the scale: approximately 169 million migrant workers are embedded in the global economy, concentrated in sectors that wealthy countries structurally depend on — agriculture, construction, domestic care, healthcare, hospitality. Second, remittances: nearly 685 billion US dollars flow annually to low- and middle-income countries, a sum that exceeds foreign direct investment and official development assistance combined. But their developmental effects are conditional — de Haas's work cautions us against both naive optimism and pessimistic dismissal. Third, exploitation: vulnerability to labor exploitation is not merely a product of bad actors but is embedded in immigration systems that tie workers to single employers, deny them labor rights, and use documentation requirements as tools of control.
