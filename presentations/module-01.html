Foundations of Global Migration

How do we define, theorize, and contextualize human mobility on a global scale?

Module 1 of 14ConceptualReflective

Narration transcript

Welcome to Global Migration 101. This opening module asks a deceptively simple question: what is migration, and who counts as a migrant? Before we can analyze data, evaluate policy, or explore lived experience, we need a shared vocabulary and a set of theoretical tools. We will find that even the most basic categories are contested — and that acknowledging that contestation is itself a form of intellectual honesty essential to rigorous teaching.

Concept

Migration as a Human Constant

  1. Human beings have been mobile for the entirety of recorded. and pre-recorded — history.
  2. According to UN DESA estimates published in 2025, approximately 304 million people lived outside their country of birth in 2024, representing about 3.7 percent of …
  3. The majority of human mobility is internal (within a country's borders) or regional.
  4. Migration is not exceptional deviance from a sedentary norm.
Key point304 million international migrants in 2024 — yet only 3.7 percent of world population (UN DESA, 2025). Most people do not migrate internationally.

Full slide detail

  • Human beings have been mobile for the entirety of recorded — and pre-recorded — history; the modern framing of migration as a 'problem' to be managed is a historically recent construction tied to the rise of the nation-state.
  • According to UN DESA estimates published in 2025, approximately 304 million people lived outside their country of birth in 2024, representing about 3.7 percent of the global population — a proportion that has grown only modestly since 1990 despite large absolute increases.
  • The majority of human mobility is internal (within a country's borders) or regional; the stereotype of South-to-North migration distorts the more complex reality of South-South flows and circular movement.
  • Migration is not exceptional deviance from a sedentary norm; as sociologist Stephen Castles argues, states that treat mobility as controllable 'flow' misunderstand it as an inherent social transformation process.

Instructor notes

Open with this slide to immediately challenge the assumption that migration is inherently exceptional or crisis-driven. Ask students: 'What image came to mind when you heard the word migrant before this course?' Surface assumptions before introducing definitions. Castles (2017) is the reading anchor here.

Narration transcript

When we talk about migration, we are talking about one of the most fundamental human behaviors — the movement from one place to another in search of safety, opportunity, connection, or simply a different life. And yet, in contemporary political discourse, migration is frequently framed as a crisis, a wave, an invasion, or an emergency. This framing is historically unusual. It emerged alongside the consolidation of nation-states in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — the moment when territory became legally bounded and crossing those boundaries became an act requiring permission. According to UN DESA, roughly 304 million people lived outside their country of birth in 2024. That sounds like a large number. But it represents only 3.7 percent of the global population. The overwhelming majority of humanity — more than 96 percent — did not migrate internationally. This is the first important reality check we need to hold onto throughout this course.

DefinitionThe 1951 Convention refugee definition is 75 years old. Does it still capture who needs protection today?

Full slide detail

  • International migrant (UN DESA): a person residing in a country other than their country of birth for at least one year, regardless of legal status or reason — an administrative and statistical definition that deliberately brackets cause.
  • Refugee (1951 Refugee Convention, Article 1A): a person outside their country of nationality who cannot return owing to a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group.
  • Asylum seeker: a person who has applied for refugee status and is awaiting a determination; they have refugee rights under international law while their claim is pending but do not yet hold formal refugee status.
  • Internally displaced person (IDP): someone forced to flee their home who remains within their own country's borders; IDPs are not covered by the 1951 Convention and are the single largest category of forcibly displaced people, numbering 68.7 million at end-2025 according to UNHCR — 58 percent of all forcibly displaced people.
  • Stateless person: someone lacking nationality under the law of any state; UNHCR counted 4.5 million stateless people at end-2025, though the true figure is likely higher.

Instructor notes

Students often conflate refugee and migrant in casual speech. This slide is an opportunity to explain that in international law these are distinct legal categories with very different rights and obligations attached. Graduate students should be pushed to consider how Tazzioli and Scheel (2022) argue that the very act of categorization is political and embeds methodological nationalism.

Narration transcript

Let us establish the core vocabulary. The broadest term is 'international migrant,' a statistical category used by UN DESA to mean simply: someone living outside their country of birth. It does not tell us why they moved, how they got there, or what rights they hold. It is a counting category. Within that broad universe, we distinguish those who are forcibly displaced. The 1951 Refugee Convention defines a refugee as someone outside their country of nationality who cannot return due to a well-founded fear of persecution on specific grounds — race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. An asylum seeker is someone who has made that claim and is awaiting determination. But the largest category of forced displacement is actually IDPs — internally displaced persons — people who fled but never crossed an international border. At the end of 2024, UNHCR counted 73.5 million IDPs. They are not covered by the 1951 Convention. That gap is one of the most consequential structural failings in international protection, and we will return to it repeatedly in this course.

Concept

The Voluntary–Forced Continuum

  1. The binary distinction between 'voluntary' economic migrants and 'forced' refugees is analytically and empirically unstable.
  2. Jørgen Carling's aspiration/ability framework distinguishes between the desire to migrate and the capacity to do so.Carling's, 2002
  3. A person may be 'pushed' by poverty, gang violence, or crop failure and simultaneously 'pulled' by family networks, labor demand, or regulatory pathways.
  4. The legal threshold of refugee determination forces this continuum into a binary (refugee / not-refugee) that shapes who receives protection.
Key pointCarling (2002): 'Involuntary immobility' — the condition of wanting to migrate but being unable to — is as analytically important as migration itself.

Full slide detail

  • The binary distinction between 'voluntary' economic migrants and 'forced' refugees is analytically and empirically unstable; most migration decisions involve a mixture of coercion, constraint, aspiration, and structural pressure.
  • Jørgen Carling's (2002) aspiration/ability framework distinguishes between the desire to migrate and the capacity to do so; those who wish to leave but cannot — what Carling calls 'involuntary immobility' — are rendered invisible by frameworks that focus only on movers.
  • A person may be 'pushed' by poverty, gang violence, or crop failure and simultaneously 'pulled' by family networks, labor demand, or regulatory pathways — no single factor fully determines movement.
  • The legal threshold of refugee determination forces this continuum into a binary (refugee / not-refugee) that shapes who receives protection; scholars note this creates systematic protection gaps for mixed-motive migrants.

Instructor notes

This slide tends to generate strong student reactions because it complicates intuitive categories. A productive discussion prompt: 'If a person leaves because their region has experienced both drought and gang violence, are they an economic migrant or a forced migrant?' There is no clean answer — and that is the pedagogical point. Draw attention to how the legal binary has real protective consequences.

Narration transcript

One of the most important conceptual moves in migration studies is to question the voluntary-forced binary. We often speak as if migrants choose to move while refugees are compelled to. In practice, the line is far murkier. Someone fleeing extreme poverty in a context of structural violence may not meet the legal definition of a refugee, but describing their departure as 'voluntary' misrepresents the degree of constraint they face. Jørgen Carling introduced the aspiration-ability framework in a landmark 2002 article, distinguishing between wanting to move and being able to move. This gives us four categories: those who aspire to migrate and can; those who aspire to migrate but cannot — what he calls 'involuntary immobility'; those who can migrate but do not wish to; and those caught somewhere in between. Recognizing involuntary immobility as a real condition — with documented mental health consequences — is important for faculty who want to teach migration humanely and rigorously.

Key pointEach classical theory illuminates a different scale: individual wage calculus, household strategy, labor market structure, or global economic hierarchy.

Full slide detail

  • Neoclassical economics (Ravenstein, 1885; Todaro & Harris, 1970): migration as rational individual response to wage differentials between regions; predicts migrants move from low-wage to high-wage areas and stop when differentials equalize.
  • New Economics of Labor Migration (Stark & Bloom, 1985): migrates as a household-level strategy to diversify income and manage risk, not just an individual choice — shifts unit of analysis from individual to household.
  • Dual labor market theory (Piore, 1979): international migration is driven not by push factors in sending countries but by structural demand in advanced economies for low-wage labor in a secondary labor market that native workers reject.
  • World systems theory (Wallerstein; Sassen, 1988): migration flows follow the penetration of global capitalism into peripheral regions; colonial and post-colonial economic ties channel migration from South to North.
  • Massey et al. (1993) in their canonical Population and Development Review article synthesize these frameworks, noting each captures a different scale and mechanism while none alone is sufficient.

Instructor notes

For undergraduate students, focus on neoclassical and new economics — they are intuitive entry points. For graduate students, push toward Massey et al.'s synthetic critique: each theory tells part of the story, but none is complete. A useful board exercise is to take a single real migration flow (e.g., Filipino nurses to the UK) and ask: which theories best explain it? How do they complement or contradict each other?

Narration transcript

Migration scholars have developed a rich set of theoretical frameworks, each starting from different assumptions about human behavior and social structure. The oldest and most intuitive is neoclassical economics: people move when expected wages elsewhere exceed wages at home, accounting for the costs of moving. A more sophisticated variant — the New Economics of Labor Migration — shifts the unit of analysis to the household, recognizing that families send migrants abroad partly as an insurance strategy. Michael Piore argued from a different angle entirely: it is not just that migrants want to leave their countries, but that advanced economies structurally demand them. A second, informal labor market for low-wage work that native workers reject creates permanent pull. And world systems theorists like Saskia Sassen show how the global reach of capital generates migration flows along the same channels — colonial relationships, trade networks, military presence — through which capital itself has penetrated. As Massey and colleagues showed in their 1993 synthesis, none of these frameworks is sufficient on its own. They illuminate different scales and mechanisms. The best teaching approach holds them in productive tension.

Full slide detail

  • Network theory (Massey et al., 1993): once established, migrant networks lower the costs and risks of subsequent moves, creating self-reinforcing flows that persist long after the original structural conditions have changed — explaining migration's path-dependence.
  • Cumulative causation: migration changes conditions in both sending and receiving communities (remittances, cultural capital, land use) in ways that generate further migration — the process becomes self-perpetuating.
  • Transnationalism: migrants maintain simultaneous ties to origin and destination communities through economic remittances, political participation, and cultural practices; 'assimilation' into one society is not the only or predominant outcome.
  • De Haas (2010): integrating 'new economics of labor migration,' livelihood approaches, and transnational perspectives to argue that migration's development impacts are context-dependent and heterogeneous — neither uniformly positive nor negative.
  • Carling (2024): revisits the foundational question 'why do people migrate?' through push-pull, capabilities, volition, and opportunity lenses, calling for multi-causal explanations that resist reductionism.

Instructor notes

Network theory is highly teachable because students can see it in their own communities — migration chains connecting specific sending towns to specific receiving cities. A useful exercise is to ask students to trace a specific migration corridor and identify the network mechanisms operating within it. Emphasize de Haas's key contribution: migration outcomes are not determined by migration per se but by the wider political economy in which migration occurs.

Narration transcript

Once we move beyond individual-level and economic explanations, we encounter frameworks that foreground social structure, historical accumulation, and transnational simultaneity. Network theory, as elaborated by Massey and colleagues, shows that migration is deeply path-dependent. Once a flow is established, networks of family and community ties reduce the information costs and risks for subsequent migrants, sustaining the flow even when original economic differentials narrow. This is why migration corridors — say, from Michoacán to Chicago, or from Sylhet to Bradford — persist over decades. Cumulative causation takes this further: migration changes conditions in sending communities — land prices rise, labor becomes scarce, social norms shift — in ways that generate further migration. And transnationalism complicates the assumption that migrants simply depart and assimilate. Many live in simultaneously meaningful ways in multiple places at once, maintaining economic, political, and cultural ties across borders. Hein de Haas synthesizes these insights to argue that migration's relationship to development is deeply contingent — context shapes outcomes far more than migration itself.

Key pointLabeling someone an 'economic migrant' versus a 'refugee' is not merely semantic — it determines whether they receive legal protection or deportation.

Full slide detail

  • Tazzioli and Scheel (2022) argue that migrant categories are not neutral descriptions but political acts: the nation-state-centered point of view embedded in the term 'migrant' produces hierarchies of legitimacy and stigma.
  • The economic migrant / refugee binary does political work: labeling a person an 'economic migrant' can strip them of protection claims even when economic deprivation is intertwined with political violence and climate change.
  • Contested categories in practice: 'climate migrant' lacks formal legal definition; 'survival migrant' (Betts, 2013) names those who fall outside the 1951 Convention but face severe deprivation; 'mixed movements' describes flows containing multiple categories simultaneously.
  • The act of counting: who gets included in official migration statistics shapes who receives resources, protection, and policy attention — undocumented migrants, nomadic populations, and short-term movers are systematically underrepresented.
  • Castles (2017) argues that migration policies fail systematically because states attempt to manage social transformation processes using a migration-as-flow metaphor that misrepresents the phenomenon's nature.

Instructor notes

This slide connects directly to the signature experience (reflective essay on migration narratives). Ask students: 'Can you think of a case where the label applied to a group of migrants changed their fate?' The Haitian temporary protected status debates, or the EU's treatment of Ukrainian versus Afghan asylum seekers in 2022, offer rich contemporary examples. Graduate students should engage with Tazzioli and Scheel on methodological nationalism.

Narration transcript

Categories are never innocent. When a government official, a journalist, or an international organization labels someone an 'economic migrant' rather than a 'refugee,' that label carries enormous practical consequences. Refugees have rights under the 1951 Convention — to non-refoulement, to legal documentation, to a determination hearing. 'Economic migrants' can be detained and deported. Martina Tazzioli and Stephan Scheel, in a 2022 article, argue that the very concept of 'the migrant' is constructed from a nation-state-centered perspective that treats crossing borders as a deviation from normal stasis rather than a human practice with its own logic. They encourage us to examine who benefits from particular categorizations and whose interests are served by maintaining strict definitional boundaries. Stephen Castles puts it slightly differently: states treat migration as a controllable flow when it is actually an expression of deep social transformation — inequality, conflict, ecological change, family networks — and the mismatch between the model and reality is why restrictive policies so consistently fail to achieve their stated goals.

The Right to Migrate and the Right Not to Migrate

  1. Article 13 of the Universal Declaration of Human RightsRights, 1948 'Everyone has the right to leave any country, including his own, and to return to his country'
  2. The asymmetry international law robustly protects the right of exit.
  3. The right NOT to migrate the 2018 Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration acknowledges that development cooperation should reduce …
  4. Hollifield's 'liberal paradox'Hollifield's, 2004 liberal democracies face a structural tension between openness to capital and goods (required by globalization) and …
  5. Indigenous perspectives challenge the migration/stasis binary entirely for some communities, any movement away from ancestral land is itself a form of dispossession, not a free choice.
Key pointThe right to leave is enshrined in international law. The right to enter is not — and that gap is where the most violent enforcement occurs.

Full slide detail

  • Article 13 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948): 'Everyone has the right to leave any country, including his own, and to return to his country' — a right to exit but conspicuously silent on the right to enter elsewhere.
  • The asymmetry: international law robustly protects the right of exit; it is largely silent on an affirmative right of entry into another state, leaving admission discretion primarily with receiving states.
  • The right NOT to migrate: the 2018 Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration acknowledges that development cooperation should reduce 'adverse drivers' so that people can stay if they wish — recognizing that forced departure is itself a rights violation.
  • Hollifield's (2004) 'liberal paradox': liberal democracies face a structural tension between openness to capital and goods (required by globalization) and restriction of labor mobility; this tension generates contradictory policies.
  • Indigenous perspectives challenge the migration/stasis binary entirely: for some communities, any movement away from ancestral land is itself a form of dispossession, not a free choice.

Instructor notes

The phrase 'right not to migrate' may be unfamiliar to students but resonates immediately when explained: no one should be forced to leave their home through poverty, violence, or climate catastrophe. This reframes migration policy from a question of how to manage arrivals to how to address root causes. This also provides an opening for the Cabrini Institute's human dignity framework without making the course explicitly religious.

Narration transcript

One of the most generative frameworks for this course is the tension between two rights: the right to migrate and the right not to migrate. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights protects the right to leave any country. But international law says comparatively little about the right to enter another one. Receiving states retain broad — though not unlimited — discretion over who enters. This asymmetry is not accidental; it reflects the political architecture of a world organized around sovereign nation-states. But the reverse framing is equally important: the right not to migrate. No one should be compelled to flee their home because of structural violence, environmental collapse, or extreme deprivation. This framing appears in the 2018 Global Compact on Migration, which explicitly names reducing adverse drivers so that migration becomes truly voluntary as one of its core objectives. James Hollifield captures the structural tension facing liberal democratic states: they want free movement of capital and goods because globalization demands it, but they simultaneously want to restrict labor mobility — and this contradiction generates incoherent, often cruel, policies.

Full slide detail

  • Myth: migrants are predominantly poor and uneducated. Reality: migration requires resources; the very poorest are least likely to be able to migrate internationally; the 'migration hump' theory (Martin & Taylor, 1996) shows migration initially increases as incomes rise before declining.
  • Myth: most migration is from South to North. Reality: approximately 40 percent of international migration is South-to-South; roughly one-third of all international migrants move among developing countries (IOM World Migration Report 2024).
  • Myth: migration is rising steeply as a share of world population. Reality: international migrants as a share of world population were 2.9 percent in 1990 and 3.7 percent in 2024 — a modest increase over 34 years despite sharp growth in absolute numbers (UN DESA 2025).
  • Myth: immigrants take jobs from native workers. Reality: economic research consistently finds immigrants fill labor market niches, complement native workers, and increase overall economic output; short-term displacement in narrow occupation bands is offset by economic expansion.
  • Myth: sending more people back solves irregular migration. Reality: Castles (2017) and empirical evidence show deportation rates correlate poorly with deterrence; structural drivers persist regardless of enforcement intensity.

Instructor notes

This slide is essential for the 'myths vs. realities' theme in the module outcomes. Prepare for students who have strong prior beliefs. The goal is not to convince students of a particular politics but to demand evidence. For each myth, ask: 'Where does this belief come from? What does the evidence actually show?' Cite sources explicitly — modeling the standard you expect from students.

Narration transcript

Before we proceed into the analytical core of migration studies, it is worth clearing away some of the most persistent misconceptions that shape public debate and that students will have absorbed from media and everyday conversation. The idea that migrants are predominantly poor, uneducated, and desperate reverses the empirical pattern: international migration requires resources. Research on the 'migration hump' shows that migration rates actually increase as countries develop economically before eventually declining. The framing that most migration flows from South to North also distorts reality. The IOM World Migration Report finds roughly 40 percent of migration is South-to-South. And the claim that migration is exploding as a share of world population is contradicted by data: in 1990 international migrants were 2.9 percent of the global population; by 2024, 3.7 percent. That is meaningful growth but not the dramatic surge the political discourse implies. Finally, the intuition that immigrants displace native workers is consistently contradicted by labor economics research, which finds complementarity, not substitution, at the aggregate level.

Migration as a Global System

  1. Migration systems theoryKritz, Lim & Zlotnik, 1992 migration flows and counterflows link origin and destination societies through economic, political, cultural, and …
  2. Key features of a migration system persistent bilateral flows, feedback loops (remittances, return, information), shared colonial or economic history, and …
  3. The global migration system is hierarchical wealthy destination countries exercise disproportionate power over the rules, including which visa categories exist …
  4. Globalization and migration have a paradoxical relationship capital, goods, and information move with increasing freedom.
  5. Migration systems are not static the Ukraine displacement crisis, Venezuelan emigration, and Bangladesh-to-Gulf labor corridors represent newly …

Full slide detail

  • Migration systems theory (Kritz, Lim & Zlotnik, 1992): migration flows and counterflows link origin and destination societies through economic, political, cultural, and historical ties that constitute relatively stable 'migration systems.'
  • Key features of a migration system: persistent bilateral flows, feedback loops (remittances, return, information), shared colonial or economic history, and network density — the Australia-UK corridor and the US-Mexico corridor are canonical examples.
  • The global migration system is hierarchical: wealthy destination countries exercise disproportionate power over the rules, including which visa categories exist, how asylum is processed, and how labor mobility is structured.
  • Globalization and migration have a paradoxical relationship: capital, goods, and information move with increasing freedom; labor — especially low-skill labor — faces intensifying restriction. This is the 'liberal paradox' Hollifield identifies.
  • Migration systems are not static: the Ukraine displacement crisis, Venezuelan emigration, and Bangladesh-to-Gulf labor corridors represent newly consolidated and rapidly evolving system components.

Instructor notes

The migration systems lens is useful for graduate courses because it shifts attention from individual decisions to the relational structures that sustain flows. For undergraduate classes, map a few specific systems on a whiteboard — US-Mexico, UK-South Asia, Gulf-South/Southeast Asia — and ask students to identify the colonial, economic, and social ties that sustain each. This demonstrates the theory without requiring students to read the technical literature.

Narration transcript

Migration is not a collection of unrelated individual decisions. It is a system — or rather, a set of overlapping systems — in which historical ties, economic interdependencies, social networks, and political arrangements structure who moves, where, and through what channels. Migration systems theory, developed in the early 1990s, asks us to think about stable, persistent sets of bilateral relationships between origin and destination regions. The US-Mexico system, consolidated over more than a century through the Bracero Program, family networks, and labor demand, is one of the most studied in the world. The Gulf state labor migration system, drawing workers from South and Southeast Asia under temporary contract regimes, is another. Each system has its own logic, its own history, and its own rules. Understanding the systemic character of migration is crucial for both analysis and policy: interventions that ignore system dynamics — like building walls or tightening visa quotas without addressing root causes — tend to redirect flows rather than stop them, often into more dangerous routes.

Key pointColombia hosts nearly 2.9 million Venezuelan refugees and migrants — one of the largest single-country hosting efforts in the world, without receiving the attention given to European reception.

Full slide detail

  • Venezuela has produced what R4V (the UN regional platform) calls the largest displacement crisis in the Western Hemisphere: as of mid-2024, 7.8 million Venezuelan refugees and migrants had fled the country, predominantly to Colombia (2.9 million), Peru (1 million), Ecuador, and Chile.
  • Drivers are multiple and entangled: economic hyperinflation (reached over 1 million percent in 2018), political repression under the Maduro government, healthcare system collapse, food insecurity, and violence — demonstrating the voluntary/forced continuum in a live case.
  • Almost all (97 percent) of displaced Venezuelans have remained in Latin America, challenging the 'Global South–to–North' migration stereotype and demonstrating South-South migration dynamics at scale.
  • Legal status is complex: most Venezuelans do not qualify as refugees under the narrow 1951 Convention definition but have received humanitarian protection under the broader 1984 Cartagena Declaration, which Latin American states have creatively applied.
  • The Venezuelan case has tested regional reception capacity: Colombia granted Temporary Protection Status (TPS) to nearly 1 million Venezuelans in 2021 — a major humanitarian innovation, praised as a model for integration-oriented responses.

Instructor notes

This case works at multiple levels: it exemplifies South-South migration, demonstrates multi-causal drivers, tests legal definitions, and showcases a policy innovation (Colombia's TPS) that is often overlooked in North-Atlantic-centric migration courses. Ask students: 'Why does the Venezuelan crisis receive less media attention in North America and Europe than the Syrian or Afghan crises? What does that tell us about how migration narratives are shaped?'

Narration transcript

To ground these theoretical frameworks in lived reality, consider Venezuela. By mid-2024, according to the UN's R4V platform, 7.8 million Venezuelans had fled the country — making this the largest displacement crisis in the Western Hemisphere. What drove them? The answer is almost impossible to disentangle: an economy in freefall, political repression, collapsed public services, violence. This is the voluntary-forced continuum in vivid form. Is a person who leaves because their salary is worth nothing and the hospitals have run out of medicine making a 'voluntary economic choice'? The Venezuelan case also challenges geographic stereotypes. 85 percent of displaced Venezuelans remain in Latin America — they did not head to Europe or North America. They are guests of Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, and Chile — middle-income countries under enormous strain. Colombia's decision to grant Temporary Protection Status to nearly one million Venezuelans in 2021 is one of the most significant humanitarian policy innovations of this decade, and it deserves far more attention in migration courses than it typically receives.

Concept

Cross-Disciplinary Perspectives on Migration

  1. Sociology examines migration's impact on social structures, stratification, identity, and community.
  2. Economics analyzes migration through labor markets, remittances, fiscal impacts, and development.
  3. Political science focuses on state sovereignty, immigration policy, citizenship, and the politics of belonging.
  4. Geography and demography track spatial patterns, population dynamics, and the 'geography of opportunity'
  5. Law and human rights provide the normative and procedural framework.

Full slide detail

  • Sociology examines migration's impact on social structures, stratification, identity, and community — migration reshapes families, gender roles, and class hierarchies in both sending and receiving societies.
  • Economics analyzes migration through labor markets, remittances, fiscal impacts, and development — findings are often more nuanced and more positive than political discourse suggests.
  • Political science focuses on state sovereignty, immigration policy, citizenship, and the politics of belonging — including the rise of restrictionist political movements across democratic polities.
  • Geography and demography track spatial patterns, population dynamics, and the 'geography of opportunity' — the uneven distribution of resources that shapes who moves and where.
  • Law and human rights provide the normative and procedural framework — international refugee law, domestic immigration law, human rights treaties — within which states define obligations and individuals claim protection.
  • Anthropology and cultural studies contribute attention to lived experience, identity, narrative, and the construction of belonging and exclusion — essential for centering human dignity.

Instructor notes

For faculty from non-social science disciplines, this slide validates their existing expertise as an entry point into migration studies. A sociologist brings one toolkit; an economist another; a theologian yet another. The course's interdisciplinary design is a strength, not a weakness. Invite faculty to identify which disciplinary lens feels most natural to them and where they feel most challenged to stretch.

Narration transcript

Migration is not owned by any single discipline. It is inherently interdisciplinary, and that is one reason it is such rich material for teaching. Sociologists bring frameworks for understanding stratification, community change, and identity. Economists bring tools for analyzing labor markets, remittances, and fiscal impacts. Political scientists examine how states manage migration, how parties mobilize around it, and what citizenship means in an era of global mobility. Geographers and demographers track the spatial and population dynamics — where flows originate, what routes they follow, how they reshape urban and rural communities. Lawyers and human rights scholars examine the normative frameworks — who has rights, under what conditions, enforceable by whom. And anthropologists and cultural studies scholars attend to lived experience, to the texture of what it means to be a migrant, to belong somewhere, or to be excluded. This course deliberately draws on all these perspectives. Faculty from different fields will find their disciplinary home here — and will be stretched toward unfamiliar terrain. That discomfort is pedagogically productive.

Full slide detail

  • Human dignity as a migration studies foundation: regardless of legal status, nationality, or origin, migrants are rights-bearing persons with inherent worth — a claim grounded in international human rights law and across major religious and secular ethical traditions.
  • The dignity frame does not predetermine policy outcomes: it is compatible with acknowledging that states have legitimate interests in managing borders; it insists, however, that these interests cannot override fundamental rights obligations.
  • The right to family unity, the prohibition of torture and degrading treatment, the right to seek asylum, and protections for children are all expressions of the dignity norm that carry legal force regardless of a person's immigration status.
  • Pope Francis in 'Laudato Si' (2015) and 'Laudate Deum' (2023) and the Catholic Social Teaching tradition more broadly frame the response to migration in terms of 'welcoming, protecting, promoting, and integrating' — a framework that has influenced humanitarian practice globally.
  • For secular institutions: the dignity frame maps onto human rights law and cosmopolitan political philosophy (Benhabib, Nussbaum) without requiring religious commitment.

Instructor notes

Given the Villanova/Cabrini Institute context, this slide makes the course's values explicit without being exclusionary. Emphasize that the dignity frame is also grounded in international human rights law, accessible to secular and religious institutions alike. The point is not that all migration should be unrestricted but that the humanity of every person in the migration system — border agent, asylum seeker, undocumented worker, unaccompanied child — is inviolable.

Narration transcript

Before we close this foundational module, it is important to name one framing that runs through the entire course: human dignity. Whatever our disciplinary background, whatever policy positions we hold or teach, every person in the migration system — every person who has crossed a border, who is waiting in a camp, who is working without documents, who is manning a border post — is a human being with inherent worth. This is not a naive or purely aspirational claim. It is grounded in international human rights law. The prohibition of torture, the right to seek asylum, the protections for children — these are legally binding obligations, not optional courtesies. They apply regardless of whether a person has the right paperwork. The human dignity frame does not resolve the hard policy questions — it does not tell us exactly how many people a state must admit, or what kind of enforcement is proportionate. But it insists that these questions must be answered within constraints set by the irreducible humanity of every person involved. That is the foundation from which this course operates.

Narration transcript

This module's signature experience is a reflective essay. The prompt asks: what shapes migration narratives — in your discipline, your media environment, and your own prior assumptions — and how has this first module challenged or confirmed those narratives? Notice that this exercise does not ask students to take a policy position. It asks them to examine the frames through which they have been learning about migration before this course began. That is a methodological practice, not just a personal exercise. Good researchers in any field surface their own positionality. They ask: what am I assuming? Where did that assumption come from? What would I have to see to update it? Graduate students are asked to extend this by applying two or more theoretical frameworks from the module to a specific migration flow and evaluating what each one illuminates. Both versions of the assignment serve the same core goal: moving from unreflective assumption to examined knowledge.

Module 1 Takeaways: Foundations of Global Migration

  1. Migration is a fundamental human behavior, not an aberration.
  2. Core legal definitions (refugee, asylum seeker, IDP, stateless) have precise meanings with real protective consequences.
  3. The voluntary/forced binary is analytically insufficient.
  4. No single theory explains migration — neoclassical economics, new economics of labor migration, dual labor market theory, world systems theory, network theory, and …
  5. Migration categorization is political: labels carry legal consequences.
  6. The human dignity frame, grounded in both international human rights law and major ethical traditions, provides a normative foundation that is compatible with …

Full slide detail

  • Migration is a fundamental human behavior, not an aberration — 304 million international migrants in 2024 represent 3.7 percent of world population, with far more migration occurring internally and regionally.
  • Core legal definitions (refugee, asylum seeker, IDP, stateless) have precise meanings with real protective consequences — confusing them produces analytical and policy errors.
  • The voluntary/forced binary is analytically insufficient; Carling's aspiration/ability framework captures the spectrum more accurately, including involuntary immobility.
  • No single theory explains migration — neoclassical economics, new economics of labor migration, dual labor market theory, world systems theory, network theory, and transnationalism each illuminate different scales and mechanisms.
  • Migration categorization is political: labels carry legal consequences; methodological nationalism — the assumption that the nation-state is the natural unit of analysis — embeds hidden biases in how we count and describe migrants.
  • The human dignity frame, grounded in both international human rights law and major ethical traditions, provides a normative foundation that is compatible with rigorous policy analysis.

Instructor notes

Return to these takeaways at the start of Module 2. Invite students to add one additional insight they are carrying forward from the week's readings and discussion. Building cumulative synthesis habits early in the course pays dividends later when frameworks from multiple modules must be held simultaneously.

Narration transcript

Let us consolidate what we have covered in this opening module. Human migration is as old as humanity itself — the analytical and policy challenge is not the movement but the systems of control, protection, and meaning-making that have grown up around it. We have established the core vocabulary: migrant, refugee, asylum seeker, IDP, stateless person — each a distinct legal and analytical category with real consequences. We have seen that the voluntary-forced binary dissolves under scrutiny into a continuum, and that Carling's aspiration-ability framework is more precise and more humane than the blunt binary. We have surveyed the major theoretical traditions, finding each illuminating and each incomplete. We have seen that the act of defining and counting migrants is itself political. And we have grounded the entire enterprise in a human dignity frame that does not resolve policy debates but insists they must respect the irreducible humanity of every person in the migration system. These are the foundations. The next twelve modules build on them.
