Drivers of Migration

Why do people move — and why can't they always?

AnalyticalSystems Thinking

Statistics updated June 2026 (UNHCR Global Trends 2026, IOM World Migration Report 2026). Audio narration may cite the earlier end-2024 figures.

Narration transcript

If the first two modules gave us vocabulary and data, this module asks the explanatory question: what actually causes migration? The answer is always plural and always contextual. Economic deprivation, political violence, environmental stress, family networks, cultural aspiration, and structural constraint interact in ways that resist simple monocausal explanations. This module introduces the frameworks for analyzing migration drivers — and challenges students to apply them to real, complex cases.

Key pointLee's push-pull model, published in 1966, remains ubiquitous in migration teaching — yet it was designed for a world quite different from the one migrants navigate today.

Full slide detail

  • Lee's (1966) push-pull model: migration results from origin-area negatives (push), destination-area positives (pull), intervening obstacles (cost, distance, border enforcement), and personal factors (risk tolerance, information, networks).
  • The model's enduring intuitive power: it organizes a wide range of empirical observations and can accommodate many factors; it remains the dominant framework in introductory migration teaching.
  • Massey (2018) argues that the push-pull lens was designed for a world of economic migration within relatively stable nation-states and fundamentally mischaracterizes twenty-first-century drivers: climate change, forced displacement, South-South flows, and digital network effects require updated frameworks.
  • Structural critique: push-pull treats migrants as individual rational actors making cost-benefit calculations; it understates the degree to which structural forces — colonialism, global inequality, debt regimes — constrain and channel individual choices.
  • A balanced teaching approach: retain push-pull as an accessible entry point, then layer structural, contextual, and agentic critiques to build toward multi-causal understanding.

Instructor notes

Begin by asking students to list push and pull factors for a specific case — say, migration from Honduras to the United States. They will generate a rich list quickly. Then ask: 'Why doesn't everyone who experiences these push factors migrate?' The answer — intervening obstacles, personal factors, and the aspirations-capabilities gap — is the wedge into more sophisticated frameworks. This exercise takes about 10 minutes and immediately demonstrates the model's limitations.

Narration transcript

Everett Lee's push-pull model, published in 1966, is probably the most widely taught framework in migration studies — and for good reason. Its logic is intuitive: people leave places that have too little of what they need and move toward places that offer more, subject to obstacles along the way and filtered by personal characteristics. Applied to almost any migration flow, it generates a useful initial analysis. But as Douglas Massey argued in a 2018 article, the push-pull lens has a serious limitation: it was designed for a twentieth-century world of orderly economic migration between relatively stable nation-states, and it strains badly when applied to twenty-first-century realities. Climate change produces displacement that is neither purely 'economic' nor 'political' in the classic sense. Forced displacement crises — Syria, Sudan, Myanmar — involve combinations of political violence, economic collapse, and environmental stress that cannot be neatly separated into push and pull columns. South-South migration defies the assumption that the 'pull' is always a wealthy destination. And the role of digital communication networks in shaping both information about destinations and social connections across borders is structurally new. Push-pull remains a useful starting point — but only a starting point.

Full slide detail

  • Wage differentials between countries remain one of the most powerful predictors of migration propensity: average manufacturing wages in Mexico are roughly one-fifth of US wages — a differential that creates sustained migration pressure regardless of enforcement intensity.
  • The 'migration hump' (Martin & Taylor, 1996): migration rates initially increase as incomes rise in sending countries (more people can afford to move) before eventually declining as wage gaps close — meaning development may temporarily increase migration before reducing it.
  • Labor demand in receiving countries drives migration independently of conditions in sending countries: Piore's dual labor market theory holds that low-wage secondary labor markets in wealthy economies permanently demand migrant labor that native workers reject.
  • Remittances as economic incentive: households in high-emigration communities may come to depend structurally on remittance income, creating 'cultures of migration' where out-migration becomes a normative household strategy.
  • Structural inequality at the global level: the world's richest 10 percent of countries hold over 50 percent of global income (World Inequality Database); this structural configuration is the deep condition that makes wage-gap migration predictable.

Instructor notes

The migration hump is counterintuitive and worth dwelling on: development assistance intended to reduce migration may, in the short run, increase it. This has important policy implications for initiatives like the EU Trust Fund for Africa. Graduate students should engage with de Haas's (2010) critique of the development-as-migration-reduction assumption.

Narration transcript

Economic drivers are the most commonly invoked explanation for migration, and they are genuinely powerful. The wage gap between sending and receiving countries is one of the best predictors we have of migration propensity. Average manufacturing wages in Mexico are roughly one-fifth of US wages. In the Philippines, a nurse or engineer can triple their income by working abroad. These differentials create sustained structural incentives that persist across generations. Michael Piore's insight adds an important supply-side dimension: wealthy economies do not just attract migrants — they structurally need them. The secondary labor market, characterized by dirty, dangerous, and low-status work that native workers increasingly refuse, creates permanent demand that employers fill with migrants, often through irregular channels when regular ones are unavailable. And remittances are both an effect and a cause: once a household strategy of sending members abroad becomes established in a community, the income it generates creates dependencies that reinforce the practice across generations — what scholars call 'cultures of migration.'

Key pointSudan's civil war generated 14.3 million displaced people by end-2024 — yet received far less international media and policy attention than smaller crises in wealthier regions.

Full slide detail

  • Political persecution, state repression, civil war, and targeted violence are the primary drivers of forced displacement; the 1951 Convention refugee definition focuses specifically on these political triggers.
  • The Sudan displacement crisis (2024): Sudan's civil war between the national army and the Rapid Support Forces generated 14.3 million displaced Sudanese by end-2024 — 3.5 million more than 2023 — making it the largest displacement crisis globally (UNHCR Global Trends 2025).
  • Gang and criminal violence: displacement by organized crime and gang violence — as in Honduras, El Salvador, and Guatemala's 'Northern Triangle' — creates forced migration that often does not qualify under the 1951 Convention's narrow persecution grounds, producing a systematic protection gap.
  • State fragility: UNHCR data consistently shows that the world's largest displacement crises originate in the world's most fragile or failed states (Sudan, Afghanistan, Syria, Democratic Republic of Congo, Somalia); fragility creates both direct and indirect displacement drivers.
  • Conflict-environment nexus: Groth et al. (2020), studying Ethiopia, show that conflict and environmental stress co-occur and interact; neither alone fully explains who migrates, how far, or where.

Instructor notes

The gang violence/Northern Triangle case is pedagogically important because it illustrates a systematic protection gap: people fleeing genuine life-threatening violence who cannot access refugee protection because their persecutors are non-state actors in a legally ambiguous gray zone. This generates rich discussion about the adequacy of the 1951 Convention framework and what reforms might look like.

Narration transcript

Political and conflict drivers are distinct from economic drivers in an important sense: they produce forced displacement rather than voluntary migration. When a government imprisons dissidents, when an ethnic minority faces targeted violence, when a civil war makes an entire region uninhabitable, the people who flee are not choosing among employment options — they are escaping danger. The 1951 Convention was built precisely to respond to this category of driver. But the political driver landscape has evolved since 1951. Gang violence and organized crime create conditions of extreme insecurity in parts of Central America that are functionally indistinguishable from war — yet the 1951 definition does not easily accommodate non-state persecutors without clear political motivation. Sudan, in 2024, generated the single largest displacement crisis in the world: 14.3 million people displaced by the civil war between the national army and the Rapid Support Forces — receiving far less sustained international attention than smaller crises in parts of the world more visible to North Atlantic media. This selectivity in global attention is itself a subject worth examining.

Full slide detail

  • Environmental drivers of migration span a spectrum from rapid-onset events (floods, cyclones, earthquakes) to slow-onset processes (drought, desertification, sea-level rise, glacier melt) — the latter are harder to attribute and therefore legally invisible.
  • IDMC estimated 26.4 million new displacement events from weather-related disasters in 2023 alone — more than double displacements from conflict — though many are short-term and return-oriented rather than permanent migration.
  • The Sahel: a persistent drought belt in combination with conflict has produced cyclical displacement across Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso, and Chad, generating some of the most complex multi-causal migration systems in the world.
  • Pacific Islands: low-lying island states including Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands face existential threat from sea-level rise; this is the clearest case of climate-driven migration where the cause is unambiguous — yet international law provides no dedicated protection framework.
  • Groth et al. (2020) in Ethiopia: using Qualitative Comparative Analysis across multiple sites, they show that environmental stress alone does not drive migration — capabilities (assets, networks) and aspirations must also be present; poverty without aspirations or networks can produce immobility.

Instructor notes

The Groth et al. (2020) Ethiopia finding is counterintuitive and valuable: environmental stress does not mechanically cause migration. Poor people who lack networks or assets may be stuck in place — environmentally induced immobility — while those with more resources migrate. This directly connects to Carling's aspiration/ability framework and challenges environmental determinism. the 'Climate Change and Migration' module covers climate displacement in depth; this slide introduces the theme early.

Narration transcript

Environmental drivers have moved from the margins to the center of migration research and policy debate over the past decade — accelerated by deepening evidence of climate change's distributional impacts. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre estimated 26.4 million new displacements from weather-related disasters in 2023, more than double the figure from conflict. But these numbers require careful interpretation. Most weather-driven displacements are short-term — people evacuating a flood zone and returning when conditions improve — rather than the permanent international migration that dominates political debate. The harder and more consequential question concerns slow-onset processes: the multi-decade drying of the Sahel, the progressive inundation of Pacific island atolls, the retreat of Himalayan glaciers that water entire river systems. These changes unfold over decades, making them legally and politically invisible: there is no moment of emergency, no discrete event that triggers a protection claim. And as Groth and colleagues showed in Ethiopia, environmental stress does not produce migration mechanically. It interacts with the capabilities and aspirations of affected households — meaning that the most environmentally stressed communities are not necessarily the ones that produce the most migrants, because extreme poverty can produce immobility.

Full slide detail

  • Carling's (2002) framework: migration is the outcome of the intersection of aspiration (the desire to move) and ability (the capacity to move); these are independent axes that produce four analytically distinct conditions.
  • The four conditions: (1) aspiration + ability = migration; (2) no aspiration + no ability = immobility by choice and circumstance; (3) no aspiration + ability = voluntary immobility (can move but chooses not to); (4) aspiration + no ability = involuntary immobility — trapped.
  • Involuntary immobility (Carling, 2002): the condition of wishing to migrate but lacking the resources, documents, legal status, or physical capacity to do so — produces documented psychological and economic harm distinct from voluntary non-migration.
  • Vanden Hautte et al. (2025), in a scoping review of 13 studies, confirm that involuntary immobility is associated with anxiety, depression, hopelessness, and reduced quality of life — the well-being costs of being unable to leave.
  • COVID-19 as a natural experiment: Jolivet, Fransen, Adger et al. (2023), drawing on data from six cities across four continents, show how pandemic border closures simultaneously and unequally suppressed both aspirations and abilities to migrate, revealing the framework's empirical applicability.

Instructor notes

The aspiration/ability framework is one of the most teachable theoretical contributions in contemporary migration studies because it generates immediate intuitive clarity. Draw the two-by-two grid on the board and ask students to populate each quadrant with real examples from migration cases they have studied or experienced. The COVID border closure case from Jolivet et al. is particularly valuable: students can recall their own experiences of pandemic travel restrictions and apply the framework to themselves.

Narration transcript

One of the most intellectually powerful tools for understanding why people migrate — or do not migrate — is Jørgen Carling's aspiration-ability framework, first published in 2002 and still generating productive empirical and theoretical work today. The key insight is simple but profound: migration is the intersection of wanting to move and being able to move. These are independent dimensions. A person may strongly desire to move but lack the resources, the documents, the legal status, the family support, or the physical capacity to do so. Carling calls this condition 'involuntary immobility' — being trapped in place against one's will. This population is almost entirely invisible in migration statistics, because statistical systems count movers, not stayers-who-wish-they-were-movers. But their experience is real and documented. Vanden Hautte and colleagues, reviewing the evidence base in a 2025 scoping review, confirm that involuntary immobility is associated with anxiety, depression, and diminished life satisfaction — the psychological costs of unfulfilled aspirations. The COVID-19 pandemic provided a global natural experiment in what happens when both aspirations and abilities are simultaneously disrupted, and Jolivet and colleagues' cross-national study shows that the framework travels well empirically.

Case study

Case Mapping: Multi-Causal Drivers in One Flow — Northern Triangle to the US

Key pointThe GDP per capita differential between Honduras and the United States exceeds 13 to 1. Yet not all poor Hondurans migrate — which means poverty alone is insufficient as an explanation.

Full slide detail

  • The Northern Triangle of Central America (Honduras, Guatemala, El Salvador): one of the most studied multi-causal migration systems, combining gang violence and criminal extortion, extreme poverty and inequality, climate variability (droughts, hurricanes), family separation from earlier migration cycles, and political corruption.
  • Economic dimension: the GDP per capita gap between the US ($80,000 PPP) and Honduras ($6,000 PPP) creates a wage differential of over 13 to 1 — among the largest source-destination differentials in the world, generating sustained economic migration pressure.
  • Violence dimension: El Salvador and Honduras have periodically ranked among the world's highest homicide-rate countries; MS-13 and Barrio 18 extortion of businesses and recruitment of youth creates direct flight-level threat that meets the functional criteria of forced migration even when it fails the legal standard.
  • Environmental dimension: the 'Dry Corridor' of Central America has experienced repeated severe droughts, the 2020 back-to-back hurricanes Eta and Iota destroyed crops and infrastructure in Honduras, driving new displacement waves.
  • Structural dimension: the US Bracero Program (1942–1964) recruited Mexican and Central American workers through bilateral agreement — a historical labor recruitment policy that established networks, expectations, and dependencies that persist decades after the program ended.
  • The case demonstrates that no single framework — economic, conflict, environmental, network — adequately explains the flow; all operate simultaneously and interactively.

Instructor notes

This is the module's signature experience case. Assign students to produce a 'layered drivers map' for this or another flow: a visual or tabular representation of economic, political, environmental, and network drivers, showing how they interact. The key learning goal is demonstrating multi-causality — that no single factor explains the flow, and that the interaction of factors matters. Ask: if Honduras's economy doubled in size, would migration to the US stop? Why or why not?

Narration transcript

Let us apply the multi-causal framework to a specific case: migration from Central America's Northern Triangle — Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador — to the United States. This is one of the most intensively studied migration systems in the world, and its drivers are impossible to disentangle cleanly. There is an enormous economic dimension: the GDP gap between Honduras and the United States exceeds 13 to 1, creating structural incentives as powerful as any in the world. There is a violence dimension: gang extortion and recruitment create direct personal threats that are functionally forced migration even when they fail the 1951 Convention's strict legal test. There is an environmental dimension: the Dry Corridor of Central America has experienced deepening droughts, and back-to-back hurricanes in 2020 destroyed entire agricultural regions. And there is a network dimension rooted in history: the US Bracero Program actively recruited Central American labor from the 1940s to the 1960s, creating networks, expectations, and migration cultures that are now multigenerational. These drivers do not take turns. They operate simultaneously and interact. A household that is economically stressed becomes more vulnerable to gang extortion; a drought that destroys crops accelerates economic stress; a family network in Los Angeles makes the aspiration to migrate concrete. This is what multi-causality means — and it is why policy interventions that address only one driver so rarely produce the outcomes their designers anticipate.

Networks, Social Capital, and the Perpetuation of Migration

  1. Migrant networks personal relationships connecting migrants to would-be migrants in origin communities that reduce information costs …
  2. Network effects once established, networks generate self-perpetuating flows that persist even when original economic push or pull …
  3. Social capital theoryPortes, 1995 migrant networks are a form of social capital that provides access to resources through social relationships.
  4. Institutional theoryMassey et al., 1993 as migration flows grow, a set of private profit-seeking institutions (smugglers, recruiters, legal services, housing …
  5. The dark side of networks the same networks that facilitate regular migration facilitate irregular migration and trafficking.

Full slide detail

  • Migrant networks: personal relationships connecting migrants to would-be migrants in origin communities that reduce information costs (where to go, which employer, how to cross) and economic costs (housing, credit, employment) at the destination.
  • Network effects: once established, networks generate self-perpetuating flows that persist even when original economic push or pull factors weaken — explaining why migration corridors persist for decades after initial conditions have changed.
  • Social capital theory (Portes, 1995): migrant networks are a form of social capital that provides access to resources through social relationships — but its distribution is unequal within communities, benefiting those with stronger social ties.
  • Institutional theory (Massey et al., 1993): as migration flows grow, a set of private profit-seeking institutions (smugglers, recruiters, legal services, housing agencies) develops to facilitate migration, creating a commercial infrastructure that outlasts initial drivers.
  • The dark side of networks: the same networks that facilitate regular migration facilitate irregular migration and trafficking; they are not inherently protective or exploitative — their character depends on the regulatory environment.

Instructor notes

Network theory is one of the most empirically well-supported in migration studies, and students can see it operating in their own communities. A reflective exercise: ask students to think of an example of a migration corridor in their own family or community history — where people moved from and to over generations — and identify the network mechanisms (specific family members as pioneers, established neighborhoods, ethnic businesses) that sustained the flow.

Narration transcript

Once a migration flow is established, social networks take on an independent explanatory role. This is one of the most powerful and empirically robust insights in migration theory. Migrant networks — the personal ties that connect people already at a destination with people still at the origin — reduce the costs and risks of migration for subsequent movers. They provide information, housing, job contacts, and emotional support. As network density increases, migration becomes progressively easier and cheaper for community members, which is why specific villages in Mexico send migrants to specific cities in the United States, why particular regions of Bangladesh are connected to particular industries in the United Kingdom, and why these patterns persist across generations. The commercial infrastructure of migration — recruitment agencies, remittance services, smuggling operations, legal service providers — develops alongside networks and creates yet another layer of institutional self-perpetuation. Understanding this dynamic is essential for policy: it means that migration flows cannot be switched off simply by removing the original economic push factor. The network has its own momentum.

Full slide detail

  • Fresnoza-Flot (2024) proposes a 'situated framework' for migration decision-making that centers human agency, emotional dimensions, and relational contexts — challenging mainstream frameworks that overemphasize rational cost-benefit calculation.
  • Migration decisions are embedded in relationships: the decision to migrate is rarely purely individual; it involves negotiation with family members, community expectations, gender norms, and obligations of care.
  • Gendered dimensions: migration aspirations and abilities are distributed unequally along gender lines; women migrants face different structural constraints, different network access, and different risks; their migration decisions are shaped by gendered norms around mobility, care, and safety.
  • The role of emotion: hope, fear, grief, shame, pride, and obligation all shape migration decisions in ways that purely rational models cannot capture — yet these emotions are not irrational, they reflect the real stakes of the decision.
  • Castelli (2018) synthesizes the empirical drivers literature and confirms that no single category of driver — economic, political, environmental, social — is sufficient: migration is multi-determined and context-dependent.

Instructor notes

This slide connects to the 'Migration Journey and Experience' module's trauma content and the 'Migrant Lives and Identities' module's identity content. For faculty who work in disciplines that privilege quantitative analysis, the humanizing dimension can feel methodologically soft. Reframe: qualitative research on migration decision-making is methodologically rigorous and produces insights that surveys and censuses cannot generate. Fresnoza-Flot's situated framework is a valuable tool for faculty who want to integrate humanistic approaches.

Narration transcript

Even the most sophisticated structural and economic frameworks can underestimate the human dimensions of migration decisions. Asuncion Fresnoza-Flot's 2024 article proposes a situated framework that brings human agency, emotional life, and relational context back to the center of migration theory. Migration decisions happen inside families, inside communities, inside webs of obligation and aspiration that are irreducibly human. The decision of a mother in the Philippines to leave her children with a grandmother and work as a nurse in Saudi Arabia for three years is not primarily a wage-optimization calculation. It involves love, sacrifice, fear, pride, and an assessment of who she owes what to whom. The decision of a young man in Senegal to risk the Atlantic crossing to the Canary Islands is not purely a response to economic push factors. It is also a response to masculine norms about proving oneself, to the social shame of failure, to the hope that he will be the one who makes it. These dimensions do not make the decision irrational. They make it human. And teaching migration well requires holding structural analysis and humanistic understanding in productive tension.

Debate

Why Policies Aimed at Reducing Migration Often Fail

  1. CastlesCastles, 2017 immigration policies are 'problematic because they are about migration'
  2. The deterrence paradox increasing enforcement costs and risks of irregular migration may shift routes (to more dangerous paths) or reduce …
  3. The development paradox aid and investment in sending countries may initially increase migration (by raising aspirations and abilities) before …
  4. Network resilience migration networks survive enforcement crackdowns by adapting: when one route closes, another opens.
  5. Multi-causal structural drivers mean that single-lever interventions are inherently limited: addressing only economic inequality, or only conflict, or only …
Key pointCastles (2017): migration policies fail systematically because states apply a 'flow management' model to what is fundamentally a process of social transformation. You cannot manage social transformation with a tap.

Full slide detail

  • Castles (2017): immigration policies are 'problematic because they are about migration' — states apply simple management models to social transformation processes, and the mismatch systematically produces policy failure.
  • The deterrence paradox: increasing enforcement costs and risks of irregular migration may shift routes (to more dangerous paths) or reduce returns (keeping migrants in place who would otherwise circulate) without reducing total flows.
  • The development paradox: aid and investment in sending countries may initially increase migration (by raising aspirations and abilities) before eventually reducing it — the migration hump — meaning short-run and long-run effects of development are opposite.
  • Network resilience: migration networks survive enforcement crackdowns by adapting: when one route closes, another opens; when one smuggling network is disrupted, another fills the space.
  • Multi-causal structural drivers mean that single-lever interventions are inherently limited: addressing only economic inequality, or only conflict, or only environmental stress, while leaving other drivers in place, produces at best partial and temporary reductions in flow.

Instructor notes

This is a provocative but evidence-grounded conclusion: immigration restriction policies tend to be less effective than political discourse suggests. This does not mean they have no effect — they do shift where, how, and who migrates. But they rarely achieve their stated goal of substantially reducing flows, and they often produce serious unintended consequences. Teach this without political valence: the evidence is relevant to evaluating any policy, regardless of political orientation.

Narration transcript

One of the most important — and politically contentious — empirical findings in migration studies is that policies designed to reduce migration through restriction and enforcement tend to be systematically less effective than their proponents claim. Stephen Castles made this argument with particular force in a 2017 essay: states apply a 'flow management' model to migration as if people were water in a pipe that can be redirected with enough pressure. But migration is not a flow — it is a social transformation, driven by structural inequalities, historical ties, and human networks that do not respond linearly to enforcement interventions. The deterrence paradox is well-documented: higher enforcement costs at the US-Mexico border since the 1990s did not reduce the stock of undocumented migrants in the US — it reduced circular migration, meaning migrants who would previously have moved back and forth decided to stay permanently once they crossed. Unintended consequence. Development aid intended to reduce migration from Africa may initially increase it — the migration hump — before eventually reducing it. Another unintended consequence. This does not mean borders are meaningless or that policy cannot shape migration. It means that effective policy requires understanding the multi-causal system, not treating migration as a simple problem with a simple solution.

Definition

Defining Migration Drivers: A Conceptual Vocabulary

Push factor
a condition at the origin that reduces the utility of remaining.
Pull factor
an attractive condition at the destination — higher wages, political stability, family networks, legal pathways.
Intervening obstacles
factors that impede or facilitate movement between origin and destination, including distance, border enforcement, visa …
Structural driver
a systemic condition that produces migration pressure at a macro level.
Precipitating event
a specific occurrence that triggers migration that structural conditions have made likely.
DefinitionLee (1966): migration is a function of push factors at origin, pull factors at destination, intervening obstacles, and personal factors. Simple but enduring — and critics note it understates structural forces.

Full slide detail

  • Push factor: a condition at the origin that reduces the utility of remaining — poverty, violence, political persecution, environmental degradation, lack of opportunity; pushes are negatives at origin in Lee's (1966) classical formulation.
  • Pull factor: an attractive condition at the destination — higher wages, political stability, family networks, legal pathways; pulls are positives at destination in Lee's model.
  • Intervening obstacles: factors that impede or facilitate movement between origin and destination, including distance, border enforcement, visa regimes, cost, and legal barriers; Lee's inclusion of obstacles was ahead of its time and anticipates the capabilities dimension of the aspirations/ability framework.
  • Structural driver: a systemic condition that produces migration pressure at a macro level — trade patterns, colonial histories, climate systems, global wage differentials; structural drivers operate regardless of individual decisions and constrain what individuals can choose.
  • Precipitating event: a specific occurrence that triggers migration that structural conditions have made likely — a drought after years of rural poverty, a political crackdown after years of repression, a factory closure after years of industrial decline; most 'sudden' migrations have long structural pre-histories.

Instructor notes

This definitional slide provides the vocabulary students need for the case mapping assignment. Making the distinction between structural drivers and precipitating events explicit is important: it helps students avoid the common misreading that migration is 'caused' by a single event (a drought, a gang murder) without attention to the background conditions that made flight the only available response. The distinction also helps explain why addressing 'root causes' is so difficult — structural drivers are deeply embedded.

Narration transcript

Before we go deeper into specific driver categories, let us establish the vocabulary. The classic framework — still widely used as a starting point despite its limitations — is Everett Lee's 1966 push-pull model. Lee identified four elements: push factors at the origin (conditions that make staying costly or dangerous), pull factors at the destination (conditions that make moving attractive), intervening obstacles (barriers that must be overcome to move), and personal factors (individual characteristics that affect how people respond to the same structural conditions). This was actually quite sophisticated for its time: Lee's inclusion of intervening obstacles anticipates what Carling would later call the 'ability' dimension of migration — the recognition that wanting to move and being able to move are different things. But the framework has been criticized for treating migration as if it were simply a rational calculation by individuals, underplaying the structural forces that constrain individual choice. It is also important to distinguish structural drivers — which create conditions that make migration likely over a long period — from precipitating events, the specific occurrences that trigger the actual departure. Most migrations that appear sudden actually have long structural pre-histories.

  • Wage differentials: World Bank data indicates that average GDP per capita (purchasing power parity) in high-income OECD countries was approximately $52,000 in 2022, compared to approximately $2,400 in low-income countries — a 20-to-1 differential that represents powerful economic migration pressure.

  • Poverty and migration: contrary to the assumption that poverty drives international migration, research consistently shows that the very poorest populations migrate the least — they lack the resources to move; middle-income populations in developing countries migrate more, creating what migration scholars call a 'migration hump' as development proceeds.
  • Multi-causal: Castelli (2018) and Groth et al. (2020) both document that real migration events combine multiple drivers; the 'single cause' narrative (this migration is 'economic'; that migration is 'conflict-driven') obscures the layered realities that migrants actually navigate.

Instructor notes

The 'migration hump' finding — that very poor populations migrate less, not more — consistently surprises students and challenges the common assumption that poverty straightforwardly drives international migration. It is one of the most policy-relevant findings in migration economics: it suggests that rapid development in low-income countries may initially increase rather than decrease emigration, with important implications for policies that frame development aid as a migration-reduction tool.

Narration transcript

Let us look at some of the key figures that quantify migration drivers globally. The economic driver is perhaps the most quantifiable: World Bank data shows that GDP per capita in high-income OECD countries is roughly twenty times that of low-income countries on a purchasing power parity basis. This is an enormous wage differential, and it creates powerful economic incentives for migration. But here is the counterintuitive finding: the very poorest people migrate internationally the least. Migration costs money — transport, visas, smuggler fees, subsistence costs during transit. People in extreme poverty often lack the resources to move far. This creates what researchers call a 'migration hump': as countries develop and incomes rise somewhat above subsistence, international migration initially increases, not decreases. For conflict-driven displacement, the scale at end of 2024 was historically unprecedented: UNHCR documented 123.2 million forcibly displaced people globally. Sudan, Syria, Afghanistan, and Ukraine together accounted for more than a third of all forcibly displaced people worldwide. And climate projections add a further dimension: the World Bank's Groundswell report projected that, without significant climate action, up to 216 million people could be internally displaced by 2050 due to slow-onset climate impacts alone.

Instructor notes

This discussion slide is designed to surface the relationship between analytical framing and policy prescription — one of the most important meta-analytical skills for students of migration. The same person's migration can be framed very differently depending on which factors you emphasize, and those different framings lead to different legal categories and policy responses. The signature experience for the 'Drivers of Migration' module is a case mapping exercise, and this discussion slide prepares students to think about how the layers of drivers in a real case interact.

Narration transcript

Let us apply the frameworks we have developed to a concrete case. A young woman in rural Guatemala leaves for the United States in 2023. Her village has experienced two consecutive failed harvests due to drought. Her family's small business is being extorted by a gang. Her sister is already working in Los Angeles and is sending remittances home. And there is no legal visa pathway for unskilled agricultural workers from Guatemala to enter the United States. Now: what drove her migration? If you emphasize the wage differential and family reunification, she looks like a voluntary economic migrant making a rational choice. If you emphasize the drought, the extortion, the absence of legal alternatives, she looks like someone with severely constrained options in which 'staying' carried risks that cannot fairly be called a free choice. Asuncion Fresnoza-Flot's 2024 work argues that both explanations are partial — that we need a framework that holds structural constraints and individual emotional dimensions together, acknowledging aspiration and agency without denying the real limits that structure the choice. The pedagogically important question is whether the analytical framing you choose is neutral — or whether it encodes policy preferences. I would argue it does, and that making that relationship explicit is essential intellectual honesty.

Key takeaways

Takeaways: Drivers of Migration

  1. Migration is always multi-caused: economic, political, environmental, and social-network drivers interact and reinforce each other.
  2. Lee's push-pull model is a useful entry point but understates structural forces and treats migrants as purely rational individual actors.
  3. Carling's aspiration/ability framework captures the voluntary-forced continuum and makes involuntary immobility analytically visible.
  4. Networks are both cause and self-perpetuating mechanism of migration — once established, they sustain flows that outlast original structural drivers.
  5. Immigration restriction policies tend to be less effective than proponents claim because they engage only one dimension of a multi-causal system.

Narration transcript

Module 3 has taken us inside the question of why people move. The answer is always plural. Economic wage gaps, political persecution, environmental stress, network connections, cultural aspiration, structural constraint — these are not competing explanations but complementary lenses, each illuminating a different dimension of a multidimensional reality. The case of Northern Triangle migration to the United States demonstrates what multi-causality looks like in practice: no single driver is sufficient, all are simultaneously operative, and their interaction creates dynamics that no single-variable policy can address. The aspiration-ability framework gives us analytical precision: we can now distinguish between voluntary migration, involuntary migration, voluntary immobility, and the profoundly important and neglected category of involuntary immobility. The module closes with a sobering empirical finding: policies aimed at reducing migration through enforcement alone tend to fail because they engage only one lever of a multi-causal system. In Module 4, we will step back further and examine the historical roots of the migration systems we see today — because understanding drivers in the present requires understanding where they came from.
