Borders, Sovereignty, and Policy

How do states regulate migration — and what legal, ethical, and practical limits constrain that authority?

LegalAnalytical

Statistics updated June 2026 (UNHCR Global Trends 2026, IOM World Migration Report 2026). Audio narration may cite the earlier end-2024 figures.

Narration transcript

Migration governance sits at the intersection of two powerful but sometimes incompatible principles: state sovereignty — the right of a government to control who enters its territory — and international human rights law, which constrains how that control may be exercised. This module examines how borders work legally and practically, what tools states use to manage migration, and where the tension between domestic authority and international obligation plays out in law, policy, and lived experience.

Concept

State Sovereignty and the Right to Control Migration

Key pointHollifield (2004): the 'liberal paradox' — liberal states need open economies but face political pressure for closed borders. Rights-based constraints limit closure, while economic interests limit openness. The result is permanent tension.

Full slide detail

  • Under international law, states retain the sovereign right to control admission to their territory; no treaty generally requires a state to admit non-citizens (with narrow exceptions such as diplomatic status and refugee non-refoulement).
  • Hollifield's (2004) concept of the 'liberal paradox': liberal democracies face a structural tension between the economic openness they need (requiring labor mobility) and the political pressure for closure; rights-based constraints inscribed in constitutions and international treaties limit what governments can actually do to exclude.
  • The 'migration state' concept: as states have built more elaborate border control apparatus, they have also accumulated more international legal obligations — creating a ratchet effect in which more control generates more legal vulnerability.
  • Historical evolution: the universalized system of passports and visas as a requirement for movement emerged after World War I; prior to 1914, much of international movement was relatively unrestricted; border control is a twentieth-century institutional construction, not a natural feature of the international order.
  • The politics-policy gap: public demand for strict immigration control consistently outstrips what governments can achieve in practice; Stephen Castles (2017) argues this is because migration is a social transformation process, not a manageable flow — states are trying to control something that operates at a deeper level than policy can reach.

Instructor notes

Begin by establishing the baseline: international law does give states broad rights to control entry. This is important to acknowledge before introducing the constraints, so students don't misread the legal framework. The liberal paradox concept helps explain why immigration policy so often frustrates everyone — restrictionists find restrictions don't work, and advocates find protections are inadequate. Hollifield's article is foundational reading for graduate students.

Narration transcript

Let us start from the most basic legal principle: states have the right to control their borders. This is a foundational element of the Westphalian system of sovereign states — the right to determine who may enter your territory is one of the core attributes of sovereignty, and no general international treaty overrides it. But James Hollifield's influential 2004 analysis identified what he called the 'liberal paradox': wealthy liberal democracies need open economies to function. They need migrant labor, capital mobility, and trade. But they also face intense political pressure for immigration restriction. And critically, they have inscribed rights-based constraints into their own constitutions, courts, and treaty obligations that limit how far they can actually go in restricting entry. Courts strike down deportation orders. International obligations require asylum processes. Anti-discrimination law constrains enforcement. Hollifield's point is that liberal democracies are, in a structural sense, unable to fully control immigration even when they want to — not because borders are open, but because their own legal commitments constrain the tools available. This is why every major receiving country has a politics-policy gap: politicians promise more control than the legal and institutional system can deliver.

Definition149 states are party to the 1951 Refugee Convention or 1967 Protocol. Non-refoulement is considered jus cogens — binding on all states regardless of ratification.

Full slide detail

  • The 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol form the cornerstone of international refugee law; 149 states are parties to one or both instruments, making it one of the most widely ratified human rights treaties.
  • Article 33: the principle of non-refoulement prohibits states from returning a person to a territory where they face persecution; this is generally considered a jus cogens norm — binding on all states regardless of treaty ratification — under customary international law.
  • Non-refoulement applies not only at the border but also extraterritorially wherever a state exercises effective jurisdiction or control — a contested but increasingly accepted interpretation that makes interdiction at sea and offshore processing legally problematic.
  • Article 1F exclusions: the Convention excludes from refugee protection those who have committed crimes against peace, war crimes, crimes against humanity, or serious non-political crimes outside the country of refuge — these exclusions are intended to be narrowly construed but are sometimes applied broadly.
  • The Convention's temporal limitation (originally for events before 1951) was removed by the 1967 Protocol, making it apply to all refugee situations globally; this universal application created the modern global refugee protection system.

Instructor notes

Faculty may need to explain jus cogens — the category of peremptory norms from which no derogation is permitted under any circumstances. Non-refoulement being jus cogens means even states that have not ratified the Convention are theoretically bound. In practice, enforcement mechanisms are weak. Graduate students should engage with the legal debate about extraterritoriality, which is the most contested area of contemporary refugee law. Lindsay Harris (U of San Francisco Law) and Michele Pistone (Villanova) bring expert legal grounding for these issues.

Narration transcript

The 1951 Refugee Convention is the foundation of international refugee protection law. Adopted in the aftermath of World War II, it defines who a refugee is and what protections they are entitled to. The most critical provision is Article 33: no state shall return a refugee to a country where their life or freedom would be threatened. This is the principle of non-refoulement. It has a special status in international law: it is generally considered jus cogens — a peremptory norm from which no derogation is permitted, binding even on states that have not ratified the Convention. One hundred and forty-nine states are parties to the Convention or its 1967 Protocol, which removed the original temporal and geographic limitations. But legal clarity about what non-refoulement requires in principle has not translated into consistent application in practice. The most contested contemporary question is whether non-refoulement applies extraterritorially — that is, whether a state violates the principle when it intercepts people on the high seas or in foreign territory before they ever reach its border. International human rights bodies have increasingly said yes. States have largely tried to avoid that conclusion.

Framework

Immigration Policy Tools: Visas, Quotas, Pathways

  1. Visa systems pre-entry screening through visa requirements constitutes the most powerful migration control mechanism.
  2. Admissions quotas and caps numerical limits on categories of legal entry (employment visas, family reunification, humanitarian admissions) are the …
  3. Labor migration pathways H-1B visas (US), Tier 2 skilled worker visas (UK), and seasonal worker programs create legal channels for specific …
  4. Family reunification in most OECD countries, family reunification is the single largest category of legal permanent immigration.
  5. Temporary protection status (TPS) a category used by the US and other states to grant temporary legal status to nationals of countries experiencing crisis.
Key pointVisa requirements are not applied uniformly: a Finnish passport enables visa-free access to 186 countries; an Afghan passport to about 28. Nationality determines mobility — not individual characteristics.

Full slide detail

  • Visa systems: pre-entry screening through visa requirements constitutes the most powerful migration control mechanism; wealthy states impose visa requirements on nationals of countries with high emigration pressure while maintaining visa-free access for nationals of wealthy origin states — structuring mobility by nationality.
  • Admissions quotas and caps: numerical limits on categories of legal entry (employment visas, family reunification, humanitarian admissions) are the primary domestic policy instruments; the US admits approximately 1.1 million lawful permanent residents annually; the UK and Canada use points-based systems that score applicants on human capital criteria.
  • Labor migration pathways: H-1B visas (US), Tier 2 skilled worker visas (UK), and seasonal worker programs create legal channels for specific labor needs while excluding most would-be migrants from legal pathways — creating incentives for irregular entry.
  • Family reunification: in most OECD countries, family reunification is the single largest category of legal permanent immigration; political efforts to restrict it (Canada's 'points system' reforms, UK spousal income thresholds) generate significant human rights concerns about family integrity.
  • Temporary protection status (TPS): a category used by the US and other states to grant temporary legal status to nationals of countries experiencing crisis — currently used for Venezuelans, Ukrainians, and others — with periodic renewal uncertainty creating long-term status precarity.

Instructor notes

The comparative visa index point is highly effective for illustrating structural inequality in the global mobility regime. Most students are unaware that passport power varies so dramatically by nationality. This is worth spending time on because it concretizes an otherwise abstract point about how the international system structures who can move freely and who cannot. The points-based system debate is a good comparative policy discussion, especially contrasting UK, Canadian, and Australian approaches.

Narration transcript

States use a range of tools to control who enters legally. The most powerful is the visa system — a pre-entry screening mechanism that requires travelers to obtain permission before boarding a flight or crossing a border. Visa requirements are not applied equally: wealthy states grant visa-free access to nationals of other wealthy states while requiring visas from nationals of countries with high emigration pressure. This means that your passport — your nationality — largely determines your mobility in the modern world. A Finnish passport provides visa-free access to 186 countries. An Afghan passport provides it to about 28. This passport privilege is not based on individual characteristics. It is based on where you were born. Beyond visas, states use numerical quotas, points-based admissions systems, and categorical pathways — for skilled workers, family members, and humanitarian cases — to manage legal entry. These systems shape incentives powerfully: when legal pathways are narrow or inaccessible, irregular entry becomes more likely. Understanding the architecture of legal admissions is essential for understanding why so much migration is characterized as irregular.

Case study

Comparing Immigration Systems: US, Canada, EU, Australia

  1. United States a mixed system combining family-based (approximately 65 percent of lawful permanent residence), employment-based …
  2. Canada a points-based Express Entry system scores applicants on human capital (education, language, work experience, age).
  3. Australia a similar points-based system for skilled migration combined with a humanitarian program.
  4. European Union no single EU immigration system.
  5. Comparative finding no system has resolved the policy paradoxes Hollifield identified.
Key pointThe Dublin Regulation assigned asylum responsibility to the EU country of first arrival — generating structural overload in Greece and Italy, and incentivizing onward unauthorized movement by asylum seekers to reach preferred destinations.

Full slide detail

  • United States: a mixed system combining family-based (approximately 65 percent of lawful permanent residence), employment-based (approximately 13 percent), and humanitarian (approximately 13 percent) admissions; the diversity visa lottery adds approximately 50,000 annually; the undocumented population is estimated at 10-12 million.
  • Canada: a points-based Express Entry system scores applicants on human capital (education, language, work experience, age); family class and humanitarian categories supplement economic immigration; Canada's federal-provincial dynamics allow regional immigration programs addressing local labor shortages.
  • Australia: a similar points-based system for skilled migration combined with a humanitarian program; offshore detention of unauthorized maritime arrivals at Nauru and Manus Island (Papua New Guinea) has been condemned by UN human rights bodies but has continued under successive governments.
  • European Union: no single EU immigration system; Schengen creates internal free movement among 27 member states; the Dublin Regulation (now under reform) assigned asylum responsibility to the first state of arrival — generating disproportionate burden on frontline states like Greece and Italy and producing systematic asylum seeker suffering.
  • Comparative finding: no system has resolved the policy paradoxes Hollifield identified; all face irregular migration flows, asylum backlog pressures, and political polarization; the tension between labor market needs and public opinion on restriction is universal.

Instructor notes

This comparative slide works well as a structured discussion: what is each system trying to achieve, and what trade-offs does it make? Students often find the Australia offshore detention model particularly challenging to discuss because it involves explicit cruelty as deterrence. This is worth addressing directly: deterrence-based models raise fundamental questions about whether states may treat asylum seekers cruelly to deter others from seeking safety. Graduate students can engage with the legal critique of deterrence models through the lens of Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen. Expert Lorena Avila (Villanova) and Sarah Diaz (Loyola Chicago Law) bring important comparative legal expertise.

Narration transcript

Looking at immigration systems comparatively reveals both diversity in design and convergence in the problems they face. The United States operates a complex mixed system that prioritizes family reunification, with employment-based and humanitarian categories adding additional streams. Canada and Australia have moved toward points-based systems that explicitly prioritize human capital attributes — education, language skills, work experience. The European Union is a unique case: the Schengen Agreement creates a borderless internal zone among 27 member states, but external migration governance remains fragmented among national systems. The Dublin Regulation attempted to rationalize asylum responsibility by assigning it to the first country of arrival — but this created structural overload in Greece and Italy, which receive most irregular arrivals, while northern European countries received most of the applications from asylum seekers who moved onward without authorization. The result was a system that satisfied no one and produced documented harm to the people it was supposed to process. Every one of these systems faces the same fundamental challenge: labor markets want more workers than voters want to admit, and the legal and ethical obligations of refugee protection impose constraints that political majorities sometimes reject.

Detention and Deportation: Rights and Realities

  1. Immigration detention administrative — not criminal — detention used to manage people in immigration proceedings.
  2. Indefinite detention where removal is not feasible (stateless persons, countries refusing to accept deportees), detainees can be held …
  3. Documented harms medical neglect, sexual abuse, solitary confinement, and deaths in custody have been documented in immigration …
  4. Deportation effectiveness gap EU member states issued approximately 420,000 removal orders in 2022 but effectively returned only about 20 percent of …
  5. Family separation the US 'zero tolerance' policy of 2018 separated approximately 5,500 children from parents at the border.
Key pointEU: 420,000 removal orders in 2022, only 20 percent effectively executed. The majority of people under deportation orders remain in the country in precarious limbo — the enforcement gap creates suffering without achieving its stated purpose.

Full slide detail

  • Immigration detention: administrative — not criminal — detention used to manage people in immigration proceedings; the US held an average of approximately 40,000 immigration detainees per day in 2023; EU states collectively detained hundreds of thousands annually.
  • Indefinite detention: where removal is not feasible (stateless persons, countries refusing to accept deportees), detainees can be held indefinitely in administrative limbo; UN human rights bodies have consistently found prolonged administrative detention without judicial review violates the right to liberty.
  • Documented harms: medical neglect, sexual abuse, solitary confinement, and deaths in custody have been documented in immigration detention facilities in the US, UK, Australia, and EU member states; independent monitoring access is often restricted.
  • Deportation effectiveness gap: EU member states issued approximately 420,000 removal orders in 2022 but effectively returned only about 20 percent of those ordered deported — meaning 80 percent of removal orders are not executed, creating large populations with precarious status and no resolution.
  • Family separation: the US 'zero tolerance' policy of 2018 separated approximately 5,500 children from parents at the border; pediatric, psychiatric, and human rights bodies documented severe psychological harm including developmental regression and long-term trauma.

Instructor notes

The deportation effectiveness gap is one of the most politically underappreciated facts in migration policy. Public debate tends to assume that removal orders translate into removals. The data shows they mostly do not — because destination countries refuse to accept deportees, because courts intervene, because people cannot be located, or because administrative capacity is insufficient. Students should understand that 'tough' rhetoric about deportation frequently does not match operational reality, and that the gap between rhetoric and reality creates the large population of people living in legal limbo.

Narration transcript

Immigration detention and deportation are the bluntest instruments of state migration control — the point at which sovereign authority becomes physical coercion. The United States operated one of the world's largest immigration detention systems in 2023, holding roughly 40,000 people on any given day in facilities ranging from purpose-built federal centers to county jails contracted by ICE. These are not criminal detainees — they are administrative detainees held while their immigration cases are resolved. But the conditions in many facilities have been documented by oversight bodies, journalists, and human rights organizations as involving medical neglect, sexual abuse, and in some cases death. Deportation — the actual removal of people — is legally authorized but operationally complex. EU member states issued some 420,000 removal orders in 2022 but effectively returned only about 20 percent of those people. Countries of origin sometimes refuse to accept deportees. Courts issue last-minute stays. People cannot be located. Administrative capacity is limited. The result is that the vast majority of people under removal orders remain in the country in a state of legal precarity — not formally authorized to stay, not actually removed. This limbo has documented social, economic, and mental health consequences.

Debate

Non-Refoulement Under Pressure: Cooperative Deterrence

  1. Hathaway and Gammeltoft-HansenHathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen, 2015 analyze 'cooperative deterrence' — bilateral and multilateral arrangements in which destination states pay or pressure …
  2. The evasion logic if the destination state funds the transit state to commit the interception, and the transit state violates …
  3. PijnenburgPijnenburg, 2020 the 'containment instead of refoulement' concept shows that transit state proxies shift but do not eliminate state …
  4. The Italy-Libya Memorandum of Understanding2017, renewed 2023 Italy funds the Libyan Coast Guard to intercept migrants in the Mediterranean and return them to Libya.
  5. Broader EU externalizationStatement, 2016 the EU-Turkey Statement and EU-Tunisia Memorandum (2023) follow similar logic.
Key pointHathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen (2015): cooperative deterrence allows states to violate non-refoulement indirectly — the legal fiction of distance must not be permitted to shield states from responsibility for foreseeable harms they fund and design.

Full slide detail

  • Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen (2015): analyze 'cooperative deterrence' — bilateral and multilateral arrangements in which destination states pay or pressure transit states to intercept and contain migrants before they reach the destination's territory, diffusing legal responsibility.
  • The evasion logic: if the destination state funds the transit state to commit the interception, and the transit state violates non-refoulement, the destination state claims no direct responsibility; Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen argue this legal fiction must not be permitted — complicity liability attaches.
  • Pijnenburg (2020): the 'containment instead of refoulement' concept shows that transit state proxies shift but do not eliminate state responsibility; destination states remain legally implicated under the law of state responsibility for internationally wrongful acts.
  • The Italy-Libya Memorandum of Understanding (2017, renewed 2023): Italy funds the Libyan Coast Guard to intercept migrants in the Mediterranean and return them to Libya; systematic torture, enslavement, and extrajudicial killing in Libyan detention facilities are extensively documented — making Italy's role potentially complicit in non-refoulement violations.
  • Broader EU externalization: the EU-Turkey Statement (2016) and EU-Tunisia Memorandum (2023) follow similar logic — paying transit states to prevent departures in exchange for development aid, visa liberalization, or other concessions.

Instructor notes

This is the most intellectually demanding slide in the module — it requires students to track the structure of indirect legal responsibility. A clarifying analogy: if I pay someone to commit an act I am prohibited from committing myself, most legal systems hold me liable as an accessory. Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen argue the same logic should apply to states. This debate is live in European human rights courts right now, and the legal outcome will shape the future of border externalization globally. Graduate students should engage with the primary texts.

Narration transcript

The most consequential legal debate in contemporary migration governance concerns what Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen call 'cooperative deterrence' — the proliferation of bilateral arrangements through which wealthy destination states pay transit states to intercept migrants before they can reach the destination's territory. The Italy-Libya arrangement is the clearest example: Italy funds the Libyan Coast Guard to intercept boats in the Mediterranean and return their passengers to Libya — where torture, enslavement, and extrajudicial killing in Libyan detention facilities are extensively documented by United Nations investigators and human rights organizations. Italy's legal position is that it bears no responsibility for what happens in Libya after the interception. Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen argue this is a legal fiction. If a state designs, funds, and directs an interception scheme with foreseeable lethal consequences, it cannot escape legal responsibility simply because the physical act is performed by a proxy. Pijnenburg's 2020 analysis develops this argument further, showing that the law of state responsibility for internationally wrongful acts can and should reach states that facilitate non-refoulement violations by transit state partners. These legal arguments are now being tested in European human rights courts.

Case study

Border Externalization: The EU Trust Fund for Africa

  1. The EU Emergency Trust Fund for AfricaEUTF, 2015–2023 a 4.9 billion euro fund established after the 2015 European migration crisis to address 'root causes' of migration and …
  2. PacciardiPacciardi, 2024 argues the EUTF is not a development instrument but a 'narrative device'
  3. The Khartoum Process within the EUTF framework, the EU cooperated with Sudan's Omar al-Bashir government on border management.
  4. Effectiveness evidence that the EUTF reduced irregular arrivals to Europe is methodologically contested.
  5. Normative implications using development aid as a migration-control instrument blurs the humanitarian-security boundary.
Key pointThe EU Trust Fund for Africa cooperated with the Bashir government — indicted by the ICC for genocide — as a migration-control partner. Migration-security imperatives overrode human rights conditionality.

Full slide detail

  • The EU Emergency Trust Fund for Africa (EUTF, 2015–2023): a 4.9 billion euro fund established after the 2015 European migration crisis to address 'root causes' of migration and slow irregular arrivals from Africa, combining development assistance, governance support, and border management aid.
  • Pacciardi (2024): argues the EUTF is not a development instrument but a 'narrative device' — reframing migration containment as development cooperation and extending EU border control into African territory under the cover of humanitarian language.
  • The Khartoum Process: within the EUTF framework, the EU cooperated with Sudan's Omar al-Bashir government on border management — providing capacity-building and equipment — despite the ICC having issued an arrest warrant for Bashir for crimes against humanity and genocide in Darfur.
  • Effectiveness: evidence that the EUTF reduced irregular arrivals to Europe is methodologically contested; route shifts, Libyan political dynamics, and seasonal factors complicate causal attribution; the core African migration systems it targeted (Sahel, Horn of Africa) have not fundamentally changed.
  • Normative implications: using development aid as a migration-control instrument blurs the humanitarian-security boundary; it transforms African governments into border guards for Europe, raises conditionality questions, and may undermine longer-term development relationships.

Instructor notes

The Khartoum Process dimension of the EUTF is one of the most striking real-world illustrations of how migration-control imperatives can override other policy values. Students should be asked: what does it tell us about the EU's stated commitment to human rights that it cooperated with a genocide-indicted government because that government controlled a migration corridor? The answer is not simple — it illustrates the genuine trade-offs in foreign policy — but it should not be glossed over.

Narration transcript

The EU Emergency Trust Fund for Africa, established in 2015 with approximately 4.9 billion euros, is one of the most consequential and contested migration policy instruments of the past decade. On paper, it funds development projects in African countries to address the root causes of migration. In practice, as Agnese Pacciardi argues in her 2024 analysis, the EUTF is primarily a border externalization instrument — using the language and legal architecture of development cooperation to extend EU migration control into African territory. The most striking illustration is the Khartoum Process: within the EUTF framework, the EU cooperated directly with the Sudanese government of Omar al-Bashir on border management, providing training, equipment, and institutional capacity-building to Sudanese border forces — at the exact same time that the International Criminal Court had issued an outstanding arrest warrant against Bashir for crimes against humanity and genocide in Darfur. Migration control literally overrode human rights conditionality. The EU judged that Bashir's government controlled a key migration corridor, and that controlling that corridor was more urgent than maintaining human rights standards in external relations. This is a case study that illustrates, with uncomfortable clarity, the hierarchy of priorities in EU external migration governance.

Concept

Domestic vs. International Law: Rights Conflicts in Practice

  1. Constitutional constraints many liberal democracies have constitutional provisions protecting due process, equal protection, and liberty interests …
  2. Dualist vs. monist systems: in dualist legal systems (US, UK), international treaty obligations do not automatically become domestic law.
  3. The European Court of Human Rights the ECHR has been a major arena for challenging EU member state migration practices.
  4. Non-refoulement in domestic litigationByrne, 2020 Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne show how the crisis in EU asylum law is partly a crisis of legal scholarship.
  5. The UK Rwanda policy the UK Supreme Court ruled in November 2023 that Rwanda was not a 'safe third country' for asylum seekers, blocking the …
Key pointUK Supreme Court, November 2023: Rwanda is not a 'safe third country.' The court found real risk of refoulement — blocking the government's flagship deterrence policy and forcing a constitutional confrontation between Parliament and the courts.

Full slide detail

  • Constitutional constraints: many liberal democracies have constitutional provisions protecting due process, equal protection, and liberty interests that apply to non-citizens on their territory; courts have repeatedly struck down executive immigration actions on constitutional grounds (US Supreme Court cases on DACA, travel ban; UK Supreme Court on Rwanda deportation policy).
  • Dualist vs. monist systems: in dualist legal systems (US, UK), international treaty obligations do not automatically become domestic law — Parliament or Congress must incorporate them; in monist systems (many EU member states), treaty obligations are directly applicable in domestic courts.
  • The European Court of Human Rights: the ECHR has been a major arena for challenging EU member state migration practices; Article 3 (prohibition of torture and inhuman or degrading treatment) has been applied to deportations to countries with inadequate protection.
  • Non-refoulement in domestic litigation: Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne (2020) show how the crisis in EU asylum law is partly a crisis of legal scholarship — the gap between international law doctrine and political-institutional reality has widened, requiring courts to fill gaps left by political actors.
  • The UK Rwanda policy: the UK Supreme Court ruled in November 2023 that Rwanda was not a 'safe third country' for asylum seekers, blocking the government's plan to deport asylum seekers there; the government subsequently attempted to legislate its way around the ruling — illustrating the friction between political will and legal constraint.

Instructor notes

The UK Rwanda case is an excellent live teaching example because it involved the full range of legal issues covered in this module: non-refoulement, safe third country concepts, the relationship between domestic and international law, and the tension between executive policy and judicial review. Graduate students engaging in legal analysis through the signature experience assignment (legal analysis: migration law case study) may find this case particularly tractable.

Narration transcript

The tension between domestic political authority and international legal obligation is not merely theoretical — it plays out in courtrooms regularly. In November 2023, the UK Supreme Court ruled that Rwanda was not a safe third country for asylum seekers, blocking the government's flagship policy of deporting asylum seekers to Rwanda for processing. The court found there was a real risk that people deported to Rwanda would be returned from Rwanda to countries where they faced persecution — a non-refoulement violation one step removed. The UK government's response was to attempt to pass legislation declaring Rwanda safe as a matter of law, regardless of the factual evidence — a direct confrontation with the principle of judicial review. This episode illustrates Hollifield's liberal paradox in action: a government with a democratic mandate to restrict asylum faced constitutional and international legal constraints on how it could do so. The broader lesson is that in liberal democracies, immigration policy is made not just by parliaments and executives but also by courts interpreting constitutional provisions and international obligations — and the tensions between these institutions are structural, not accidental.

Instructor notes

The data on global border walls is striking and worth dwelling on: the Berlin Wall fell in 1989 and was celebrated as the end of walls — yet in the subsequent three decades the world built far more walls than the Cold War era ever contained. This is a powerful inversion of the expected narrative and invites discussion of why. The cost data — tens of billions annually — is also worth contextualizing against humanitarian aid budgets, which are routinely under-resourced.

Narration transcript

The scale of investment in border enforcement globally is staggering. Researchers at the Transnational Institute have documented that the total length of border walls and fences worldwide grew from approximately 1,000 kilometers in 1989 — the year the Berlin Wall fell — to over 63,000 kilometers by 2022. That is a sixty-fold increase over three decades. The United States alone spent approximately 25 billion dollars on border enforcement in fiscal year 2023, recording 3.2 million encounters at the southern border — the highest in recorded history. Despite that investment, the undocumented population in the United States has remained relatively stable at an estimated 10 to 12 million people, according to Pew Research Center analysis. The EU's border agency Frontex had a budget of approximately 845 million euros in 2023, a figure that has grown dramatically over the decade. Meanwhile, UNHCR routinely operates at massive funding shortfalls — facing a 52 percent gap between needs and available funds in 2024. The juxtaposition of escalating border enforcement budgets against chronically under-resourced humanitarian response is a structural feature of the current global migration governance order.

Full slide detail

  • What is 'effectiveness' in migration policy — and who defines it? Reducing irregular arrivals? Protecting human rights? Meeting labor market needs? Maintaining public trust? These goals frequently conflict.
  • Castles (2017): migration policies systematically fail because states try to manage an inherent social transformation through bureaucratic controls — the aspiration to 'control migration' may be fundamentally misconceived.

Instructor notes

This is the generative discussion slide for the module. Faculty should allow significant time here. Students often begin from the assumption that there is a technically correct answer to migration policy — this discussion is designed to surface the underlying value conflicts. The 'design from scratch' prompt works especially well in graduate seminars. For undergraduate classes, the Castles argument that policy may be fundamentally misconceived is productively provocative and often generates pushback worth exploring.

Narration transcript

Before we close this module, I want to pose a central question for reflection. What does it even mean for migration policy to be 'effective'? Effective for whom, and by what measure? For a restrictionist government, effectiveness means reducing arrivals. For a labor economist, it means matching workers to jobs. For a refugee lawyer, it means ensuring no one is returned to persecution. For a receiving community, it might mean smooth integration with minimal social friction. These goals do not always align — and choosing between them involves value judgments that policy analysis alone cannot resolve. Stephen Castles made a provocative argument in 2017 that migration policies are structurally prone to failure — not because governments are incompetent, but because they are trying to control something that is, at its root, a social transformation process. Migration is not a flow to be turned on and off. It is a consequence of interconnected global systems — economic inequality, political instability, social networks, climate change — that states can influence at the margins but cannot fundamentally control. If that is right, it suggests that the most important question in migration governance may not be how to restrict migration more effectively, but how to manage it more humanely and with greater respect for the rights of everyone involved.

Key point2018: Two global compacts — the GCM and GCR — established the first comprehensive multilateral frameworks for migration and refugee governance. Non-binding, but normatively significant.

Full slide detail

  • The Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (GCM, 2018): the first intergovernmental agreement covering all dimensions of international migration; 164 states endorsed it; it is non-legally binding but represents the first global normative framework for migration governance.
  • The GCM's 23 objectives include: reducing irregular migration drivers, facilitating regular pathways, combating trafficking, providing access to services, improving data, and managing return and reintegration — a comprehensive agenda that reflects the full spectrum of migration governance challenges.
  • The Global Compact on Refugees (GCR, 2018): a parallel instrument on refugee protection reaffirming burden-sharing principles, expanding resettlement, and committing to support host countries — but also non-binding and dependent on state voluntary action.
  • Limits: the non-binding nature of both Compacts reflects the political limits of multilateral migration governance; several states (US, Hungary, Australia) refused to sign or withdrew; implementation depends entirely on national political will with no enforcement mechanism.
  • Significance: despite their limitations, the Compacts represent an important discursive shift — establishing shared language and normative expectations for migration governance that advocates and civil society organizations can use to hold states accountable.

Instructor notes

The Global Compacts are important to teach because they represent both the high-water mark of multilateral migration governance ambition and its limitations. The fact that they are non-binding reflects the political reality that states are not willing to cede sovereignty over migration decisions to international institutions. Graduate students can evaluate whether non-binding commitments have any practical value — and the evidence on whether the GCM has changed state behavior is mixed but worth examining.

Narration transcript

In 2018, the international community adopted two landmark agreements on migration governance. The Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration — the GCM — was the first intergovernmental agreement to address all aspects of international migration in a single document, endorsed by 164 states. The Global Compact on Refugees reaffirmed international burden-sharing principles and expanded commitments on resettlement. Both are important milestones in the development of global migration governance. Both are also, critically, non-binding. They represent political commitments, not legal obligations. Their implementation depends entirely on the voluntary decisions of individual states. Several major states, including the United States under the Trump administration, refused to endorse them. Australia and Hungary also declined. This is the fundamental challenge of global migration governance: unlike trade law or even international criminal law, there is no international institution with enforcement authority over migration decisions. The Compacts create shared language, normative expectations, and accountability mechanisms, but they cannot compel state action. This is where the module's core tension — between sovereignty and international obligation — remains unresolved.

Full slide detail

  • Hollifield's migration state: as states have built more elaborate immigration bureaucracies, they have simultaneously accumulated more international legal obligations and domestic constitutional commitments that constrain what those bureaucracies can actually do — a paradox of governance.
  • Castles (2017): migration policies are structurally prone to failure because states attempt to regulate what is fundamentally a social transformation process; policy can shift migration flows temporarily but cannot sustainably suppress migration driven by deep structural inequalities.
  • The politics-policy gap: in virtually every major receiving country, enforcement rhetoric exceeds what operational, legal, and constitutional constraints allow; the gap between promised and delivered control generates public disillusionment that fuels political polarization.
  • Irregular migration as a policy artifact: much irregularity is created by policy — visa regimes that offer no legal channel for needed workers produce irregular workers; border walls that block legal crossings produce clandestine crossings; policies that criminalize asylum seeking generate more unauthorized border crossings as people try to evade processing.
  • Toward governance: the shift from thinking about 'migration control' to 'migration governance' reflects an emerging scholarly and policy consensus that sustainable management requires orderly legal pathways, development investment in origin regions, international burden-sharing, and rights-consistent enforcement — not simply more restriction.

Instructor notes

This slide is best positioned immediately before the GCM slide in the deck — it provides the critical framing that explains why the multilateral governance approach of the Global Compacts represents an attempt to transcend the limits of unilateral state control. The shift from 'control' to 'governance' language is itself analytically significant and worth dwelling on with graduate students.

Narration transcript

James Hollifield's observation about the migration state points toward a deeper paradox in migration governance. The more elaborate the enforcement apparatus a state builds — the more visa regulations, border walls, detention facilities, deportation programs — the more it also builds up legal obligations, court challenges, and bureaucratic complexity that constrains what it can actually do. And Stephen Castles made the uncomfortable argument that the fundamental aspiration of migration control — treating migration as a flow to be turned on and off by government decree — may be misconceived. Migration is not a flow. It is a social process, driven by deep structural forces: global inequality, demographic imbalances, climate pressures, social networks, and the aspirations of millions of individual human beings. These forces do not respond simply to enforcement pressure. They respond by finding new routes, new methods, new legal categories. Much of what we call 'irregular' migration is, in an important sense, a policy artifact — created by the absence of legal pathways for needed workers, by visa regimes that lock out people who have compelling reasons to move, by enforcement measures that push migration into dangerous terrain. The emerging scholarly and policy consensus, reflected in the 2018 Global Compacts, is that governing migration sustainably requires orderly legal pathways, international burden-sharing, development investment in origin regions, and rights-consistent enforcement — not simply escalating restriction. Whether states have the political will to implement that consensus is a different question.

Full slide detail

  • States have broad sovereign authority to control entry, but this authority is constrained by international human rights obligations, domestic constitutional law, and the inherent social dynamics of migration that policy can influence but not fully control.
  • Non-refoulement — the prohibition on returning people to persecution — is the cornerstone of international refugee protection; it is increasingly tested by cooperative deterrence arrangements, offshore processing, and border externalization strategies.
  • No major receiving country has 'solved' migration governance; all face versions of Hollifield's liberal paradox — the structural tension between open economies, closed politics, and rights-based legal constraints.
  • Enforcement-heavy approaches (detention, deportation, border walls) generate large public expenditures and documented human rights harms; their effectiveness in achieving policy goals is contested in the scholarly literature.
  • The 2018 Global Compacts represent the current horizon of multilateral migration governance ambition — comprehensive in scope, significant in normative impact, but non-binding and unevenly implemented.

Instructor notes

Close the module by returning to the central tension introduced at the beginning: sovereignty versus international obligation. Students should leave with a clear analytical vocabulary for thinking about migration policy — tools (visas, detention, externalization), constraints (non-refoulement, domestic courts, international law), and the structural paradoxes (liberal paradox, Castles' social transformation argument). Narration transcript

Let us close by drawing together the central threads of this module. States have sovereign authority over their borders — that is the foundational principle of the international system. But sovereignty is not unlimited. International human rights law, embodied most powerfully in the 1951 Refugee Convention's principle of non-refoulement, places real constraints on how that authority may be exercised. Domestic courts in liberal democracies add another layer of constraint. The result is a permanent tension that no state has fully resolved. Enforcement-heavy approaches — walls, detention, deportation, externalization — can shift migration patterns but cannot eliminate migration pressure, and they generate documented human rights harms and massive expenditures. The Global Compacts of 2018 established a normative framework for better governance but left implementation to voluntary state action. The fundamental insight from this module is that migration governance is not primarily a technical problem awaiting a technocratic solution. It is a site of genuine value conflict — between sovereignty and human rights, between economic interests and political sentiment, between the rights of citizens and the rights of migrants — and governing it well requires honest engagement with those conflicts rather than promises of control that the evidence suggests cannot be delivered.
