Migrant Lives and Identities

Who are migrants — and how does migration remake who they are?

AnalyticalReflective

Statistics updated June 2026 (UNHCR Global Trends 2026, IOM World Migration Report 2026). Audio narration may cite the earlier end-2024 figures.

Narration transcript

Migration does not leave identity intact. The act of moving — crossing borders, languages, cultures, and legal categories — reshapes who people are and how they are seen. This module examines how migrants navigate identity, belonging, and adaptation; how race, gender, disability, and legal status compound one another; and why the categories we use — refugee, migrant, citizen — often fail to capture the complexity of real lives. We draw on transnationalism theory and intersectional analysis to move beyond both the assimilation model and the victimhood framework.

Key pointMethodological nationalism (Wimmer & Glick Schiller, 2002): when researchers automatically use the nation-state as the unit of analysis, they cannot see the transnational social fields migrants actually inhabit.

Full slide detail

  • Migration raises fundamental questions about who belongs, who counts, and on whose terms: host society assimilation demands, origin society expectations of loyalty, and migrants' own sense of self often pull in different directions.
  • Classical assimilation theory (Park, Gordon, 1960s): migration involves a linear, one-directional process of incorporation — immigrants become 'American' (or German, or French) over time, shedding origin culture.
  • Segmented assimilation (Portes and Zhou, 1993): immigrants do not assimilate uniformly upward; outcomes vary by race, human capital, and context of reception — some integrate into professional middle class, others into an underclass, others into ethnic enclaves.
  • Transnationalism (Glick Schiller et al., 1992): migrants do not simply leave one society and join another — they build and maintain social fields that simultaneously span origin and destination countries, creating 'transmigrant' identities.
  • The challenge for analysis: Wimmer and Glick Schiller (2002) argue that 'methodological nationalism' — treating the nation-state as the natural container for social life — has systematically distorted migration theory by missing these transnational social fields.

Instructor notes

Start with the assimilation debate because most students already have some intuitive assimilation model in their heads. The segmented assimilation critique is important for showing how race shapes integration outcomes. Wimmer and Glick Schiller's methodological nationalism critique is one of the most influential arguments in contemporary migration studies — spend time on it. Ask: how does the framing of 'integration' presuppose that migrants should become more like the host society? What assumptions does that carry?

Narration transcript

Migration is, among other things, an identity event. It disrupts the taken-for-granted social world — the language, the cultural cues, the social relationships, the legal status — that forms the background of everyday identity. How scholars have conceptualized what happens to migrants' identities over time has changed dramatically. The classical assimilation framework, developed by Robert Park and elaborated by Milton Gordon in the 1960s, imagined integration as a linear, one-directional process: immigrants gradually shed origin culture and take on the culture of the host society. This framework dominated US immigration scholarship for decades, but it was challenged on both empirical and normative grounds.

Key pointHomeland ties do not disappear with migration — digital communication has made transnational community maintenance qualitatively different for contemporary migrants than for earlier waves.

Full slide detail

  • Transnational communities maintain simultaneous involvement in origin and destination societies: regular communication, financial transfers, participation in hometown associations, dual citizenship, visits, and maintenance of origin-country social ties.
  • Hometown associations (HTAs): migrant organizations built around shared origin community; send collective remittances for infrastructure projects; influence origin-country politics; provide social support in destination — active in US Latino communities, West African diaspora, Philippines abroad.
  • Second-generation identity: children of migrants navigate hyphenated identities — 'Korean-American,' 'British-Pakistani' — often pressured to perform ethnic authenticity for the ethnic community and civic integration for the host society simultaneously.
  • Glick Schiller's 'long-distance nationalism': migrants participate in origin-country politics — donating to political parties, voting (where permitted), pressuring diaspora lobbying — sometimes more intensely than those remaining in the origin country.
  • Digital technology has intensified transnationalism: WhatsApp groups, Facebook communities, video calls, and social media allow daily connection across borders that was impossible for earlier waves of migrants — changing the emotional and social texture of transnational life.

Instructor notes

The hometown association concept is a powerful concrete example of organized transnationalism that most students have not encountered. Use it to make transnationalism tangible. The second-generation identity pressures are often personally resonant for students with immigrant backgrounds — create space for that while maintaining analytical distance. The long-distance nationalism point is interesting for political science students and connects to debates about diaspora political participation.

Narration transcript

Transnational communities are not merely abstract theoretical constructs — they are organizations, communication networks, financial flows, and lived social relationships. Hometown associations provide one of the clearest concrete examples: organizations formed by migrants from a specific origin community that maintain strong ties to that hometown, sending collective remittances for infrastructure projects like schools, water systems, or churches, organizing festivals, and lobbying both origin and destination governments on issues that affect their home community. These associations are active across the US Latino diaspora, West African diaspora communities in Europe and North America, and Filipino communities globally. For children of migrants — the so-called second generation — transnational identity takes a different form.

DefinitionTransnationalism vs. assimilation: these are not competing empirical predictions — they describe different aspects of the same lives. Most migrants simultaneously maintain origin ties AND adapt to host societies.

Full slide detail

  • Transnationalism: the sustained, simultaneous participation of migrants in social, economic, political, and cultural life across two or more nation-states — distinct from simple immigration in that ties to origin are actively maintained, not severed.
  • Assimilation: the process by which immigrants adopt the language, norms, values, and cultural practices of the host society over time — ranges from straight-line assimilation (Park/Gordon) to segmented assimilation (Portes/Zhou) depending on structural conditions.
  • Diaspora: a dispersed ethnic or national population maintaining strong collective identity, ties to a real or imagined homeland, and often a sense of displacement or exile — may or may not be transnational in the active sense.
  • Intersectionality (Crenshaw, 1989): the analytical framework recognizing that systems of oppression — race, gender, class, sexuality, legal status — are not additive but mutually constituting, producing qualitatively distinct experiences at their intersections.
  • Methodological nationalism (Wimmer and Glick Schiller, 2002): the tendency to take the nation-state as the natural unit of analysis, making transnational social fields and global processes invisible — a bias the course works against throughout.

Instructor notes

This definitional slide is important for students who come from different disciplinary traditions with different default vocabularies. Sociology, political science, law, and anthropology use overlapping but distinct terminologies for migration phenomena. Establish shared vocabulary before building the analytical discussion. The distinction between diaspora (collective identity) and transnationalism (active cross-border practices) is commonly conflated.

Narration transcript

Before we can analyze migrant identities rigorously, we need a shared vocabulary. Transnationalism, as defined by Nina Glick Schiller and colleagues in the early 1990s, refers to the sustained, simultaneous participation of migrants in the social, economic, political, and cultural life of two or more nation-states. The key word is 'simultaneous' — transnationalism is not about visiting one's country of origin on holiday, but about actively maintaining membership in multiple social fields at once. Assimilation, the older paradigm, describes the process by which immigrants adopt the language, norms, and cultural practices of the host society over time. Robert Park's 'race relations cycle' and Milton Gordon's seven-stage assimilation model treated this as a linear, inevitable process; Portes and Zhou's segmented assimilation framework showed that the process is neither uniform nor guaranteed to produce upward mobility.

Full slide detail

  • Crawley and Skleparis (2018, Journal of Ethnic and Migration Studies) interviewed 215 people who crossed the Mediterranean and found that nearly all had mixed motivations combining economic, conflict, and family dimensions — the refugee/migrant binary failed to describe any of them accurately.
  • The label 'refugee' confers legal protection but is also politically deployed to create hierarchies of worthiness: who counts as a 'genuine' refugee vs. an 'economic migrant' reflects political judgment, not purely legal assessment.
  • Stateless persons occupy the most precarious legal category: they are recognized by no state as citizens, making them invisible to the international refugee system unless they can demonstrate individual persecution — approximately 4.5 million stateless persons identified globally (UNHCR, 2024 estimate).
  • Cassadee Yan (2025) demonstrates how patriarchal nationality laws, which transmit citizenship through the father, have been a primary mechanism of statelessness production for women and children — an intersection of gender and legal status.
  • The political stakes of labeling: in Europe's 2015-2016 'migration crisis,' media and politicians' deployment of 'migrant' vs. 'refugee' vocabulary directly shaped public opinion, border policy, and reception conditions.

Instructor notes

This is one of the most important conceptual debates in the course. The refugee/migrant distinction is politically loaded, legally consequential, and empirically inadequate. Crawley and Skleparis's phrase 'categorical fetishism' is useful and memorable. Connect to the 'Refugees, Asylum, and Forced Displacement' module on refugee law — here we see the human cost of that classification system. Yan's 2025 work on gendered statelessness production is cutting-edge and shows how legal architecture produces dispossession.

Narration transcript

How we categorize people who move is never purely descriptive — it is politically consequential. The distinction between 'refugee' and 'migrant' determines access to asylum procedures, protection from refoulement, resettlement opportunities, and social services. Yet research increasingly shows that this binary fails to describe the people it is applied to. Heaven Crawley and Dimitris Skleparis conducted 215 interviews with people who had crossed the Mediterranean in 2015 and 2016 and found that virtually everyone had mixed motivations: they were fleeing violence but also seeking economic opportunities; they had family networks pulling them toward specific destinations; they had survived environmental disasters that combined with political insecurity. No single category captured their situation. Crawley and Skleparis call this 'categorical fetishism' — treating administratively convenient categories as if they were natural, stable kinds that cleanly divide the world.

Full slide detail

  • International migrants number approximately 281 million globally, representing about 3.6 percent of world population — but their distribution is highly concentrated, with 68 percent residing in just 20 countries (UN DESA, 2020).
  • Second-generation populations are large and growing: in the United States, approximately 17 percent of adults report having at least one immigrant parent; in France and Germany, second-generation populations represent 10-15 percent of the working-age population.
  • Stateless persons: UNHCR estimates approximately 4.5 million stateless persons identified globally as of 2023 — with the true figure likely higher, as statelessness is severely undercounted. The Rohingya (at least 600,000 in Bangladesh) are the largest single stateless population.

  • Undercounted populations: children, disabled migrants, undocumented migrants, and LGBTQ+ individuals are systematically undercounted in migration statistics — reinforcing the Migration Data and Global Trends lesson that who gets counted shapes whose needs are addressed in policy.

Instructor notes

This data slide grounds the identity analysis in quantifiable reality. The stateless population figure is particularly important — it represents perhaps the starkest form of identity without legal recognition. Connect the undercounting point to the 'Migration Data and Global Trends' module's data literacy themes: the populations that are hardest to count are often those whose exclusion is most systematic. The 281 million / 3.6 percent figure is the standard benchmark from UN DESA 2020.

Narration transcript

To ground the identity analysis in empirical scale, it is worth returning to the data. UN DESA's 2020 estimates placed the global international migrant population at approximately 281 million — about 3.6 percent of world population. But this figure is heavily concentrated: approximately 68 percent of all international migrants live in just 20 countries. Second-generation populations — children born in the host country to at least one immigrant parent — are large and in many wealthy countries represent a significant share of the working-age population. In the United States, approximately 17 percent of adults report having at least one immigrant parent. In France and Germany, second-generation populations represent 10 to 15 percent of the working-age cohort. These are not marginal populations — they are central to the economic and cultural life of host societies.

Full slide detail

  • Intersectionality (Kimberlé Crenshaw, 1989): systems of oppression — race, gender, class, legal status — do not operate independently but interact, mutually constituting one another in ways that produce qualitatively distinct experiences at their intersections.
  • Applying intersectionality to migration: an undocumented Black woman in domestic work experiences not just the sum of racism plus sexism plus immigration precarity — she experiences a distinct, compounded vulnerability that single-axis analyses miss.
  • Legal status as an intersectional axis: immigration status — documented, undocumented, asylum seeker, refugee, temporary visa — shapes access to labor rights, social services, and legal protection in ways that compound with race and gender.
  • Chițac (2023, International Journal of Entrepreneurial Behavior and Research): Romanian women entrepreneurs in Western Europe negotiate shifting gender, national, and ethnic identities that produce distinct entrepreneurial identities — intersectionality is not only about disadvantage but shapes forms of agency.
  • Yan (2025): stateless women face the intersection of patriarchal nationality law, migration control, and economic subordination — a triple bind that produces profound, compounded legal and social exclusion.

Instructor notes

Intersectionality is a concept many students have encountered but not always applied rigorously to migration contexts. Be explicit that intersectionality means the axes produce qualitatively distinct experiences, not just cumulative disadvantage. Chițac's study of Romanian women entrepreneurs is valuable for showing intersectionality producing complex agency, not just victimhood. Yan on stateless women is the cutting-edge legal application.

Narration transcript

Kimberlé Crenshaw's concept of intersectionality, developed in the context of Black women's experiences in the United States, has become one of the most powerful analytical tools in migration studies. Intersectionality argues that race, gender, class, and other axes of social differentiation are not independent variables that add together — they interact, mutually constitute one another, and produce qualitatively distinct experiences at their intersections that neither single-axis analysis can capture. Applied to migration, intersectionality reveals how an undocumented Black woman working in domestic service in a European city does not experience simply the sum of racism, plus sexism, plus immigration precarity.

Full slide detail

  • Approximately one-third of all forcibly displaced people are children under 18 (UNHCR, 2024). Millions are unaccompanied or separated from their families — highly vulnerable to trafficking, exploitation, and detention.
  • Unaccompanied migrant children (UMC): under international law (UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, 1989), all children have rights to protection, family unity, and asylum proceedings that consider the best interest of the child — regardless of immigration status.
  • Child detention: children in immigration detention suffer documented harms including developmental delay, trauma, and mental health damage; the UNHCR, UNICEF, and Committee on the Rights of the Child have called for ending child immigration detention globally.
  • Educational continuity: refugee and migrant children face language barriers, credential discontinuity, school violence, discrimination, and instability — yet evidence shows early educational integration is one of the strongest predictors of long-term social integration.
  • Lost generation risk: UNHCR data indicate that only approximately 68 percent of refugee children attend primary school, 40 percent secondary school, and just 6 percent access higher education — a systemic education gap with lifetime implications.

Instructor notes

Children are often treated as a footnote in migration analysis — make them central here. The 6 percent higher education figure is striking and memorable. The child detention debate is important for connecting to the 'Borders, Sovereignty, and Policy' module's human rights framework. Ask students: if a state cannot use criminal detention for minor immigration violations in adult cases (in many legal systems), what justifies child detention?

Narration transcript

Children represent one of the most vulnerable groups within global migration flows, and one of the most underanalyzed. According to UNHCR, approximately one-third of all forcibly displaced people are under 18 years of age — and among those, millions are unaccompanied or separated from their families, traveling alone or with non-family adults. International law is clear on their rights: the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, ratified by virtually every country in the world, establishes that all children have rights to protection, family unity, and asylum processes that take the best interest of the child as a primary consideration — regardless of their immigration status. But implementation falls dramatically short. Child immigration detention is one of the most contested practices in contemporary migration governance.

Instructor notes

Disability in migration is genuinely underresearched, and Duda-Mikulin's 2024 study is a rare empirical contribution. This slide challenges faculty to expand the intersectional lens beyond the gender-race-class axes that dominate migration studies. Ask: how do the legal procedures and physical infrastructure of asylum systems systematically disadvantage disabled claimants? Narration transcript

Among the many dimensions of intersectionality in migration, disability remains one of the least analyzed and most poorly documented. Eva Duda-Mikulin's 2024 qualitative study in Social Sciences, based on narratives from disabled EU migrants to the United Kingdom, offers a rare window into this intersection. Her research reveals compounding disadvantage: migrants who already face barriers in accessing services, social networks, and legal protections face additional, multiplicative challenges when disability is added to the equation. Care networks built in origin countries are disrupted by migration. Accessible services designed for native populations may be structurally inaccessible to migrants without language support or documentation. Social isolation compounds for disabled migrants in ways that are qualitatively distinct from what disabled citizens or non-disabled migrants experience.

Key pointUNHCR guidance: SOGI refugee claims require neither proof of homosexual acts nor stereotyped tests — yet such assessments persist in some adjudication systems, violating international standards.

Full slide detail

  • Sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) are recognized as grounds for refugee status under the 1951 Convention's 'membership in a particular social group' category — but recognition is inconsistent and often requires claimants to 'prove' their identity in degrading ways.
  • Approximately 70 countries criminalize same-sex relationships; in a subset of these, the penalty is death — creating conditions that compel LGBTQ+ individuals to flee and meet the refugee definition.
  • Credibility assessment in SOGI claims: European and North American asylum systems have been criticized for requiring disclosure evidence and using stereotyped tests of 'gay behavior' that violate dignity and fail legal standards.
  • LGBTQ+ asylum seekers in camps and detention face particular danger: gay men, lesbian women, bisexual and trans individuals in heteronormative, sometimes homophobic camp environments can face violence from co-residents and exclusion by authorities.
  • Intersecting vulnerabilities: LGBTQ+ migrants who are also undocumented, from racial minorities, or living with HIV face compound barriers to protection, healthcare access, and social integration.

Instructor notes

SOGI refugee claims are an important and underteaught area. Be careful to present this as a human rights and legal standards question, not a political opinion question. The credibility assessment problem is well-documented and raises serious legal and ethical issues about how asylum systems function. Faculty may encounter students who hold personal views on LGBTQ+ issues — focus the discussion on international legal standards and lived human experience.

Narration transcript

Sexual orientation and gender identity represent another critical axis of vulnerability in migration. Approximately 70 countries criminalize same-sex relationships, and in a small number, the penalty can include death. For LGBTQ+ individuals fleeing such contexts, the 1951 Refugee Convention provides a pathway to protection through its 'particular social group' clause — but accessing that protection is often deeply problematic. Asylum credibility assessments for sexual orientation and gender identity claims have been extensively criticized by UNHCR, academic researchers, and human rights organizations. Some adjudicators have demanded explicit disclosure of sexual behavior, required applicants to demonstrate their identity through stereotyped markers, or questioned the sincerity of claims based on the applicant not appearing visibly gay.

Debate

Cultural Adaptation, Belonging, and the Integration Debate

  1. Three theoretical models of incorporation assimilation (full cultural absorption into host society).
  2. The 'integration paradox' (Walhout et al.) highly educated, well-integrated second-generation migrants sometimes report greater perceived discrimination than …
  3. Belonging is not guaranteed by legal status or integration program participation legal citizenship or permanent residence does not resolve emotional, social, and racial dimensions of belonging.
  4. Cultural adaptation is bidirectional Berry's acculturation framework identifies four strategies.
  5. The host society reception context matters enormously discrimination, racism, xenophobia, and economic exclusion in the host society shape adaptation outcomes as much as …
Key pointThe integration paradox: those who integrate most successfully — gaining language, education, and civic engagement — sometimes report experiencing more discrimination, not less.

Full slide detail

  • Three theoretical models of incorporation: assimilation (full cultural absorption into host society); multiculturalism (recognition and maintenance of cultural difference within a shared civic framework); civic integration (shared civic norms without cultural assimilation demands).
  • The 'integration paradox' (Walhout et al.): highly educated, well-integrated second-generation migrants sometimes report greater perceived discrimination than less-integrated parents — rising expectations collide with persistent barriers.
  • Belonging is not guaranteed by legal status or integration program participation: legal citizenship or permanent residence does not resolve emotional, social, and racial dimensions of belonging — particularly for racialized minorities who remain visibly 'other.'
  • Cultural adaptation is bidirectional: Berry's acculturation framework identifies four strategies — assimilation, separation, integration (biculturalism), and marginalization — and finds that integration (maintaining heritage culture + engaging host culture) produces best psychological outcomes.
  • The host society reception context matters enormously: discrimination, racism, xenophobia, and economic exclusion in the host society shape adaptation outcomes as much as migrants' own characteristics and choices.

Instructor notes

The multiculturalism vs. assimilation vs. civic integration debate is politically live in many countries and connects to faculty's own institutional contexts. Berry's acculturation framework is a useful psychological model. The integration paradox is counterintuitive and provokes good discussion — why would more integration produce more perceived discrimination? Because it raises expectations and makes discrimination more personally devastating when it still occurs. The host society reception context point is critical: too often integration is framed as something migrants need to achieve, while the host society's responsibilities are downplayed.

Narration transcript

How should host societies relate to immigrant cultures? This has been one of the most politically contentious questions in Western democracies for three decades. The classical assimilation model held that immigrants should and eventually would fully absorb into the dominant culture — a process that was both inevitable and desirable. Multicultural policy frameworks, adopted in varying degrees by Canada, the Netherlands, and the UK from the 1970s through the 1990s, held instead that cultural difference should be recognized and accommodated within a shared civic framework. Civic integration models — requiring language tests, civics exams, and commitment to host-society values as conditions of residence or citizenship — attempt a middle path.

Racism, Xenophobia, and Structural Exclusion

  1. Racial discrimination in migration non-White migrants in European and North American host societies face documented discrimination in housing, labor …
  2. Racialized asylum hierarchies the Ukrainian refugee response in Europe in 2022 demonstrated clearly that White, Christian, European refugees received …
  3. Islamophobia as a migration issue Muslim migrants and refugees — from Turkey, Morocco, Syria, Somalia, Bangladesh — face distinct forms of discrimination …
  4. Institutional racism and migration policing, housing, education, and healthcare systems that were not designed with migrant populations in mind may …
  5. Zogata-Kusz et al. show how Polish and Czech media constructed Ukrainian refugees as 'deserving' through cultural and racial proximity.2023, Journal of Refugee Studies
Key pointThe Ukrainian refugee response: unprecedented speed, generosity, and political consensus for a refugee crisis — throwing into stark relief the differential treatment of non-European, non-White forced migrants.

Full slide detail

  • Racial discrimination in migration: non-White migrants in European and North American host societies face documented discrimination in housing, labor markets, education, and interactions with police and criminal justice systems — distinct from and in addition to migration-specific barriers.
  • Racialized asylum hierarchies: the Ukrainian refugee response in Europe in 2022 demonstrated clearly that White, Christian, European refugees received systematically more welcoming policy responses than Black African, Middle Eastern, or Afghan refugees facing comparable displacement — a pattern of racialized deservingness.
  • Islamophobia as a migration issue: Muslim migrants and refugees — from Turkey, Morocco, Syria, Somalia, Bangladesh — face distinct forms of discrimination linked to religious and racial othering in European and North American host societies.
  • Institutional racism and migration: policing, housing, education, and healthcare systems that were not designed with migrant populations in mind may produce racially disparate outcomes without explicit discriminatory intent.
  • Zogata-Kusz et al. (2023, Journal of Refugee Studies) show how Polish and Czech media constructed Ukrainian refugees as 'deserving' through cultural and racial proximity — illuminating by contrast how non-White refugees are constructed as 'undeserving.'

Instructor notes

This is a sensitive but essential topic. The Ukrainian refugee comparison is one of the clearest recent examples of racialized differential treatment in refugee policy — multiple EU officials, African and Middle Eastern commentators, and refugee advocates explicitly named the racial dimension. Use Zogata-Kusz et al. for the media analysis dimension. Be prepared for faculty discomfort with the racialized asylum hierarchy framing — present it as an empirical observation grounded in research, not advocacy.

Narration transcript

Racism and xenophobia are not peripheral issues in migration studies — they are structurally central. Non-White migrants in European and North American societies face documented discrimination in housing, employment, educational systems, and criminal justice that compounds the specific barriers associated with migration status. The response to the Ukrainian refugee crisis in Europe in 2022 made the racial dimensions of refugee policy unusually visible. Multiple EU governments moved with remarkable speed to offer temporary protection status, open borders, and social services to Ukrainians — responses that contrasted sharply with the policies applied to Syrian, Afghan, Somali, and Congolese refugees in preceding years. Several African and Middle Eastern governments explicitly named the disparity.

  • Venezuelan migrants in Colombia, Peru, and Chile face discrimination, labor exploitation, housing insecurity, and hostility from host communities — despite sharing the Spanish language and regional cultural proximity.

Instructor notes

The Venezuelan case is the largest displacement crisis in Latin American history and provides rich material for all dimensions of this module: identity, intersectionality, belonging, and structural exclusion. The intra-regional cultural proximity means that discrimination of Venezuelans by Colombians, Peruvians, or Chileans is not explained by cultural or linguistic difference — it is explained by economic competition, class dynamics, and in some cases racial hierarchy. The differentiation between earlier (professional) and more recent (more vulnerable) waves is important for nuanced analysis.

Narration transcript

The Venezuelan displacement crisis offers a compelling case study for the identity and belonging themes of this module. With nearly 7.9 million Venezuelans having left their country by the end of 2024, it represents the largest displacement crisis in Latin American history — and one of the most rapidly evolving. Unlike many refugee situations, the vast majority of Venezuelans have remained within the Latin American and Caribbean region, hosted primarily by Colombia, Peru, Chile, Ecuador, and Brazil. What makes the Venezuelan case particularly interesting for identity analysis is that it challenges many common assumptions. Venezuelans share the Spanish language and broad cultural reference points with most host populations — yet they still face significant discrimination, labor exploitation, and xenophobia.

Full slide detail

  • Discussion prompt 1: Wimmer and Glick Schiller argue that methodological nationalism has distorted migration research by treating nation-states as natural containers for social life. What would migration research — and migration policy — look like if it genuinely took transnational social fields as its unit of analysis?
  • Discussion prompt 2: The 'integration paradox' suggests that successful integration does not always reduce discrimination. What does this imply for integration programs and policies? Who bears responsibility for integration — migrants, host societies, or both?
  • Discussion prompt 3: Should host societies have legal and moral obligations to facilitate migrants' cultural maintenance, or are migrants obligated to assimilate? On what grounds could either position be defended?
  • Discussion prompt 4: The Ukrainian refugee response revealed racialized hierarchies in European asylum policy. Is this simply inconsistency that should be corrected — or does it reveal something structural about how European asylum policy has always worked?

Instructor notes

Prompt 1 pushes students to think about what genuinely transnational policy would look like — an intellectually difficult but important exercise. Prompt 2 on the integration paradox often surfaces personal experiences among students with immigrant backgrounds. Prompt 3 on cultural maintenance vs. assimilation is politically live but has deep philosophical dimensions. Prompt 4 on Ukrainian refugees requires students to engage the racial dimensions of European asylum policy — which many European faculty may find uncomfortable but which is empirically documented.

Narration transcript

Migration transforms identity — but questions of identity, belonging, and cultural adaptation are not only about individual migrants. They are questions about what kind of societies host countries want to be. Wimmer and Glick Schiller's challenge to methodological nationalism asks us to imagine a world in which the nation-state is not treated as the natural horizon of social life — in which belonging is not primarily national belonging, in which rights are not primarily tied to citizenship, in which social analysis begins with the relationships and communities people actually build rather than the legal categories that classify them.

Key pointCore insight to carry forward: Migrants are not just people who moved — they are people who live in multiple worlds simultaneously, and our analytical frameworks must be capable of seeing that.

Full slide detail

  • Migration is an identity event: it does not simply move people between places — it disrupts, reshapes, and multiplies the social fields and cultural frameworks through which people understand themselves.
  • Transnationalism has replaced assimilation as the dominant empirical framework: contemporary migrants build and maintain simultaneous ties to multiple societies — methodological nationalism makes these transnational social fields invisible.
  • Intersectionality is not optional: the compounding of race, gender, legal status, disability, age, and sexual orientation produces qualitatively distinct experiences that single-axis analyses systematically miss.
  • Categories like 'refugee' and 'migrant' are political artifacts, not natural kinds: their deployment shapes access to protection and creates hierarchies of worthiness that reflect power dynamics as much as legal criteria.
  • The host society bears responsibility for integration outcomes: discrimination, racism, housing exclusion, and labor market barriers are host-society features that shape migrant adaptation as powerfully as migrants' own characteristics.

Instructor notes

Close by asking students to identify one dimension of migrant identity that they had not thought about before this module. The takeaway is that the field has moved well beyond both the simple assimilation model and the simple victim-migrant model — toward a framework that takes seriously both transnational agency and structural constraint, both cultural complexity and political power.

Narration transcript

This module has explored how migration reshapes identity through multiple lenses: transnationalism and its challenge to methodological nationalism; intersectionality and the compounding of race, gender, legal status, disability, and sexual orientation; the politics of classification and the human cost of categories like 'refugee' and 'migrant'; the particular vulnerabilities of children, disabled migrants, and LGBTQ+ asylum seekers; cultural adaptation and the debate between assimilation and multiculturalism; and the structural role of racism and xenophobia in shaping integration outcomes.
