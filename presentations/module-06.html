Refugees, Asylum, and Forced Displacement

What protections does international law offer — and where does the system fail?

Module 6 of 14ConceptualPolicy

Narration transcript

International refugee law promises protection to those who flee persecution. In theory, the 1951 Refugee Convention and its Protocol create a robust framework of rights and obligations. In practice, the system is under severe strain: underfunded, politically contested, and structurally limited in ways that leave tens of millions of forcibly displaced people without adequate protection. This module examines the framework, its achievements, and its critical gaps.

DefinitionNon-refoulement — the prohibition on returning a person to a country where they face persecution — is the cornerstone of international refugee law. It is legally binding even on states that have not ratified the Convention.

Full slide detail

  • The 1951 Refugee Convention defines a refugee as a person outside their country of nationality who cannot return owing to a well-founded fear of persecution on five specific grounds: race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group.
  • Non-refoulement (Article 33): the cornerstone principle — no state shall return a person to a territory where they face a serious risk of persecution; widely regarded as a norm of customary international law binding on all states regardless of Convention ratification.
  • Rights under the Convention: access to courts, public education, employment, travel documents, social security, and documentation — rights that apply on a spectrum based on the degree of attachment to the territory (lawful presence, lawful residence, durable integration).
  • The 1967 Protocol: removed the original geographic (Europe only) and temporal (events before 1951) limitations, making the Convention's protection universal in geographic scope; today 149 states are party to the Convention and/or Protocol.
  • Inclusion and exclusion clauses: Article 1F excludes from refugee status those who have committed war crimes, serious non-political crimes, or crimes against peace — protecting the integrity of the refugee category from abuse while creating potential gaps for complex cases.

Instructor notes

Faculty teaching outside law departments may be unfamiliar with the Convention's text. Have students read Article 1A(2) directly — the actual definition — and discuss its elements: 'outside their country of nationality,' 'well-founded fear,' 'persecution,' 'particular social group.' Each element has generated substantial legal interpretation. Lindsay Harris, Erin Barbato, Michele Pistone, and Anna Benvenue are the module's legal experts and can provide authoritative interpretation.

Narration transcript

The 1951 Refugee Convention is the foundational document of the international refugee protection system. It was adopted by a UN Conference of Plenipotentiaries in Geneva in July 1951, largely to address the massive displacement left by World War II and the emerging Cold War. Its core definition — a person outside their country of nationality with a well-founded fear of persecution on one of five specified grounds — has been remarkably durable. Its cornerstone principle, non-refoulement, the obligation not to return a person to a country where they face serious risk of persecution, is today widely accepted as a norm of customary international law, binding on all states regardless of whether they have formally ratified the Convention. 149 states are party to the Convention and/or its 1967 Protocol. But the Convention is showing its age. It was designed for European refugees in 1951. Its grounds for protection do not explicitly include climate displacement, gang violence, or the kinds of generalized violence that characterize contemporary conflict. Understanding both what the Convention does and what it was never designed to do is essential for evaluating contemporary protection debates.

Instructor notes

The IDP protection gap is one of the most important structural failings in international protection. Students often assume that being displaced within one's own country is 'better' than crossing a border. In practice, IDPs may face greater danger: they remain within reach of the government or armed group that persecuted them, and the international system has no mandatory obligation to protect them. Walter Kälin's chapter in the Oxford Handbook of International Refugee Law is the authoritative source on the Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement.

Narration transcript

UNHCR's 2025 Global Trends report gives us the most current comprehensive picture of forced displacement. At the end of 2024, 42.7 million people were refugees — including Palestine refugees under UNRWA. 73.5 million were internally displaced. 8.4 million were asylum seekers. The total: 123.2 million forcibly displaced people — a thirteenth consecutive annual record. Four countries account for more than one third of all displacement: Sudan, Syria, Afghanistan, and Ukraine. Geography matters: 71 percent of the world's refugees are hosted in low- and middle-income countries. The countries that dominate Western political debates about migration — Germany, the United States, Australia — are not, in global terms, the primary hosts of forced displacement. And the IDP situation deserves particular emphasis: 73.5 million internally displaced persons vastly outnumber refugees, yet they lack a dedicated international legal framework. The Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement, developed by Walter Kälin and the UN in 1998, and the African Union's Kampala Convention of 2009, provide important guidance — but they are not binding treaties in the way the 1951 Convention is. IDPs remain under the legal jurisdiction of the very state that may have displaced them.

Key pointIn the US, represented asylum seekers are 3–5 times more likely to succeed than unrepresented ones. Access to justice in the asylum system is, in practice, determined by ability to pay for legal representation.

Full slide detail

  • The asylum claim process: a person who arrives in or at the border of a country and claims fear of persecution must be given an opportunity to have their claim assessed before being removed — this is the procedural dimension of non-refoulement.
  • Credibility and evidence assessment: determining whether a fear of persecution is 'well-founded' requires fact-finding under difficult conditions; applicants often lack documentation; credibility assessment is subject to cultural bias and procedural inconsistency.
  • Recognition rates vary dramatically: global average refugee recognition rates are approximately 40–50 percent, but national rates vary enormously — from under 10 percent in some EU countries for specific nationalities to over 90 percent for others; this variation reflects both country conditions and procedural quality.
  • Legal representation: access to legal counsel dramatically improves asylum outcomes; in the US, represented asylum seekers are 3 to 5 times more likely to succeed than unrepresented ones (TRAC Immigration data) — creating a justice gap based on resources.
  • Appeals and delays: asylum systems in many countries are severely backlogged; in the US, the immigration court backlog exceeded 3.7 million cases in 2024; waiting periods of 3 to 7 years are not uncommon — creating prolonged legal limbo with documented mental health and economic consequences.

Instructor notes

The legal representation gap is a powerful entry point for discussions about structural inequality in the asylum system. Expert Michele Pistone (Villanova) and Erin Barbato (UW Law School Immigrant Justice Center) have direct experience with asylum representation, and their perspectives on systemic barriers will be valuable in live sessions. For classroom exercises, role-playing an asylum interview — faculty member as adjudicator, student as applicant — can demonstrate how much the outcome depends on framing, language access, and prior knowledge of the legal standard.

Narration transcript

The asylum process — the mechanism by which refugees receive formal recognition and legal protection — is complex, inconsistent, and increasingly overwhelmed by demand. When a person claims asylum, they are asking a state to formally recognize that they meet the 1951 Convention definition and therefore cannot be returned. The procedural right is grounded in non-refoulement: before a state can remove someone, it must at minimum assess whether removal would put them at risk. In practice, the quality of that assessment varies enormously. Credibility assessment — determining whether a claimed fear is genuine and well-founded — requires sophisticated fact-finding about conditions in countries of origin, conducted under resource pressure and sometimes by adjudicators with limited expertise. Recognition rates for the same nationality can vary by 20 to 30 percentage points between different European countries processing the same claims. In the United States, having legal representation increases the probability of a successful asylum claim by three to five times — yet representation is not guaranteed. The immigration court system had a backlog exceeding 3.7 million cases in 2024. People wait years. The legal limbo of prolonged asylum processing is itself a documented human rights concern, and it is produced by deliberate under-resourcing of the adjudication system.

Full slide detail

  • The encampment model: when large numbers of refugees arrive rapidly, governments and humanitarian agencies often establish camps as emergency responses; camps provide concentrated delivery of food, health, and education services but restrict movement, economic participation, and dignity.
  • Urban refugees: the majority of the world's refugees live not in camps but in urban areas, often without formal recognition; they access private housing and informal labor markets, avoiding the camp system but also its services — and facing exploitation, housing insecurity, and social isolation.
  • Earle (2024): comparing urban refugee experiences in Nairobi, Amman, and Addis Ababa, finds that urban refugees face a Hobson's choice — take humanitarian assistance in camps (with the loss of urban autonomy and economic participation) or live self-reliantly in cities without access to formal support.
  • Spiegel and Mhlanga (2022): analyze Zimbabwe's Tongogara camp as a case where encampment has lasted over 20 years; the three 'durable solutions' (voluntary return, local integration, resettlement) are all effectively blocked; the camp becomes permanent despite being framed as temporary.
  • Policy implications: UNHCR's 2016 comprehensive refugee response framework and the 2018 Global Compact on Refugees both push toward more urban-inclusive and self-reliance approaches; but national governments frequently prefer encampment because it limits refugee integration and visibility.

Instructor notes

The camps-versus-urban debate is a genuine policy controversy with defensible positions on both sides. Camps concentrate resources and enable efficient humanitarian delivery; they also create dependency, restrict rights, and can become permanent despite temporary intentions. Urban approaches preserve autonomy but leave refugees vulnerable without adequate support. Encourage students to resist false dichotomies and think about the conditions under which each approach serves protection objectives better.

Narration transcript

Where do refugees actually live? The image that dominates media coverage — the sprawling tent camp — is real but not representative. UNHCR estimates that the majority of the world's refugees live in urban areas, not in camps. They rent accommodation in city neighborhoods, work in informal labor markets, send their children to local schools, and generally avoid the camp system — partly because camps are often distant from economic opportunities, and partly because remaining in camp confers a status of dependency and visibility that many prefer to escape. But urban refugees face their own severe challenges: housing insecurity, labor market exploitation, inability to access formal services without legal documentation. Lucy Earle's 2024 comparative study of Nairobi, Amman, and Addis Ababa captures the dilemma precisely: urban refugees face a Hobson's choice. Accept humanitarian assistance in camps and sacrifice economic agency. Or live self-reliantly in cities and sacrifice protection. Neither is a genuine solution. Spiegel and Mhlanga's analysis of Zimbabwe's Tongogara camp shows where the camp model leads when durable solutions remain politically blocked: a camp originally established as a temporary emergency response has persisted for over twenty years, creating a permanent population living in a space designed for temporariness.

Key point1.4 million Rohingya people are stateless — denied citizenship by Myanmar and therefore without any state's formal protection obligation. Statelessness is a legal condition that leaves people structurally invisible to the international protection system.

Full slide detail

  • The climate displaced: no international legal instrument provides refugee status to those displaced primarily by climate change or environmental degradation; these individuals fall through both the 1951 Convention (no persecution by an actor) and standard migration channels.
  • Gang violence and criminal persecution: those fleeing MS-13 or cartel violence in Central America often do not qualify as refugees under the Convention because gangs are not state actors with political motivation, creating systematic protection gaps for Central American asylum seekers in the United States.
  • The Palestinian exception: Palestinian refugees are explicitly excluded from UNHCR's mandate by Article 1D of the Convention (covered instead by UNRWA); Erakat (2014) documents how Palestinian refugees doubly displaced by the Syrian war fell between UNRWA's territorial mandate and UNHCR's exclusion clause.
  • Stateless persons: 4.4 million stateless people (UNHCR, 2024) lack the nationality that would entitle them to either a home state's protection or, in many cases, formal refugee status; the Rohingya — 1.4 million of whom are stateless — are a paradigmatic case.
  • Secondary displaced: Ferris and Kerwin (2023) identify that even for those who formally qualify as refugees, the three durable solutions (return, integration, resettlement) all remain largely inaccessible; formal status does not guarantee a durable outcome.

Instructor notes

The Rohingya case is one of the most compelling illustrations of how legal categories — specifically statelessness — interact with physical displacement to produce extreme vulnerability. The Rohingya were stripped of Burmese citizenship under the 1982 Citizenship Law, leaving them without the nationality that is the foundation of international protection rights. Graduate students engaging with the Oxford Handbook chapter by Kälin will find authoritative analysis of the IDP protection gap that complements this slide's discussion of the refugee protection gap.

Narration transcript

International refugee law provides robust protection for those who meet its definition. But the definition has edges, and millions of people fall off those edges into protection gaps. Climate-displaced persons have no legal category. People fleeing gang violence in Central America frequently do not meet the persecution threshold because their persecutors are non-state actors. Palestine refugees, explicitly excluded from UNHCR's mandate by Article 1D, fall under UNRWA — but as Noura Erakat's 2014 analysis shows, when Palestinian refugees were doubly displaced by the Syrian civil war, they fell between UNRWA's territorial mandate and UNHCR's exclusion clause, into a protection vacuum. Stateless persons — 4.4 million documented by UNHCR, almost certainly far more undocumented — lack the nationality that is the foundational prerequisite for both state protection and most international protection mechanisms. The 1.4 million Rohingya, stripped of Burmese citizenship under the 1982 law, are the paradigmatic case: displaced, persecuted, and legally stateless — a combination that creates compounded vulnerability with no clean legal remedy. These gaps are not incidental. They are structural consequences of a protection system built around the nation-state and the persecution-flight narrative that does not map cleanly onto the complexity of twenty-first-century displacement.

Case study

The EU Asylum System: A Case Study in Structural Crisis

  3. Fortress Europe the combination of the Dublin system, Frontex border enforcement, the EU-Turkey Deal, border externalization through …
  4. The New Pact on Migration and Asylum2020, adopted 2024 the EU's most recent attempt to reform the asylum system.
  5. Greece and Hungary case studies in member state defection from protection obligations.
Key pointThe EU's Dublin Regulation, which assigns asylum responsibility to the first country of entry, has been in place since 1990. After multiple crises (2011 Libya, 2015 Syria, 2022 Ukraine), it has not been replaced — its dysfunctions serve different member states' interests simultaneously.

Full slide detail

  • The Dublin Regulation: the EU's primary rule for allocating asylum responsibility among member states; in principle, the first EU member state a person enters is responsible for their claim — in practice, this places disproportionate burden on southern and eastern border states (Greece, Italy, Hungary).
  • Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne (2020): argue that the EU asylum system is in 'constitutional crisis' — not merely under operational stress but structurally unable to reconcile the protection imperative of the 1951 Convention with member states' sovereign interest in restriction.
  • Fortress Europe: the combination of the Dublin system, Frontex border enforcement, the EU-Turkey Deal, border externalization through the EU Trust Fund for Africa, and widespread illegal pushbacks has created a system that de facto restricts access to asylum while maintaining de jure commitment to protection.
  • The New Pact on Migration and Asylum (2020, adopted 2024): the EU's most recent attempt to reform the asylum system; introduces border procedures (processing at the border rather than after entry) and a 'solidarity mechanism' for sharing responsibility across member states.
  • Greece and Hungary: case studies in member state defection from protection obligations; both countries have been documented by UNHCR, ECHR, and NGOs as conducting systematic illegal pushbacks — forced returns without asylum screening that directly violate non-refoulement.

Instructor notes

The Dublin Regulation is a law school classic — its dysfunctions are studied in every international refugee law course. For non-law faculty, emphasize the political economy: the system is deliberately structured to burden frontier states (Greece, Italy) rather than distribute responsibility equitably, because interior states (Germany, France) prefer not to be the country of first asylum. This political economy insight is more important than the technical legal details.

Narration transcript

The European Union's asylum system offers one of the most studied case studies in how international protection principles operate in practice under political pressure. The Dublin Regulation assigns asylum responsibility to the first EU member state a person enters — which in practice means Greece and Italy bear the bulk of the initial burden, because they are the entry points for the Central Mediterranean and Aegean routes. This produces structural inequity: border states are overwhelmed while interior states maintain formal non-involvement. Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne, in their 2020 analysis, argue that the EU asylum system is not just operationally strained but in 'constitutional crisis' — structurally unable to reconcile the protection imperative of international law with member states' sovereign resistance to bearing protection obligations. The combination of Dublin, Frontex, the EU-Turkey Deal, border externalization through the EU Trust Fund for Africa, and documented illegal pushbacks by multiple member states has created what critics call 'Fortress Europe': a system that de facto restricts access to asylum while maintaining de jure commitment to protection. The gap between the system's legal promise and its practical operation is precisely the terrain that Noll and colleagues analyze as structural crisis.

Durable Solutions and Their Elusiveness

Key pointUNHCR identified 2.4 million refugees urgently needing resettlement in 2024. Actual resettlement arrivals: approximately 222,000. The gap between need and capacity is tenfold.

Full slide detail

  • The three durable solutions: voluntary return (to the country of origin when conditions permit), local integration (permanent settlement in the first country of asylum), and third-country resettlement (movement to a third country that agrees to provide permanent protection).
  • Resettlement: in 2024, UNHCR identified 2.4 million refugees in urgent need of resettlement; actual resettlement arrivals totaled approximately 222,000 — a gap of 2.2 million between identified need and available places.
  • Voluntary return: over 1.6 million refugees returned to their countries of origin in 2024 (UNHCR) — largely from Syria following post-Assad political changes; but 'voluntary' return is often influenced by dwindling resources and political pressure, making genuine voluntariness difficult to assess.
  • Local integration: most countries in the Global South that host large refugee populations have not adopted formal local integration policies; in many African countries, the law prohibits refugees from working, owning property, or moving freely outside designated areas.
  • Ferris and Kerwin (2023): surveying 10 cases of protracted displacement, find that barriers to all three durable solutions are structural and persistent: security conditions prevent return; political will for integration is absent; resettlement capacity is wholly inadequate. Protracted displacement is the default outcome.
  • The 2018 Global Compact on Refugees: commits states to expanding protection capacity, increasing burden-sharing, and supporting all three durable solutions — with review mechanisms but no binding obligation.

Instructor notes

The resettlement gap is one of the most concrete expressions of the global protection shortfall. The tenfold gap between identified need and available places is not a rounding error — it reflects political choices by receiving states. Graduate students engaging with Ferris and Kerwin should produce a structured analysis of which durable solutions fail for which structural reasons across the 10 cases — a comparative policy analysis that builds directly toward Module 7's governance content.

Narration transcript

International refugee law promises three pathways out of displacement: voluntary return when conditions at home are safe, local integration in the first country of asylum, and resettlement to a third country. In practice, all three remain largely inaccessible for the vast majority of the world's forcibly displaced people. The resettlement gap is the most quantifiable expression of this failure. UNHCR identified 2.4 million refugees in urgent need of resettlement in 2024. Actual resettlement arrivals totaled approximately 222,000 — a tenfold gap. For refugees who cannot return and are not integrated into their first country of asylum, resettlement is the only legal pathway to durable protection. A tenfold gap means that most people who need it will never receive it. Voluntary return is constrained by the fact that the conditions that drove displacement — war in Sudan, Taliban rule in Afghanistan, autocracy in Eritrea — have not changed for most refugees. And local integration requires political will in host countries that is mostly absent: many refugee-hosting countries in the Global South prohibit refugees by law from working, owning property, or moving freely. Ferris and Kerwin's 2023 synthesis of ten protracted displacement cases concludes that the unavailability of durable solutions is structural — the default outcome for most refugees is not a durable solution but indefinite displacement.

Asylum Procedures: Safe Country, Accelerated Removal, and the Right to an Individual Assessment

  1. Safe third country (STC) concept a state may return an asylum seeker to a country it designates as safe without conducting a full merits determination.
  2. Accelerated and expedited procedures designed to quickly process unfounded claims.
  3. Right to an individual assessment international refugee law requires that each claim be individually assessed on its merits.
  4. Dublin Regulation and first country of entry in the EU, the country where an asylum seeker first enters is responsible for their claim.
  5. Procedural quality and recognition rates recognition rates vary enormously across EU member states for the same nationalities.
Key pointAfghan recognition rates in EU member states: below 30 percent in some, above 90 percent in others (Eurostat, 2022). The same applicant from the same country faces radically different outcomes depending on which EU state processes their claim.

Full slide detail

  • Safe third country (STC) concept: a state may return an asylum seeker to a country it designates as safe without conducting a full merits determination; the STC must offer effective protection — but the criteria for designation have been criticized as arbitrary and politically driven (UK-Rwanda, EU-Tunisia designations).
  • Accelerated and expedited procedures: designed to quickly process unfounded claims; critics document that accelerated procedures systematically disadvantage applicants with legitimate claims who have difficulty presenting evidence quickly — particularly trafficking survivors, torture survivors, and unaccompanied minors.
  • Right to an individual assessment: international refugee law requires that each claim be individually assessed on its merits; mass processing, group determination, and nationality-based adjudication (fast-tracking nationals of "safe origin" countries) all risk violating this requirement.
  • Dublin Regulation and first country of entry: in the EU, the country where an asylum seeker first enters is responsible for their claim — this creates incentive for states to refuse entry and prevent registration, and has produced systematic overload in frontline states including Greece, Italy, and Hungary.
  • Procedural quality and recognition rates: recognition rates vary enormously across EU member states for the same nationalities — e.g., recognition rates for Afghan applicants ranged from under 30 percent in some member states to over 90 percent in others in 2022 (Eurostat), illustrating that procedural quality shapes substantive outcomes.

Instructor notes

The variation in recognition rates across EU member states for the same nationalities is one of the most powerful illustrations of how procedural design affects substantive outcomes in ways that cannot be justified legally. The 1951 Convention should produce consistent outcomes for similarly situated applicants; the recognition rate disparities show it does not. This is an excellent graduate seminar discussion point: what mechanisms could reduce this variation, and would harmonization require majority-rule or unanimity?

Narration transcript

Beyond the definition of who counts as a refugee, the asylum system depends critically on the procedures used to assess claims. Several procedural tools have become central to contemporary asylum management. The safe third country concept allows a state to return an asylum seeker to another country it deems safe without fully examining their claim — but the standard for what makes a country safe enough has been fiercely contested. The UK government designated Rwanda as a safe third country; the UK Supreme Court ruled it was not. The EU designated Tunisia; human rights organizations documented systematic deportation and abandonment of migrants in the desert by Tunisian authorities. Accelerated procedures are designed to quickly filter out unfounded claims, but studies consistently show they disadvantage applicants with genuine claims who have difficulty presenting evidence under time pressure — particularly survivors of torture or trafficking who may not be able to disclose their experiences quickly. Perhaps most strikingly, recognition rates for the same nationality vary enormously across EU member states: Afghan applicants have faced recognition rates below 30 percent in some EU countries and above 90 percent in others, according to Eurostat data. The same person, from the same country, facing the same conditions, receives protection or rejection depending on which border they first crossed. This is not a system applying the law consistently. It is a system in which procedural variation produces arbitrary substantive outcomes.

Key point73.5 million IDPs at end-2024 — the single largest category of the forcibly displaced. They receive less protection, less funding, and less international attention than refugees — precisely because they haven't crossed a border.

Full slide detail

  • Internally displaced persons (IDPs): people forced to flee within their own country; unlike refugees, they remain under the jurisdiction of their own government — which may be the source of their persecution; their legal protection gap is one of the most serious in the international system.
  • The Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement (1998): a non-binding normative framework developed by UN Special Rapporteur Francis Deng, drawing on international human rights law, humanitarian law, and refugee law by analogy; 30 principles covering rights of IDPs before, during, and after displacement.
  • The Kampala Convention (2009): the African Union Convention for the Protection and Assistance of Internally Displaced Persons — the first binding international instrument specifically addressing IDPs; applicable to African Union member states; Walter Kälin (2021) notes it is the most advanced IDP protection instrument globally.
  • National legislation: about 23 countries have adopted national IDP legislation; in countries without it, IDPs rely on general domestic law that may not address their specific vulnerabilities — property loss, documentation gaps, camp conditions.
  • Scale of the gap: at end of 2024, UNHCR counted 73.5 million IDPs — the single largest category of the forcibly displaced, larger than the refugee population; yet IDPs receive far less international funding, legal protection, and resettlement opportunity than refugees precisely because they remain under their state's jurisdiction.

Instructor notes

The IDP protection gap is one of the most important structural failings in the international system, and it is systematically underemphasized in public discourse about refugees and displacement. Faculty should draw students' attention to the counterintuitive fact that IDPs — often the most vulnerable because they remain within reach of the persecuting state — have the weakest formal international legal protection. Walter Kälin's chapter in the Oxford Handbook of Refugee Law is the authoritative source on this topic for graduate students.

Narration transcript

Among the most significant protection gaps in the international system concerns internally displaced persons — people who have been forced to flee but remain within their own country's borders. At the end of 2024, UNHCR counted approximately 73.5 million IDPs globally — making them the single largest category of the forcibly displaced, larger than the refugee population. Yet they receive far less international protection, far less funding, and far less resettlement opportunity. The reason is structural: IDPs remain within the jurisdiction of their own state. If that state is also the source of their persecution — as is the case in Myanmar, Syria, Sudan, and elsewhere — international law faces a direct conflict between state sovereignty and the responsibility to protect. In 1998, UN Special Rapporteur Francis Deng developed the Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement — a non-binding framework drawing on human rights and refugee law by analogy. The Kampala Convention, adopted by the African Union in 2009, went further, creating the first binding international instrument specifically addressing IDP protection. Walter Kälin's authoritative analysis in the Oxford Handbook of International Refugee Law identifies the Kampala Convention as the most advanced IDP protection instrument globally — a significant African-led contribution to international protection law. But globally, the protection gap for IDPs remains enormous.

Key pointArticle 1D of the 1951 Convention: Palestinian refugees under UNRWA's mandate are excluded from Convention protection. This was meant to provide equivalent protection through UNRWA — but UNRWA's mandate covers services, not protection, creating a systematic vulnerability.

Full slide detail

  • UNRWA and Article 1D: the 1951 Convention Article 1D excludes from its scope persons who receive protection from another UN organ or agency; Palestinian refugees under UNRWA's mandate are therefore excluded from the Convention's protection — creating a parallel (and in practice more limited) protection system.
  • UNRWA (United Nations Relief and Works Agency): established in 1949 to provide services to Palestinian refugees; now serves approximately 5.9 million registered Palestinian refugees across Gaza, the West Bank, Jordan, Lebanon, and Syria; its mandate is services (education, health, relief) rather than protection.
  • Noura Erakat (2014): analyzed the double vulnerability of Palestinian refugees displaced from Syria by the Syrian civil war — falling between UNHCR and UNRWA mandates; Palestinian-Syrians argued they could not receive UNRWA assistance because UNRWA's operations in Syria had collapsed, but were excluded from UNHCR protection by Article 1D.
  • Gaza 2023–present: the 2023 conflict in Gaza internally displaced approximately 90 percent of Gaza's population; approximately 1.4 million Gazans are registered as both refugees under UNRWA's mandate AND internally displaced — a layering of displacement categories that illustrates the inadequacy of existing frameworks.
  • Political dimensions: UNRWA's mandate and funding have been repeatedly threatened by donor state politics; the US withdrew UNRWA funding in 2018–2021 (restored under Biden); several states withdrew or suspended funding following allegations regarding UNRWA staff in 2024, creating severe humanitarian shortfalls.

Instructor notes

The Palestinian refugee situation is politically sensitive but analytically essential: it illustrates how the 1951 Convention system was designed with particular political contexts in mind and produces significant gaps when applied to populations its architects did not anticipate fully. The focus here should be on the structural legal analysis rather than the political dispute — the Article 1D exclusion and its consequences is a clear factual matter. Erakat's 2014 article is excellent for graduate students engaging with the legal analysis of protection gaps.

Narration transcript

The Palestinian refugee situation requires specific attention because it sits outside the main 1951 Convention protection system in ways that are legally significant and practically consequential. Article 1D of the 1951 Convention explicitly excludes persons who are already receiving protection or assistance from another UN organ or agency — and Palestinian refugees under UNRWA's mandate fall into this exclusion. UNRWA was established in 1949 to provide services to Palestinian refugees: education, health care, and relief. But UNRWA's mandate is services, not protection — it does not have the mandate to determine refugee status, facilitate resettlement, or provide the legal protection functions that UNHCR performs for other refugees. Noura Erakat's 2014 analysis documents the acute version of this problem: Palestinian refugees who fled the Syrian civil war found themselves excluded from both systems — excluded from UNHCR protection by Article 1D, and excluded from UNRWA assistance because UNRWA's Syrian operations had collapsed under the war. They were, in legal and practical terms, doubly unprotected. The current conflict in Gaza adds further complexity: approximately 90 percent of Gaza's population was internally displaced by the conflict, with approximately 1.4 million people registered simultaneously as refugees under UNRWA's mandate and as internally displaced persons — layers of vulnerability that existing frameworks were not designed to address.

Resettlement: The Third Durable Solution and Its Limits

Key point154,300 resettlement arrivals globally in 2023 — against approximately 43 million refugees and 8 million asylum seekers. Resettlement reaches less than 1 percent of those eligible. It is symbolically important but operationally marginal.

Full slide detail

  • Resettlement: the transfer of refugees from the country of first asylum to a third country that agrees to accept them permanently; resettlement is one of the three durable solutions alongside voluntary repatriation and local integration; it is the most resource-intensive and reaches the smallest number of refugees.
  • Scale gap: UNHCR referred approximately 2.4 million refugees for resettlement in 2023; total resettlement places offered globally were approximately 154,300 (2023 UNHCR data) — meaning resettlement reaches well under 1 percent of the global refugee population annually.
  • The US resettlement program: historically the world's largest resettlement program, admitting approximately 95,000 refugees at its peak (2016); reduced to approximately 18,000 in FY2020 under Trump; restored to approximately 125,000 in FY2023 under Biden; under the second Trump administration, resettlement was again severely curtailed in 2025.
  • Resettlement vs. local integration: UNHCR and scholars increasingly argue that local integration — allowing refugees to work, settle, and eventually citizenship in countries of first asylum — is more scalable than resettlement to third countries; but it requires major changes in host country law and public opinion, particularly in the Global South.
  • Protracted displacement: Ferris and Kerwin (2023) synthesize 10 cases of protracted displacement and find that durable solutions remain elusive due to political, legal, and resource barriers; the average duration of displacement for the world's protracted refugee situations is over 20 years.

Instructor notes

The gap between the resettlement referrals (2.4 million) and actual places (154,300) is striking and worth quantifying carefully. This is not because of bureaucratic inefficiency but because wealthy destination states simply do not offer enough places. The political economy of resettlement is worth discussing: why do wealthy states offer so little resettlement? Who advocates for it, who opposes it, and through what mechanisms? Graduate students can engage with the policy design literature on how resettlement programs have been expanded and contracted across administrations.

Narration transcript

Resettlement — the transfer of a refugee from their country of first asylum to a third country willing to accept them permanently — is the durable solution that receives the most public attention in wealthy countries but reaches the smallest share of refugees. UNHCR referred approximately 2.4 million refugees for resettlement consideration in 2023. Total resettlement places offered by all countries globally were approximately 154,300. That means resettlement reached fewer than one in fifteen of those referred by UNHCR, and far less than one percent of the global refugee population. The United States has historically been the world's largest resettlement country, admitting tens of thousands of refugees annually. But the program has been subject to dramatic political swings: admissions fell from approximately 95,000 in fiscal year 2016 to approximately 18,000 in fiscal year 2020 under the first Trump administration, before being restored under Biden. The global resettlement program is not primarily a technical problem — it is a political one. Wealthy states choose to offer far fewer places than the need requires. Elizabeth Ferris and Donald Kerwin's 2023 synthesis of protracted displacement cases found that all three durable solutions — repatriation, local integration, and resettlement — have become harder to achieve in the current political environment, and that the average protracted displacement situation now lasts more than 20 years. For the individual refugee, that is not a statistic. It is a life in waiting.

Ask the roomCritique question: does the asylum system you are evaluating protect the people it is designed to protect — and if not, why not? Is the gap a resource problem, a legal design problem, or a political will problem?

Full slide detail

  • The signature experience for Module 6 asks students to critically evaluate an asylum system's effectiveness — examining the gap between the system's stated purposes (protection, fairness, non-refoulement) and its actual operations and outcomes.
  • Analytical framework: Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne (2020) provide a model for evaluating asylum systems through a legal scholarship lens — examining how the crisis in EU refugee law widened the gap between Convention ideals and institutional realities through procedural complexity, 'safe country' designations, and accelerated procedures.
  • Discussion prompt 1: Choose one asylum system (US, EU, Australia, Canada, Kenya, Lebanon) and identify: What is the formal legal framework? What populations does it cover? What are the processing timelines? What is the recognition rate? What happens to rejected claimants?
  • Discussion prompt 2: Earle (2024) documents urban refugees in Nairobi, Amman, and Addis Ababa choosing informal urban life over camps, often without formal status — what does this choice reveal about the system's failure to provide meaningful protection?
  • Evaluation criteria: an effective asylum system should be legally compliant (respecting non-refoulement), procedurally fair (individualized assessment, right to appeal), timely (reducing years-long backlogs), accessible (reachable by those who need it), and humane (not relying on deterrence through cruelty).

Instructor notes

This slide frames the signature experience assignment directly. Faculty should distribute a rubric at this point that maps the analytical framework (legal compliance, procedural fairness, timeliness, accessibility, humaneness) onto the specific requirements of the written critique. The assignment works best when students choose contrasting systems — comparing a Global North asylum system with a major Global South host country often produces rich analysis of how burden and capacity align (or fail to align) with formal legal obligations.

Narration transcript

Let us close by framing the signature experience for this module. You have been asked to produce a policy critique of an asylum system's effectiveness — and now that we have surveyed the landscape of refugee law, protection frameworks, and protection gaps, you have the analytical tools to conduct that critique rigorously. The framework we propose draws on the legal scholarship approach developed by Noll, Vedsted-Hansen, and Byrne: examine the stated legal purpose of the system, then examine how its actual procedures and outcomes compare. Ask: who can reach this system? Who cannot? How long does determination take? What is the recognition rate for different nationalities? What happens to people whose claims are rejected but who cannot be safely returned? What does the system do to deter applications, and are those deterrence mechanisms compatible with the system's legal obligations? Lucy Earle's 2024 comparative study of urban refugees in Nairobi, Amman, and Addis Ababa adds a human dimension: when refugees choose precarious urban informality over formal camp registration, they are often voting with their feet against a protection system that offers aid but at the cost of their autonomy, livelihood, and dignity. Their choices are themselves a form of critique. An effective asylum system should not require people to choose between safety and a life worth living.

Module 6 Takeaways: Refugees, Asylum, and Forced Displacement

  1. The 1951 Refugee Convention defines the legal refugee category and establishes non-refoulement as its cornerstone.
  2. 123.2 million forcibly displaced at end-2024 (UNHCR): 42.7 million refugees, 73.5 million IDPs, 8.4 million asylum seekers.
  3. Structural protection gaps exist for climate-displaced persons, gang violence victims, stateless persons, and Palestinians.
  4. The EU asylum system exemplifies structural crisis: the Dublin Regulation creates inequitable burden distribution.
  5. Durable solutions remain inaccessible for most refugees: the resettlement gap (2.4 million in need, 222,000 places available), restricted local integration, and …
  6. The asylum process is not simply a legal determination but a social justice system: access to representation, quality of adjudication, and policy funding levels …

Full slide detail

  • The 1951 Refugee Convention defines the legal refugee category and establishes non-refoulement as its cornerstone — a norm of customary international law binding on all states regardless of ratification.
  • 123.2 million forcibly displaced at end-2024 (UNHCR): 42.7 million refugees, 73.5 million IDPs, 8.4 million asylum seekers — with IDPs representing the largest and least legally protected category.
  • Structural protection gaps exist for climate-displaced persons, gang violence victims, stateless persons, and Palestinians — groups who fall between or outside the major legal frameworks.
  • The EU asylum system exemplifies structural crisis: the Dublin Regulation creates inequitable burden distribution; widespread illegal pushbacks violate non-refoulement; and the gap between legal commitment and operational reality is itself a finding, not a flaw.
  • Durable solutions remain inaccessible for most refugees: the resettlement gap (2.4 million in need, 222,000 places available), restricted local integration, and conditional voluntary return combine to produce protracted displacement as the default outcome.
  • The asylum process is not simply a legal determination but a social justice system: access to representation, quality of adjudication, and policy funding levels determine outcomes as much as the legal merits of individual cases.

Instructor notes

Preview Module 7: the protection gaps identified in this module are largely produced by the border and sovereignty architecture examined in Module 7. The decision to conduct illegal pushbacks, to externalize borders, to restrict asylum access — these are state decisions made within (or in violation of) a legal framework. Module 7 examines the legal architecture and political economy of those decisions.

Narration transcript

Module 6 has taken us through the international protection system from its legal foundations to its practical failures. The 1951 Convention is a genuine achievement — a legally binding commitment by 149 states to protect people fleeing persecution, anchored by the non-refoulement principle. But 75 years after its drafting, the system is under severe and growing strain. 123.2 million forcibly displaced people; protection gaps for climate victims, gang violence survivors, stateless persons, and Palestinians; asylum systems overwhelmed by backlog and under-resourced; a resettlement gap of 10 to 1 between need and availability; and widespread state practices — pushbacks, border externalization, detention — that undermine the protection commitments states formally maintain. Noll and colleagues argue this is a constitutional crisis, not just an operational one. In Module 7, we examine the legal and political architecture of borders and sovereignty that produces these conditions — and ask whether the tension between state sovereignty and international protection obligations can be resolved, or whether it is a structural feature of the world we live in.
