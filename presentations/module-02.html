Migration Data and Global Trends

Who counts migrants, what gets missed, and how do numbers shape politics?

Analytical

Statistics updated June 2026 (UNHCR Global Trends 2026, IOM World Migration Report 2026). Audio narration may cite the earlier end-2024 figures.

Narration transcript

Migration generates enormous quantities of data — and yet some of the most important facts about who moves, why, and with what consequences remain stubbornly unmeasured. This module introduces the major data producers and their methodologies, maps the known gaps, and asks a fundamental critical question: when a statistic is cited in a policy debate, whose interests does it serve, and whose experience does it erase?

Key pointYou cannot manage what you cannot measure — but measurement itself shapes what gets managed and who gets counted.

Full slide detail

  • Migration is defined differently across national statistical systems: some countries use administrative residence data, others use border crossing records, others rely on census self-identification — making cross-national comparison inherently problematic.
  • The population of interest changes continuously: migrants arrive, depart, gain citizenship, have children, become undocumented or regularize; no single snapshot captures the full population.
  • Political sensitivity distorts collection: governments may under-report irregular arrivals to avoid political controversy; conversely, they may inflate 'migrant numbers' in enforcement contexts.
  • Ahmad-yar and Bircan (2021), in a systematic review of international migration statistics, identify five categories of gap: definitional inconsistencies, incomplete driver data, geographic blind spots, missing demographic groups, and time lags.
  • Despite challenges, good migration data is essential: it informs resource allocation for health, education, and housing; it enables researchers to distinguish evidence from assertion in policy debates.

Instructor notes

Open by asking students to try to answer a simple question: 'How many people migrated from Mexico to the United States last year?' They will quickly discover the answer depends entirely on the definition used — temporary visa holders, undocumented entrants, lawful permanent residents, border crossings? This exercise demonstrates the definitional problem concretely before introducing data sources.

Narration transcript

Migration data seems like it should be straightforward — count the people, map the flows, report the numbers. In practice, it is one of the most technically challenging areas of social statistics, and one of the most politically fraught. Consider the definition problem: some countries count as 'migrants' anyone who was born in another country, regardless of how long they have lived there or what citizenship they hold. Others count only recent movers. Still others rely on census questions that depend on self-identification. When you compare a statistic from Germany with one from Qatar, you may be comparing completely incommensurable things. Ahmad-yar and Bircan systematically reviewed international migration statistics in 2021 and identified five overlapping categories of gap: definitional inconsistency, incomplete data on why people move, geographic blind spots especially in conflict zones, missing demographic groups including children and people with disabilities, and time lags that mean official statistics may be years out of date. These are not minor technicalities — they mean that some of the most important migration facts are simply unavailable.

Instructor notes

Have students navigate at least one of these platforms during class or as a homework assignment. UNHCR's Refugee Data Finder (data.unhcr.org) and the World Bank's KNOMAD platform are both publicly accessible and relatively navigable. This transforms the slide from abstract description into practical data literacy. Emphasize that every dataset has a mandate — UNHCR only tracks people within its protection mandate; OECD only covers OECD destination countries.

Narration transcript

Let us survey the landscape of data producers. At the top level, UN DESA produces the International Migrant Stock dataset — the most widely cited source for how many international migrants there are globally. It uses census and administrative data and is updated every five years, which means it is always somewhat dated. The UN Refugee Agency, UNHCR, produces the most comprehensive annual data on forcibly displaced populations: refugees, asylum seekers, IDPs, and stateless persons. Its 2025 Global Trends report recorded 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at the end of 2024. The International Organization for Migration publishes the World Migration Report every two years and runs the Missing Migrants Project, tracking deaths along migration routes. The World Bank leads on remittance data through the KNOMAD platform: officially recorded remittances to low and middle income countries were estimated at 685 billion dollars in 2024. And the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, known as IDMC, is the authoritative source on internal displacement from both conflict and disasters. Each of these sources has a different mandate, a different methodology, and a different coverage. Good migration data literacy requires knowing what each source measures and what it does not.

Full slide detail

  • Forced displacement: 117.8 million forcibly displaced at end-2025, including 41.6 million refugees, 68.7 million IDPs, and 9 million asylum seekers — one in every 70 people on Earth (UNHCR Global Trends 2026).
  • Hosting countries: contrary to political discourse in wealthy countries, 68 percent of the world's refugees are hosted in low- and middle-income countries; the largest host is not Germany or the US but Turkey (via Afghan and Syrian populations), followed by Colombia and Germany (UNHCR 2024).
  • Major corridors: the Mexico-to-US corridor is the world's largest bilateral migration route; other significant flows include India-UAE, India-US, India-UK, Bangladesh-India, and Mexico-to-US reflects over a century of labor migration infrastructure.
  • Regional character: approximately 40 percent of international migration is South-to-South (IOM World Migration Report 2024); European, North American, and Gulf destinations together host roughly 60 percent of the global migrant stock.

Instructor notes

The hosting country data consistently surprises students. Use this as a leverage point for the 'Global North burden-sharing' debate in international refugee law. Draw attention to the difference between stock (how many people are there in total) and flow (how many people moved in a given period) — students and media frequently confuse these, leading to misinterpretation of trends.

Narration transcript

What does the global picture actually look like? According to UN DESA's 2025 estimates, there were 304 million international migrants in 2024 — roughly double the 1990 figure in absolute terms, but only a modest increase as a share of world population, from 2.9 to 3.7 percent. The forced displacement picture is considerably more alarming: UNHCR's June 2025 Global Trends report recorded 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at the end of 2024 — a thirteenth consecutive annual increase. One in every 67 people on earth is forcibly displaced. And where are these people? Predominantly in the Global South: 71 percent of the world's refugees are hosted in low- and middle-income countries. Turkey hosts more refugees than any other single country. Colombia hosts more than 2.9 million Venezuelans. The countries dominating political debates about migration — Germany, the United States, the UK — are not, in global terms, the primary hosts of forced displacement. This structural reality should recalibrate how we teach the 'burden-sharing' debates in international refugee law.

  3. Persons with disabilities migration data almost universally lacks disability disaggregation.

  5. Nomadic and pastoralist populations communities whose traditional mobility patterns cross international borders are often entirely absent from national …
Key pointIf a population is not counted, it is not funded, not protected, and not represented. Data gaps are also power gaps.

Full slide detail

  • Undocumented migrants: by definition, cannot be directly counted; estimates use a combination of residual methods, surveys, and administrative records; global estimates of 35-40 million undocumented are commonly cited but carry enormous uncertainty.
  • Children in migration: UNICEF estimates roughly 36 million displaced children globally (including IDPs) but child-specific migration statistics — particularly for unaccompanied and separated children — are poorly harmonized across national systems.
  • Persons with disabilities: migration data almost universally lacks disability disaggregation; the 2030 Agenda's 'leave no one behind' commitment is therefore unmeasurable in migration contexts.
  • Short-term and circular migrants: labor migration systems in the Gulf, Southeast Asia, and agriculture worldwide involve high circular mobility; short-term movers below the one-year threshold fall entirely outside standard international migrant definitions.
  • Nomadic and pastoralist populations: communities whose traditional mobility patterns cross international borders are often entirely absent from national censuses, creating a systematic gap for specific African and Central Asian populations.

Instructor notes

The gap on disability is particularly striking and often overlooked. Graduate students interested in intersectionality should be directed to the disability-migration nexus literature. For undergraduates, a simple question: 'Name a group of migrants who you think is definitely undercounted in official statistics.' Allow several answers, then discuss what methodology might be used to estimate each group. This builds methodological intuition.

Narration transcript

Every data system has a boundary, and the people outside that boundary are, in a meaningful sense, statistically invisible. Undocumented migrants cannot be directly counted. Estimates using residual methods — comparing census population to expected population — suggest global figures around 35 to 40 million, but uncertainty bands around this estimate are huge. Children are poorly tracked across national systems; UNICEF has estimated roughly 36 million displaced children globally, but data on unaccompanied and separated children is especially patchy. People with disabilities are almost entirely absent from migration statistics, despite the 2030 Agenda's explicit commitment to disaggregated data. Short-term and circular migrants — the Bangladeshi laborer in Qatar on a two-year contract, the Mexican farmworker crossing seasonally — fall below the one-year threshold that defines an international migrant in most official systems. These are not obscure edge cases. They are, in many contexts, the heart of the migration system. And because they are invisible to data, they are also invisible to policy.

Full slide detail

  • Traditional migration statistics measure stock (how many people are living somewhere at a point in time); flow data (how many people moved in a given period) is much harder to capture and historically sparse.
  • Johnston, Giraudy, Chi, Abel, and Bailey (2025), published in PNAS, demonstrate that privacy-protected Facebook location data from three billion users can generate monthly country-to-country flow estimates for 181 countries with strong correspondence to official statistics.
  • Other non-traditional data sources include mobile phone data, satellite imagery (tracking population density changes), Twitter/X geotagged posts, and LinkedIn profile changes for skilled migrants.
  • Schellerer (2023) uses a novel estimation framework to produce the first systematic global bilateral refugee flow estimates from 1990 to 2015, revealing a post-1990s decline in refugee flows and a geographic shift from Africa to Asia.
  • Ha, Dewaard, Abel and colleagues (2024) use flow estimates to test whether migration is truly 'globalizing' — finding that while the number of countries involved has increased, most flows remain regionally concentrated.

Instructor notes

The Johnston et al. (2025) paper is a pedagogically rich case: it demonstrates methodological innovation while raising ethical questions about surveillance, data ownership, and who controls knowledge about migrant populations. Graduate students should engage with both the methodological contribution and the ethical critique. Undergraduates can focus on the simpler insight: alternative data sources can fill official data gaps.

Narration transcript

One of the most exciting — and ethically complex — frontiers in migration research is the use of big data and digital trace data to fill gaps that official statistics cannot. Johnston and colleagues, publishing in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in 2025, demonstrated that privacy-protected location data from roughly three billion Facebook users can generate monthly bilateral flow estimates for 181 countries, and that these estimates correlate strongly with official statistics where those exist. This is remarkable: a single platform's data can, in principle, fill a gap that has plagued migration researchers for decades — the absence of good bilateral flow data. But it raises uncomfortable questions. Who controls this data? What happens if governments use it for enforcement rather than planning? Do migrants — many of whom use social media precisely because they are cut off from official channels — consent to having their mobility tracked? The scientific opportunity and the ethical challenge are inseparable, and that tension is worth teaching explicitly.

Key pointThe same statistic — '100 million displaced people' — can be framed as evidence of global failure requiring urgent response, or as a political threat requiring emergency enforcement. The data does not speak for itself.

Full slide detail

  • Migration statistics are not neutral: the same set of numbers can be presented to suggest a crisis (absolute numbers are at historic highs) or to suggest stability (share of world population is only 3.7 percent) — both claims use the same data.
  • Erokhin, Komendantova, and Albano (2023), analyzing 45,000 tweets, document how migration misinformation spreads through social media faster than corrections, shaping public attitudes even after corrections are issued.
  • The 'invasion' frame: political actors regularly present migration statistics alongside threat language — 'wave,' 'flood,' 'invasion' — that metaphorically renders migration as natural disaster and migrants as non-agents, justifying emergency responses.
  • Conversely, under-reporting of irregular migration may serve political interests of governments who wish to minimize the appearance of border control failure.
  • Menjívar, Kraly, and Reed (2024), reviewing 60 years of International Migration Review scholarship, note that data framing has itself been a subject of scholarly scrutiny: who produces knowledge about migration, from what methodological position, matters.
  • Faculty responsibility: teaching students to interrogate the political use of statistics — not to dismiss data but to ask 'who collected this, for what purpose, and in what political context?' — is a core media literacy and critical thinking outcome.

Instructor notes

This slide connects to the the 'Media, Narratives, and Representation' module topic on media and narratives. If you are teaching in a politically polarized context, model calm analytical engagement: do not tell students what political conclusions to draw from the data. Instead, demonstrate the analytical tools for evaluating claims: What is the source? What is the definition? What time period? What comparison? Students with very different political starting points can agree on methodological standards.

Narration transcript

Numbers do not speak for themselves. Every statistic appears in a narrative context that shapes what it means, and migration statistics are especially vulnerable to political instrumentalization. Consider this: according to UNHCR, 123.2 million people were forcibly displaced at the end of 2024. One politician might present this figure to argue for massive investment in humanitarian response and addressing root causes. Another might present the same figure to argue for emergency border closures and military deployment. The number is the same; the political conclusions drawn from it are diametrically opposed. Erokhin and colleagues, analyzing 45,000 tweets, demonstrate that migration misinformation spreads faster than corrections on social media — a structural dynamic that means false statistics can circulate widely and shape public attitudes even after they are debunked. For faculty, the task is not to tell students what the correct political conclusion is. It is to teach them the methodological tools to evaluate claims: Where did this number come from? What definition underlies it? What does it include and exclude? Over what time period? That critical literacy is perhaps the most durable skill this module can develop.

Instructor notes

A map exercise works well here: have students mark on a world map where the major corridors run, then color-code by type (labor, refugee, family reunification, internal EU). The visual pattern challenges assumptions about where migration flows and demonstrates the regional concentration Ha et al. identify. If possible, compare a 1990 map with a 2024 map using Abel & Sander's flow visualizations.

Narration transcript

Let us add geographic texture to the numbers. The US-Mexico corridor is the most studied bilateral migration route in the world — an estimated 11 million Mexico-born people lived in the United States as of 2022, a flow sustained by over a century of labor demand, recruitment programs, family networks, and geographic proximity. The Gulf Cooperation Council states — Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, and others — host roughly 35 million migrant workers under the kafala sponsorship system. In Qatar, migrants constitute 88 percent of the population; in the UAE, 87 percent. These are societies that could not function without migrant labor, yet that labor is structurally temporary, with few pathways to permanent residence or citizenship. Intra-African and South-South corridors receive far less attention: the India-Bangladesh corridor, the movements between Cote d'Ivoire and Burkina Faso, the circulation within the East African Community. Ha and colleagues, studying flow data from 1990 to 2020, find that while more countries now participate in international migration networks, the majority of flows remain regionally concentrated. The globalization of migration is real but more partial than political rhetoric suggests.

Full slide detail

  • Remittances — money sent by migrants to family and community in their origin country — totaled an estimated $685 billion to low- and middle-income countries in 2024 (World Bank, 2024), exceeding total global foreign direct investment to developing countries.
  • Top receiving countries in 2024: India, Mexico, China, Philippines, and Egypt — with Mexico receiving about $68 billion in 2023, a 7.8 percent increase, driven by US labor market strength.
  • Remittances are countercyclical: they tend to increase during economic shocks in origin countries because migrants send more when their families need support — making them more stable development finance than FDI.
  • The cost of sending: average cost of sending 200 dollars globally was 6.2 percent in Q4 2023 (World Bank), far above the 3 percent SDG 10.c target; costs are highest for Sub-Saharan Africa corridors.
  • Informal remittances (hawala, hand-carried cash) are not captured in official data and may exceed formal flows in some corridors — the 'missing' remittances problem parallels the missing migrant problem.

Instructor notes

The remittances figure consistently surprises students. Connect it to the 'Migration, Labor, and the Economy' module (migration and the global economy) by previewing the debate over whether remittances drive development or sustain dependency. For a practical exercise: have students calculate how much a migrant worker in Qatar earning the median migrant wage would need to remit to meaningfully increase their family's income — this contextualizes the macroeconomic aggregate with a human calculation.

Narration transcript

There is one financial flow in the global migration system that receives far too little attention in mainstream economics and policy: remittances. In 2024, officially recorded remittance flows to low- and middle-income countries were estimated at 685 billion dollars by the World Bank. To put that in perspective: this figure exceeds total foreign direct investment flows to developing countries. It dwarfs official development assistance. These are private transfers from individual workers to their families — not government grants, not corporate investments, but person-to-person transfers that sustain households, fund children's educations, build homes, and provide healthcare in dozens of low- and middle-income countries. Mexico alone received 66.2 billion dollars in remittances in 2023. India receives more than any other country. And these are only the officially recorded flows — the hawala networks, the hand-carried cash, the mobile phone transfers that avoid formal banking are not captured. The true figure is likely considerably higher.

Full slide detail

  • The signature experience for this module asks students to produce a 'data brief': a structured analytical summary of one migration trend using at least two data sources, with explicit identification of at least one methodological limitation or population gap.
  • Undergraduate brief components: (1) identify and describe a migration trend using official data; (2) visualize or summarize the key figure; (3) name one population that is missing from the data; (4) explain why the gap matters.
  • Graduate brief components: additionally (5) evaluate the methodological strengths and limitations of the sources used; (6) propose one concrete improvement to data collection that would fill a specific gap; (7) assess how the data gap creates a policy blind spot.
  • Example trends to analyze: Syrian forced displacement 2011–2024; Venezuelan emigration 2015–present; Gulf labor migration under kafala; unaccompanied child asylum applications in the EU; intra-African mobility.
  • Common pitfall: conflating correlation with causation in trend interpretation — e.g., concluding that increased border enforcement caused a decline in irregular migration without controlling for economic conditions.

Instructor notes

The data brief can be scaffolded differently depending on student level. For first-year undergraduates, provide a template with explicit prompts. For advanced students, require them to independently select their trend and sources. Assess not just accuracy but methodological self-awareness. A brief that acknowledges its own limitations is stronger than one that presents numbers without qualification.

Narration transcript

This module's signature assignment is the data brief. Unlike a research paper, a data brief is a structured, concise analytical document — typically two to four pages — that identifies a migration trend, presents it accurately using real data sources, and explicitly names the gaps. The requirement to identify a missing population is not a minor addendum to the main analysis. It is the methodological heart of the exercise. Why? Because every dataset reflects a set of choices about who to count and how. Naming those choices — and their consequences — is the difference between naive data use and sophisticated data literacy. If you write a brief on Syrian displacement that uses only UNHCR data and does not mention the approximately seven million Syrians displaced inside Syria — who are IDPs under IDMC's mandate, not refugees under UNHCR's — you have produced a technically accurate but substantially incomplete picture. Graduate students are asked to go further: to propose how the gap could be closed, and to explain what policy decisions are being made in the blind spot.

Full slide detail

  • The conventional wisdom: globalization has produced an era of unprecedented international mobility, with people flowing freely across borders alongside goods and capital.
  • Ha, Dewaard, Abel et al. (2024): analysis of country-to-country flow estimates from 1990 to 2020 finds that migration has become more 'global' in connectivity (more countries participate) but not in intensity (most flows remain regionally concentrated and proportionally stable).
  • The digitization of borders since 9/11 — biometric databases, visa pre-screening, carrier liability — has created infrastructure that makes international mobility more surveilled, more filtered, and more accessible for some populations while more restricted for others.
  • Mobility justice critique: the freedom to cross borders correlates almost perfectly with passport strength — a citizen of Germany or Japan faces few barriers; a citizen of Afghanistan or Syria faces nearly insurmountable ones; this differential is not natural but politically produced.
  • Menjívar, Kraly, and Reed (2024): reviewing 60 years of scholarship, identify South-South flows and mixed migration as two of the most important understudied dimensions of contemporary mobility — areas where existing theoretical models are least adequate.

Instructor notes

The mobility justice framing connects data about passport strength to normative debates about equality. The Henley Passport Index or similar passport strength databases are freely available and can be used for a quick in-class data exercise: students search their own passport's ranking and then compare with a classmate from a different country. The exercise makes visible the degree to which 'freedom of movement' is unevenly distributed.

Narration transcript

Is migration globalizing? The intuitive answer is yes — we see images of migrants from every corner of the world, border crossings are in the news constantly, and the absolute number of international migrants has roughly doubled since 1990. But Ha and colleagues' careful analysis of bilateral flow data from 1990 to 2020 complicates this picture. What they find is that migration has become more globally connected in the sense that more country pairs are now exchanging migrants — but the intensity of flows, as a share of world population, remains regionally concentrated and proportionally modest. The 'globalization of migration' is partly real and partly a political-narrative construction. What has genuinely changed is the infrastructure of control: since the early 2000s, biometric databases, airline pre-screening, carrier liability regulations, and satellite-monitored borders have transformed crossing an international boundary from a relatively simple act into one that is intensely surveilled and filtered. For holders of strong passports, this surveillance is largely invisible. For holders of weak passports — and those without any passport — it is the defining feature of any attempted movement.

Full slide detail

  • Migrant stock: the number of people living outside their country of birth at a given point in time; UN DESA's International Migrant Stock database (published every five years) is the primary global stock dataset, with the 2024 revision estimating 304 million international migrants.
  • Migrant flow: the number of people crossing a border in a given time period; flow data is far harder to collect than stock data — most countries do not systematically record departures, and administrative definitions of what constitutes a 'migration' event vary widely.
  • The stock-flow relationship: stock is the accumulated result of past flows minus deaths and return migration; using stock data to infer flow magnitudes or trends is methodologically risky — two countries may have the same stock but very different flow dynamics.
  • Bilateral flow matrices: Abel and others have developed methodologies to estimate bilateral (country-to-country) migration flows from sequential stock data; Johnston and colleagues' 2025 PNAS study used Facebook data for 181 countries to generate monthly flow estimates, closely replicating official statistics and demonstrating the potential of passive digital data.
  • Why the distinction matters for policy: a country's migrant stock can remain stable while underlying flows are large and circular — 'replacement migration,' guest worker programs, and seasonal agriculture all generate high flow with modest net stock change; conflating stock and flow leads to misleading policy analysis.

Instructor notes

The stock-flow distinction is one of the most fundamental methodological points in migration data literacy, and it is one that journalists and policymakers routinely get wrong. Spending time on this conceptually pays dividends throughout the course. The Johnston et al. 2025 study is excellent for discussing the promises and ethical challenges of big-data approaches to migration measurement.

Narration transcript

One of the most fundamental distinctions in migration data is between stocks and flows. A migrant stock is a count — how many people are living outside their country of birth at a specific point in time. UN DESA's International Migrant Stock database gives us this figure at five-year intervals; the 2024 edition put it at 304 million. Flow data, by contrast, counts how many people crossed a border in a given period — a month, a year. Flow data is conceptually more dynamic and policy-relevant, but far harder to collect. Most countries record arrivals but not departures. Administrative definitions of what constitutes a migration event — does a one-month stay count? three months? a year? — vary across countries, making international comparisons difficult. A group of researchers led by Guy Abel pioneered a methodology for estimating bilateral flow data from sequential stock figures, and a landmark 2025 paper in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences used privacy-protected Facebook data from three billion users to generate monthly flow estimates for 181 countries, closely matching official statistics. This kind of methodological innovation is exciting, but it also raises questions about data privacy, platform dependency, and representational bias that students should not overlook.

Full slide detail

  • The Brexit referendum (2016): a central claim in the 'Leave' campaign was that net migration to the UK had reached 333,000 per year, far above the government's stated target of 'tens of thousands'; the figure was technically accurate but omitted that the majority of net migration was from non-EU countries that Brexit could not affect.
  • Erokhin, Komendantova, and Albano (2023): analyzed 45,000 tweets about migration during a European political campaign and found that misinformation about migration figures spread faster and further than accurate information — consistent with broader findings from misinformation research.
  • The '2,000 migrants a day' media framing: numerous European media outlets have used daily arrival figures presented without context — without denominator (as a share of population), without comparison to historical flows, or without noting that most arrivals were asylum seekers with legal rights to a determination.
  • Data weaponization: Menjívar, Kraly, and Reed (2024) document how the same migration figures are cited selectively to support opposite conclusions by different political actors — high numbers framing migration as uncontrollable, low numbers framing policy as ineffective; the data itself becomes a political battleground.
  • Pedagogical implication: teaching students to read migration statistics critically — asking who produced the figure, what it measures, what it omits, and how it is being used — is a core analytical competency for any field where migration intersects with public policy.

Instructor notes

This case works extremely well with undergraduate students because Brexit is familiar and the analysis of the statistical claim is accessible without requiring deep quantitative skills. It also opens up an important methodological point: the same number can be both accurate and misleading depending on the context provided (or withheld). Graduate students can engage with Erokhin et al.'s misinformation analysis more formally, examining how information environments shape public responses to migration data.

Narration transcript

Perhaps no better case illustrates the political weaponization of migration statistics than the Brexit referendum. One of the central claims made by the 'Leave' campaign in 2016 was that net migration to the United Kingdom had reached 333,000 people per year — more than three times the governing party's stated target of 'tens of thousands.' The figure was technically accurate: that was what the official statistics showed. But what the campaign did not mention was that the majority of net migration at that time came from non-EU countries — and Brexit would do nothing to affect that. The European Union free movement that was the campaign's stated target accounted for less than half the total. Presenting an accurate number in a context that implies it means something different from what it actually means is a form of statistical manipulation that is common in migration politics, and students need tools to recognize it. Cecilia Menjívar and colleagues documented in their 2024 review of sixty years of migration research how the same statistics are regularly cited selectively to support diametrically opposite conclusions. Teaching students to always ask: who produced this number, what does it actually measure, what does it omit, and what argument is it being used to support — is one of the most transferable analytical skills this course can develop.
