Migration Journeys and Routes

What does the journey actually involve — and what do border policies do to the people who take it?

Module 5 of 14ReflectiveApplied

Narration transcript

Migration is not an event — it is a process, often a dangerous one. Between the decision to leave and the establishment of a new life lies a journey that can take months or years, traverse multiple countries, and involve profound physical, psychological, and economic risks. This module focuses on the journey itself: the routes people travel, the stages of migration, and the human consequences of the border enforcement regimes that increasingly define the migratory experience.

Full slide detail

  • Migration as process: rather than a single act, migration unfolds across stages — pre-departure preparation and decision, the journey itself (which may involve multiple transit countries), arrival and reception, and onward movement or settlement.
  • Mainwaring and Brigden (2016): conceptualize clandestine migration as a series of 'cumulatively dangerous passages' — each stage (crossing a border, transiting a country, reaching a city) involves discrete risks that compound over the journey.
  • Transit migration: many migrants spend significant time — months or years — in transit countries (Libya, Niger, Turkey, Mexico, Morocco) without legal status or access to services, in conditions that may be more dangerous than the origin or even the destination.
  • The role of smugglers: for those without legal pathways, smuggling networks often provide the only available route — creating complex relationships of dependency, exploitation, and debt bondage that persist into the destination country.
  • Journey length and cost: irregular journeys can be both slow and expensive; the journey from West Africa to Europe via Libya and the Mediterranean can take 1 to 3 years and cost $2,000 to $5,000 or more — often financed through family debt.

Narration transcript

Migration is not a moment — it is a process, often an extended and dangerous one. Think of the journey not as a single crossing but as a series of stages, each with its own risks. For a person migrating from sub-Saharan Africa to Europe through the Central Mediterranean route, the journey might begin with an overland crossing through the Sahara, continue through Libya — where migrants have been documented experiencing systematic violence and detention — onto a small rubber dinghy crossing to Italy or Malta, through a reception system, and then onward. Each stage carries distinct risks and requires different resources. Mainwaring and Brigden's 2016 framework calls these 'cumulatively dangerous passages': the dangers do not occur all at once; they accumulate, each crossing leaving physical and psychological marks that affect the capacity to endure what comes next. Understanding migration as a process — with stages, actors, and accumulating consequences — is essential for both analytical rigor and humane pedagogy.

Narration transcript

The world's major irregular migration routes are well-mapped, deeply dangerous, and shaped as much by policy decisions as by geography. The Central Mediterranean route, linking North Africa to Italy, has been the deadliest maritime route in the world for over a decade. IOM's Missing Migrants Project has recorded over 20,000 deaths in this stretch of water since 2014. But these are only the bodies recovered or witnessed — the true death toll is certainly higher. The Darién Gap between Colombia and Panama, once crossed by small numbers of adventurous migrants, became in 2023 one of the world's busiest and most dangerous overland crossings, with approximately 520,000 people traversing 100 kilometers of roadless jungle. The route is used by Venezuelans fleeing economic collapse, Haitians displaced by earthquake and gang violence, Ecuadorians, and many others — including thousands of unaccompanied children. At the US-Mexico border, CBP recorded approximately 2 million encounters in fiscal year 2023, the highest number ever recorded, reflecting a diverse and unprecedented mix of nationalities. These numbers tell us something important: dangerous, costly, and uncertain journeys continue in large volumes because the conditions driving people to take them are more dangerous still.

Full slide detail

  • Guarch-Rubio et al. (2025): studying Syrian and Kurdish women blocked at the Idomeni border camp in Greece, found PTSD rates of 78 to 91 percent — the highest documented rates anywhere in the forced migration literature.
  • The concept of 'torturing environments': sustained exposure to conditions of confinement, uncertainty, violence, and deprivation in transit and reception contexts can itself produce trauma, even in the absence of single traumatic events.
  • Pre-migration trauma: many forced migrants carry trauma from events that caused their displacement (war, persecution, sexual violence) before they ever begin the journey; journey trauma layers on top of this pre-existing vulnerability.
  • Post-migration legal insecurity: the uncertainty of waiting for asylum determination — sometimes for years — is independently associated with anxiety, depression, and PTSD; legal status is a social determinant of mental health.
  • The cumulative model: Arsenijević et al. (2018) propose that for male migrants on the Balkan route, deterrence measures (pushbacks, detention, beatings by border guards) amplify psychological distress beyond what origin-country trauma alone predicts — migration policy choices produce mental health outcomes.

Instructor notes

This is potentially distressing material for students with their own displacement experiences. Announce content before the session; provide space for students to step back if needed; frame the statistics as structural indictments of policy conditions rather than characteristics of individuals. Trauma-informed pedagogy requires acknowledging that students in any migration-focused classroom may have personal experience with these conditions.

Narration transcript

The mental health consequences of forced migration journeys are severe, cumulative, and — this is the important point — substantially produced by policy conditions rather than solely by pre-migration circumstances. Guarch-Rubio and colleagues' 2025 study of Syrian and Kurdish women at the Idomeni border camp in Greece documented PTSD rates between 78 and 91 percent. These women were not in a war zone — they were in a EU member state, blocked at a border that had been closed in defiance of their legal right to seek asylum. The conditions of the camp — overcrowding, cold, violence, uncertainty, separation from family — were themselves traumatizing. This is what researchers call a 'torturing environment': a context that produces trauma through sustained deprivation and indignity rather than a single dramatic event. Arsenijević and colleagues showed that for male migrants on the Balkan route, exposure to pushbacks, detention, and violence by border guards produced psychological distress beyond what pre-migration trauma predicted. These findings are not primarily clinical — they are political. They document that specific border enforcement choices produce specific mental health outcomes. The policy and the suffering are causally connected.

Emphasize the structural dimension: the sexual violence women experience on migration journeys is not random crime but is structurally enabled by the irregularity of the journey, the absence of legal protection in transit zones, and the power dynamics between smugglers and migrants. Narration transcript

Migration journeys are not gender-neutral experiences. Women and girls face a distinct set of risks that are structurally embedded in the irregular migration system. The widespread documentation of sexual violence on the Central Mediterranean route — with MSF and UNHCR fieldwork suggesting over 70 percent of women arriving via Libya experienced sexual violence during the journey — is one of the most disturbing findings in recent migration research. Mary Grace Vella's 2024 analysis shows how the EU-Turkey Deal and subsequent European border externalization agreements, by containing migrants in transit territories without adequate protection, created conditions of heightened vulnerability for women and girls. The logic of externalization — stop people before they reach EU territory — translates directly into trapping women in Libya, in Turkish camps, in Moroccan border zones, where protection systems are inadequate and exploitation is rife. Unaccompanied children — estimated at roughly 20 percent of refugee arrivals to Europe — face their own category of compounded vulnerability: no guardians, high trafficking risk, inadequate institutional responses. These are not unfortunate side effects of migration policy — they are predictable consequences of policy choices that can be evaluated and changed.

Key pointLaFleur et al. (2024): 314 migrant deaths in California's borderlands 2018–2023. Spikes in mortality correlate directly with border wall construction and COVID-era deterrence policies — deterrence kills.

Full slide detail

  • The 'prevention through deterrence' strategy: US Border Patrol formally adopted this in 1994, channeling unauthorized crossings away from urban areas into remote desert terrain; the documented result was not fewer crossings but more deaths in more dangerous areas.
  • LaFleur, On, Ceja, and Martínez (2024): document 314 migrant deaths in southern California over five years (2018–2023), with spikes in mortality linked to border wall construction and COVID-era Title 42 expulsions — demonstrating the lethal consequences of deterrence in a specific corridor.
  • Pushbacks: the practice of forcibly returning asylum seekers across borders without asylum screening, practiced by multiple EU member states (Greece, Croatia, Hungary, Poland) and documented by human rights organizations; in direct violation of non-refoulement obligations under international law.
  • The balloon effect: when one route is closed, flows redirect to another, often more dangerous route; closure of the Strait of Gibraltar pushed flows to the Canary Islands route; closure of Balkan routes pushed flows to the Central Mediterranean.
  • Camp life as journey continuation: Kreichauf (2018) argues that upon arrival in European cities, migrants often enter 'campized' spaces — mass accommodation centers that replicate the temporality, containment, and exclusion of camps — meaning the journey logic continues even after the border is crossed.

Instructor notes

The empirical evidence on deterrence and death is stark and direct. LaFleur et al.'s findings about California borderlands are particularly compelling because they use official county medical examiner data — a relatively hard data source — to connect specific policy changes to specific mortality outcomes. Students skeptical of anecdotal evidence about deterrence may be more persuaded by this mortality data.

Narration transcript

One of the most consequential debates in migration policy is the effectiveness and ethics of deterrence-based border enforcement. The United States formally adopted 'prevention through deterrence' as a strategy in 1994, based on the premise that making unauthorized crossing more difficult and dangerous would reduce the number of people who attempted it. The empirical record over the subsequent three decades is clear: it did not substantially reduce total unauthorized migration. It redirected migration from urban crossing points to remote desert terrain, dramatically increasing mortality. LaFleur and colleagues, using county medical examiner records, documented 314 migrant deaths in southern California over five years from 2018 to 2023, with spikes correlated directly to border wall construction and COVID-era Title 42 expulsions. In Europe, the pattern is the same: every time a route is closed, flows redirect to a more dangerous alternative — from the Strait of Gibraltar to the Canary Islands, from the Aegean to the Central Mediterranean. The balloon effect is well-documented. And Kreichauf's 2018 analysis shows that even arrival does not end the journey logic: in many European cities, mass accommodation facilities replicate the temporality and containment of refugee camps — the migrant who has reached a destination country may still be living in a camp.

Case study

Case: The Idomeni Camp and the EU-Turkey Deal

Key pointThe EU-Turkey Deal reduced crossings by over 90 percent — and created Moria camp, where 13,000 people lived in conditions designed for 3,000, until it burned down in 2020. Both outcomes belong to the same policy.

Full slide detail

  • Idomeni (2015–2016): when European states abruptly closed the Western Balkan route after the March 2016 EU-Turkey Deal, approximately 12,000 migrants and refugees — mostly Syrians and Iraqis — were stranded in an informal camp on the Greek-North Macedonia border in conditions without adequate shelter, sanitation, or medical care.
  • The EU-Turkey Deal (March 2016): Greece agreed to return all irregular arrivals from Turkey; Turkey agreed to accept returnees in exchange for EU funds (6 billion euros), visa liberalization promises, and resettlement pledges from EU member states.
  • PTSD at Idomeni: Guarch-Rubio et al. (2025) directly studied the mental health of women at Idomeni; the conditions documented — isolation, violence, sexual assault, uncertainty — produced the 78–91 percent PTSD rates cited earlier.
  • Human rights critiques: multiple UN agencies, Amnesty International, and Human Rights Watch documented that the Deal violated non-refoulement by returning people to Turkey without individual assessment and that Turkey does not meet the legal standard of 'safe third country' for Syrian refugees.
  • Policy outcome: the Deal significantly reduced Aegean crossings (from approximately 1 million in 2015 to under 100,000 in 2016–2017) while creating a patchwork of overcrowded islands camps (Moria on Lesbos) that became symbols of EU protection failures.

Instructor notes

The EU-Turkey Deal is a rich case for ethical analysis because its proponents can point to real evidence of reduced crossings (lives saved at sea?) while critics can point to real evidence of rights violations and inhumane camp conditions (lives blighted on shore?). Encourage students to hold both sides of the evidence simultaneously rather than reaching for a comfortable single conclusion. Vella's analysis of gendered consequences is a key analytical tool here.

Narration transcript

The EU-Turkey Deal of March 2016 stands as one of the most debated policy interventions in recent European migration history. In exchange for substantial financial payments and political concessions, Turkey agreed to accept the return of migrants and asylum seekers who crossed irregularly from Turkey to Greece. The deal reduced Aegean crossings dramatically — from approximately a million in 2015 to under a hundred thousand in subsequent years. Proponents argue that it saved lives at sea. Critics argue that it outsourced responsibility, violated non-refoulement, and created some of the worst humanitarian conditions seen in Europe since World War II. The Moria camp on Lesbos housed 13,000 people in infrastructure designed for 3,000, before burning to the ground in 2020. The Idomeni case shows what happened to people who were simply stopped: 12,000 human beings stranded in a muddy field on the Greek-North Macedonia border with no clear legal status, no durable solution in sight, and documented rates of psychological trauma that approach the highest levels ever measured. The same policy document produced both outcomes. Teaching this case requires holding both pieces of evidence simultaneously.

Full slide detail

  • The dominant narrative of migration journeys emphasizes victimhood and passivity; while risks are real and severe, migrants are also active agents who make constant calculations, exercise creativity, and demonstrate extraordinary resilience.
  • Information and communication technology: smartphones have transformed migration journeys; migrants use WhatsApp groups, Facebook, Google Maps, and dedicated apps to share route information, identify dangers, coordinate with families, and document abuses.
  • Community formation in transit: migrants stranded in transit cities — Agadez in Niger, Tijuana in Mexico, Istanbul in Turkey — form mutual aid networks, community organizations, and social support structures that provide practical assistance and solidarity.
  • Refugees as active leaders: the Kreichauf (2018) analysis notes that in campized arrival spaces, migrants themselves often organize parallel institutions, create cultural spaces, and advocate for improved conditions — challenging passive recipient narratives.
  • Research ethics: Mainwaring and Brigden (2016) and others argue that researchers must approach lived-experience data with care, ensuring that the act of research does not compound harm, that consent is genuinely informed, and that migrant voices are presented as subjects, not objects.

Instructor notes

Balance in teaching: this module contains severe content about trauma, violence, and death. The resilience dimension is not a 'silver lining' that minimizes the harm but a necessary analytical corrective to the passive-victim frame that dominates media representation. Migrants are active agents throughout their journeys — their agency is constrained by structural forces but not eliminated. The ethics note is crucial for faculty who assign interview-based work.

Narration transcript

Throughout all this documentation of danger and trauma, we must hold onto a crucial analytical point: migrants are not passive victims of forces beyond their control. They are active agents — constantly calculating, adapting, forming communities, and exercising creativity under conditions of extreme constraint. The smartphone has transformed migration journeys in ways that are still being studied: WhatsApp groups share real-time information about route conditions, police locations, and safe houses; Facebook posts connect migrants stranded in transit cities with diaspora communities who can send money; digital documentation creates evidence of abuses that can be reported to human rights organizations. In transit cities across the world — Agadez in Niger, Tijuana in Mexico, Istanbul in Turkey — migrants form mutual aid networks, community organizations, and social structures that provide practical and emotional support. These are not stories of passivity but of profound human resourcefulness under pressure. Holding both the structural analysis of violence and enforcement and the recognition of migrant agency is essential for teaching this material with both accuracy and dignity.

Narration transcript

This module's signature experience asks students to engage with first-person or interview-based material — narrative accounts of migration journeys, or interviews with migrants or those who work with them. This is powerful pedagogical material. It is also ethically complex material, and it is the faculty member's responsibility to prepare students carefully before assigning it. Who has the right to represent whose experience? What does informed consent mean when a migrant is being interviewed by a researcher with institutional power? What is the difference between bearing witness — which generates empathy and analytical understanding — and consuming suffering as dramatic content, which generates sensation without insight? Secondary traumatization is a real risk: students who engage deeply with accounts of violence, trafficking, and detention may experience distress themselves. This is normal, and it should be anticipated. Trauma-informed pedagogy does not mean avoiding difficult material — it means approaching it with care, with preparation, and with space for students to process what they encounter. The ethical preparation is itself part of the learning.

Defining the Migration Journey: Key Concepts

movement through one or more intermediate countries en route to a final destination.

DefinitionSmuggling versus trafficking: a critical distinction — smuggling ends at the destination (the migrant was a client); trafficking involves ongoing control and exploitation (the person is a crime victim). Conflating them produces both analytical and policy errors.

Full slide detail

  • Transit migration: movement through one or more intermediate countries en route to a final destination; transit may be planned (stopping deliberately to earn resources or wait for conditions) or forced (blocked by enforcement and unable to proceed); the distinction matters legally — transit countries bear different protection obligations.
  • Clandestine migration: movement that deliberately evades official border crossing points, visa requirements, or other legal channels; not synonymous with irregular migration (which includes visa overstays and document fraud) but describes the specific spatial dimension of unauthorized crossing that exposes migrants to physical risk.
  • Smuggling vs. trafficking: smuggling involves payment for assistance with illegal border crossing and terminates at the destination (the migrant is a client); trafficking involves deception, coercion, or control and exploitation that continues at the destination (the person is a victim of a crime); conflation of the two categories obscures both the prevalence of consensual smuggling and the distinct severity of trafficking.
  • Route: the specific geographic path of a migration journey; routes are shaped by geography, political conditions, enforcement presence, smuggler networks, and climate; as Mainwaring and Brigden (2016) analyze, routes are not fixed corridors but dynamically negotiated passages that shift with enforcement.
  • Stage migration: a pattern in which migrants move in incremental stages — first within their region, then internationally, then further — rather than in single long-distance moves; understanding stage migration challenges the assumption that all migrants set out for wealthy destination countries from the start.

Instructor notes

The smuggling-trafficking distinction is one that students — and many policymakers — frequently collapse. Making it clearly is important both analytically and practically: it affects how law enforcement should respond (prosecution of the smuggler vs. victim protection for the trafficked person), what data is collected, and what assistance is appropriate. Faculty may want to note that researchers estimate the vast majority of people who pay to be moved across borders are using smugglers in a transactional relationship, not being trafficked — though the two can shade into each other when debt bondage or coercion is involved.

Narration transcript

Before examining specific journey experiences in depth, we need a vocabulary. The concept of transit migration refers to movement through intermediate countries — a migrant may spend months or years in a transit country, working, waiting, or simply unable to proceed. Transit is legally significant: transit countries bear certain protection obligations under refugee law, and the proliferation of bilateral containment arrangements has made transit countries the locus of some of the most acute protection failures in the current system. Clandestine migration refers specifically to border crossings that deliberately evade official channels — physically crossing through remote terrain, crossing at unmonitored points, hiding in vehicles or containers. This is distinct from other forms of irregular migration like visa overstays. Clandestine crossing exposes migrants to the greatest physical risks: heat, drowning, dehydration, and violence from both smugglers and enforcement agents. One of the most important definitional distinctions in this field is between smuggling and trafficking. Smuggling is a commercial transaction: a migrant pays for assistance crossing a border, and the relationship ends on arrival. Trafficking involves deception, coercion, or force — the person is a victim of a crime who continues to be exploited at the destination. These require completely different legal responses: pursuing smugglers as a criminal justice matter, and protecting trafficking victims as crime victims entitled to specific legal protections.

Full slide detail

  • Unaccompanied migrant children: children who travel without a parent or legal guardian; US Customs and Border Protection encountered approximately 130,000 unaccompanied children at the US-Mexico border in FY2023; they are entitled to specific protections under the Trafficking Victims Protection Act and the Flores Settlement Agreement.
  • Developmental and psychological impacts: children who experience traumatic migration journeys — displacement, family separation, violence, detention — show elevated rates of post-traumatic stress, developmental delay, and disrupted attachment; these effects are documented to persist into adulthood and are not eliminated by eventual safety.
  • Documentation and statelessness risk: children born during migration — including on boats, in transit camps, or in countries where jus soli (birthright citizenship) does not apply — face statelessness risk; UNHCR estimates that children account for 40 percent of the forcibly displaced, disproportionate to their share of world population (29 percent).
  • Child trafficking: children traveling alone or with non-family adults face elevated trafficking risk; the IOM and UNICEF have documented networks specifically targeting unaccompanied minors crossing the US-Mexico border and the Mediterranean route for labor and sexual exploitation.
  • Policy tensions: the 'best interests of the child' standard (UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, Article 3) requires that all decisions affecting children in migration contexts prioritize their welfare — this standard often conflicts with enforcement-oriented policies including family detention and expedited removal of families.

Instructor notes

Child-specific vulnerability is an essential sub-topic that is often treated superficially. Faculty teaching this slide should be aware that some students may have personal experience with these issues — creating a sensitive but important discussion environment. The 'best interests of the child' standard provides a legal anchor that allows rigorous critique of specific policies (like the US zero tolerance family separation) in terms that are internationally recognized and cross-ideological.

Narration transcript

Children warrant specific attention in any discussion of migration journeys. According to UNHCR's 2024 Global Trends report, children account for 40 percent of all forcibly displaced people globally — significantly disproportionate to their 29 percent share of world population. Conflict and persecution hit children hardest. Unaccompanied children — those traveling without a parent or guardian — face compound vulnerabilities. US CBP encountered approximately 130,000 unaccompanied children at the southern border in fiscal year 2023 alone. International law, through the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, establishes that in all decisions affecting children, the 'best interests of the child' must be a primary consideration. This standard creates a legal framework for evaluating migration policies as they affect children — including family separation, child detention, and expedited removal — and it frequently points toward policy failures. The psychological research is unambiguous: children who experience traumatic migration, including family separation and detention, suffer developmental, attachment, and mental health consequences that can persist across their lifetimes. The American Pediatric Association, the American Psychiatric Association, and virtually every major mental health body have condemned child immigration detention on developmental grounds.

Full slide detail

  • The IOM Missing Migrants Project documents deaths and disappearances of migrants at international borders worldwide; as of 2024, the project has recorded over 66,000 deaths since 2014 — recognized as a significant undercount because most deaths in remote terrain are never discovered.
  • Mediterranean Sea: the most lethal migration route in the world; IOM has recorded over 30,000 deaths in the Mediterranean since 2014; the route remains active despite massive surveillance and rescue investments because demand for passage has not been eliminated by deterrence.
  • US-Mexico border: LaFleur and colleagues (2024) documented 314 migrant deaths in California's borderlands between 2018 and 2023, finding that spikes in mortality correlated with border wall construction (pushing crossings into more remote, dangerous terrain) and COVID-era Title 42 expulsions (increasing crossing frequency under pressure).
  • The deterrence paradox: the academic evidence consistently shows that border enforcement does not reduce migration but does shift it to more dangerous routes; this is sometimes called the 'funnel effect' — walls and surveillance funnel migrants into terrain where death risk is higher without reducing total crossings.
  • Ethical dimensions: the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals and human rights framework establish that preventable border deaths are not an acceptable cost of migration governance; the principle of non-refoulement implies a corresponding duty of search and rescue that EU member states have been found to have violated by curtailing rescue operations.

Instructor notes

Border death data is empirically important and emotionally powerful. Faculty should approach this slide with care — acknowledging both the gravity of the numbers and the complexity of the policy responses. The deterrence paradox finding is highly policy-relevant and supported by significant empirical evidence: enforcement increases danger without reducing demand. This finding should be presented as an empirical claim supported by research, not as a policy recommendation, allowing students to draw their own policy conclusions.

Narration transcript

The most stark measurement of the human cost of current border regimes is the death toll. The International Organization for Migration has operated the Missing Migrants Project since 2014 — a systematic effort to document deaths and disappearances at international borders. By 2024, the project had recorded over 66,000 deaths globally since 2014, with the explicit acknowledgment that this is a significant undercount: most people who die in remote terrain, at sea, or in transit countries are never found or formally registered. The Mediterranean Sea has become the world's deadliest migration route: over 30,000 people have been documented as having died attempting to cross since 2014, with thousands more each year. The US-Mexico border accounts for thousands more. Marni LaFleur and colleagues' 2024 study documented 314 deaths in California's borderlands alone over five years, finding that spikes in mortality followed the construction of additional border wall sections, which pushed crossings into more remote and dangerous terrain. This is the deterrence paradox: border enforcement does not appear to reduce migration but does appear to increase its lethality, by funneling movement into more dangerous routes. The academic evidence for this mechanism is substantial. Whether a governance system that produces this outcome is morally acceptable is a question that students — and societies — must answer.

Full slide detail

  • Background: the Rohingya are a Muslim ethnic minority in Myanmar's Rakhine State; Myanmar's 1982 Citizenship Law effectively rendered them stateless; they have been subject to decades of systematic discrimination, violence, and — in 2016–17 — what UNHCR and the UN Fact-Finding Mission characterized as genocide.
  • The displacement: since 2017, over 1 million Rohingya have fled to Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar district, creating what is now the world's largest refugee camp complex, housing over 900,000 people; Bangladesh's government has repeatedly refused to allow permanent settlement and has restricted Rohingya rights to work, move, and access education.
  • The Andaman Sea route: tens of thousands of Rohingya have also attempted maritime migration to Malaysia and Thailand via the Andaman Sea; these journeys involve overcrowded wooden vessels, months at sea, extreme mortality risk, and documented cases of boat-pushbacks by Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia — states that refused to allow landing.
  • Regional protection failures: Myanmar, Bangladesh, Malaysia, Thailand, and Indonesia are not parties to the 1951 Refugee Convention; there is no regional refugee protection framework in Southeast Asia; the Rohingya have faced almost total absence of formal legal protection status throughout their displacement.
  • Statelessness as a driver: the Rohingya case illustrates how statelessness — the denial of nationality — produces a particular form of vulnerability in which no state has formal responsibility for a population; Walter Kälin (2021) and others have analyzed how IDPs and stateless persons fall between the gaps of the international protection system.

Instructor notes

The Rohingya case is one of the most important contemporary cases in the course because it illustrates simultaneously: statelessness as a protection gap, the limits of the 1951 Convention system when key countries are not parties, the specific vulnerability of maritime migration routes, the 'campization' phenomenon analyzed by Kreichauf (2018), and the intersection of ethnic persecution with the voluntary-forced continuum. Graduate students can engage with the legal analysis of the pushback practices under the non-refoulement principle even where countries are not Convention parties.

Narration transcript

Let us examine one of the most severe contemporary migration journey crises: the Rohingya displacement from Myanmar. The Rohingya are a Muslim ethnic minority in Rakhine State who were rendered effectively stateless by Myanmar's 1982 Citizenship Law, which recognized only certain ethnic groups as citizens. Decades of systematic discrimination, periodic pogroms, and then the military's 2016-17 campaign — characterized by the UN Fact-Finding Mission as bearing the hallmarks of genocide — drove over a million Rohingya across the border into Bangladesh. They settled in and around Cox's Bazar, which now houses what is widely described as the world's largest refugee camp complex, with over 900,000 people. Bangladesh, which is not a party to the 1951 Refugee Convention, has refused to allow permanent settlement and has restricted Rohingya access to education, formal employment, and freedom of movement. Tens of thousands have attempted the alternative route across the Andaman Sea to Malaysia and Thailand — journeys of extreme danger in overcrowded boats, with documented mass deaths and documented pushbacks by multiple states who refused to allow vessels to land. The Rohingya case is a near-total protection failure: stateless people, displaced from a country committing atrocities against them, in a region with no binding refugee protection framework. It is one of the clearest cases study of the international protection system's structural limits.

Full slide detail

  • Migration is a multi-stage process with distinct risks and dynamics at each stage: pre-departure, transit, arrival, and settlement each require analytical attention.
  • Major irregular routes — Central Mediterranean, Eastern Mediterranean, Darién Gap, US-Mexico border — are sites of significant documented mortality; IOM Missing Migrants Project data shows consistent links between enforcement-driven route changes and increased deaths.
  • Trauma is cumulative and policy-produced: the 78–91 percent PTSD rates at Idomeni (Guarch-Rubio et al., 2025) are not intrinsic to the migrant population — they reflect conditions created by specific policy choices.
  • Gender, age, and sexual orientation shape journey vulnerability in distinct ways; women face systematic sexual violence; unaccompanied children face trafficking risk and institutionalized neglect.
  • Deterrence-based enforcement systematically redirects dangerous routes rather than eliminating them — and produces measurable mortality increases in the redirected segments.
  • Resilience and agency coexist with vulnerability: migrants are active, resourceful actors throughout their journeys, not passive recipients of fate or policy.
  • Ethical engagement with lived experience material requires explicit preparation around consent, positionality, trauma-informed pedagogy, and the distinction between witness and spectacle.

Narration transcript

Module 5 has brought us close to the migration experience at its most immediate and human: the physical journey across terrain and water, through transit zones and border posts, along routes shaped by geography, policy, and the networks of smugglers and human rights violators. We have seen that these journeys are documented killing grounds, that deterrence-based enforcement reliably produces mortality without reducing migration volumes, and that the mental health consequences are severe, cumulative, and substantially policy-produced. We have also insisted that migrants are not passive victims — they are agents who exercise creativity, build community, and demonstrate resilience under extreme pressure. And we have raised the ethical questions that arise when we teach this material: questions about representation, about consent, about trauma, and about the responsibility of those who teach from a position of safety to engage honestly with experiences of danger and displacement. In Module 6, we examine the international legal framework that is supposed to protect people who survive the journey and seek asylum — and ask how well it actually functions.
