Migration Through History

How have historical migration systems — and the norms around them — shaped the present?

Module 4 of 14ConceptualComparative

Narration transcript

Every contemporary migration pattern has a history. The corridors that exist today, the legal frameworks that govern them, and the cultural narratives that surround them were all shaped by centuries of colonial extraction, industrial labor recruitment, forced displacement, and the slow construction of norms around hospitality and protection. This module grounds the course's conceptual foundations in historical time.

Full slide detail

  • Pre-modern mobility: the Silk Road trade networks connected China, Central Asia, Persia, and the Mediterranean from roughly 200 BCE, enabling not just goods exchange but sustained migration of merchants, artisans, religious missionaries, and scholars across thousands of kilometers.
  • African deep history: Rossi (2018) documents that Africa's pre-colonial history is characterized by extensive mobility — transhumance, trade diasporas, and pastoralist networks — contradicting colonial narratives that portrayed Africans as sedentary and immobile.
  • The Bantu expansion: over 2,000 years, Bantu-speaking communities expanded from West-Central Africa across Sub-Saharan Africa, creating one of the most consequential demographic transformations in human pre-history through agriculture-driven migration.
  • Islamic world: the annual Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca, beginning in the 7th century CE, created one of the world's first institutionalized mass mobility systems, generating sustained migration and diaspora communities across North Africa, the Middle East, and South Asia.
  • The pre-modern world was not sedentary: the emergence of the passport, the border, and the 'illegal migrant' are recent inventions (principally post-1880s); mobility was historically governed by different principles than today's nation-state system.

Instructor notes

This slide challenges a common implicit assumption: that migration is a recent phenomenon responding to modern conditions. Opening with deep history helps students understand that the contemporary nation-state border regime is historically exceptional, not the natural order of things. Rossi's Oxford Encyclopedia of African History article is a valuable primary source for challenging Eurocentric migration narratives.

Narration transcript

When we speak of a 'migration crisis,' we implicitly measure against a baseline of stable sedentariness — as if human beings were naturally rooted in place and movement is the exceptional, disruptive state. But the historical record consistently contradicts this assumption. Rossi, in her survey of African migration history published in the Oxford Research Encyclopedia, documents that pre-colonial Africa was characterized by extensive and complex mobility: pastoralist circuits, trade diasporas, seasonal agricultural movement, long-distance merchant networks. This was not crisis migration — it was the normal functioning of pre-modern African society. The same is true across Eurasia: the Silk Road networks, the Islamic world's Hajj mobility, the Bantu expansion across Sub-Saharan Africa over two millennia — these are all forms of sustained human movement that shaped civilizations. The passport, the border patrol, the category of 'illegal migrant' — these are inventions of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Before them, mobility was regulated differently — by guilds, religious communities, tribute systems, and patronage networks. This historical context is essential for students who otherwise assume that today's restrictive border regime is simply 'how things are.'

Full slide detail

  • The transatlantic slave trade (approximately 1500–1900): an estimated 12.5 million enslaved Africans were forcibly transported to the Americas; approximately 10.7 million survived the Middle Passage, constituting the largest forced migration in human history outside of wartime.
  • Slavery as a migration system: the slave trade was organized through European commercial companies, state monopolies, and African intermediary networks; it systematically extracted labor from West and Central Africa to produce sugar, tobacco, and cotton for European markets.
  • Post-slavery indenture: when British slavery was abolished in 1834, British planters recruited approximately 500,000 South Asian indentured laborers to work on Caribbean, African, and Pacific plantations — a 'new system of slavery' that transplanted labor dependency from one racialized group to another.
  • Colonial labor extraction in Africa: European colonial powers implemented head taxes, pass systems, and forced labor regimes that compelled Africans to migrate to mines and plantations — the Witwatersrand gold mines of South Africa are a canonical example.
  • Laio, Davis, Ridolfi, and D'Odorico (2013), analyzing global migration network data, show that destination country choices in contemporary international migration still reflect colonial language ties, religious connections, and former empire membership — the colonial past structurally reproduces current migration patterns.

Instructor notes

This slide requires thoughtful facilitation. Acknowledge that some students may have personal family connections to the histories described. Frame this not as 'distant history' but as the structural foundation of contemporary racial and economic hierarchies that shape migration today. The Laio et al. (2013) finding that colonial ties still structure destination choices is a powerful analytical connection between historical and contemporary patterns.

Narration transcript

Any honest account of migration history must confront the transatlantic slave trade. An estimated 12.5 million enslaved Africans were forcibly transported to the Americas between approximately 1500 and 1900. Roughly 10.7 million survived the Middle Passage. This is not a marginal historical footnote — it is the largest forced migration in human history outside of wartime, and it was foundational to the development of the global economy. The plantation economies it created, the racial ideologies it generated to justify it, and the political economies it established in the Americas are all living legacies that structure contemporary migration in ways that are still underappreciated. When British slavery was formally abolished in 1834, colonial planters simply replaced enslaved African labor with indentured labor from South Asia — an estimated half a million people transported under coercive conditions to the Caribbean, Africa, and the Pacific. And in Africa itself, European colonial powers created tax systems and pass laws that functioned as instruments of forced internal migration, compelling African men to leave their communities and work in colonial extraction industries. Laio and colleagues' network analysis demonstrates that these colonial ties continue to shape migration today: people still move along the channels carved by empire, following colonial language, religious, and economic ties.

Full slide detail

  • The Age of Mass Migration (approximately 1850–1920): an estimated 55 to 60 million Europeans emigrated, primarily to the Americas; this represents the largest voluntary migration in history until the late twentieth century.
  • Key sending countries: Ireland (devastated by the 1845–1852 Great Famine, 1.5 million dead and over 1 million emigrants in the famine years alone), Southern and Eastern Europe (Italy, Poland, Russia, Austria-Hungary), and Scandinavia.
  • Key receiving countries: the United States (over 30 million European immigrants, 1850–1920), Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Australia — nations that constructed national identities explicitly through immigration.
  • The 'new immigration' controversy: by the 1880s–1900s, political resistance to Southern and Eastern European immigrants (Italian, Jewish, Polish) echoed today's debates about 'assimilability' — nativism is not a new phenomenon.
  • The US Immigration Act of 1924 (Johnson-Reed Act): established national origin quotas that severely restricted immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe and effectively barred immigration from Asia — a landmark in the history of racially organized immigration restriction.

Instructor notes

The historical parallels to contemporary debates are the pedagogical gold of this slide. Students whose own ancestors emigrated from Ireland, Italy, or Poland in the late nineteenth or early twentieth century often discover that they have personal connections to the 'huddled masses' narrative that contemporary restrictionist politics would have excluded them from participating in. This is not to make students feel guilty but to make history personally resonant.

Narration transcript

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries witnessed what demographers call the Age of Mass Migration — the largest voluntary population movement in human history to that point. Between roughly 1850 and 1920, an estimated 55 to 60 million Europeans emigrated, most to the Americas. Ireland alone lost approximately one million people to the Great Famine of the 1840s; by 1900, there were more Irish-born people in the United States than in Ireland. Italian, Polish, Jewish, and other Southern and Eastern European immigrants transformed American cities. And they were not always welcomed: the political nativism of the 1890s and 1900s targeted these 'new' immigrants as racially inferior, culturally unassimilable, and threatening to American identity — in language remarkably similar to contemporary restrictionist discourse about Latin American and Middle Eastern immigrants. The US Immigration Act of 1924 codified this restrictionism through a national origin quota system that heavily favored Northern and Western European immigrants and effectively excluded Asians entirely. Understanding this history is essential for contextualizing contemporary immigration debates: the arguments have remained remarkably stable for over a century, even as the target population has changed.

Key pointThe 1951 Refugee Convention was drafted to address European displacement after WWII — not the global forced migration crises of the 21st century. This mismatch is structural, not incidental.

Full slide detail

  • World War II displacement: an estimated 40 to 60 million people were displaced in Europe during and immediately after the war — the immediate stimulus for the International Refugee Organization (1946) and ultimately the 1951 Refugee Convention.
  • The 1951 Refugee Convention: drafted in the aftermath of the Holocaust and WWII displacement, initially limited geographically (Europe) and temporally (events before 1951); the 1967 Protocol removed these limitations, making it universal.
  • Decolonization displacements: independence movements in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East (1940s–1970s) generated massive forced displacement — partition of India and Pakistan (1947, estimated 12–20 million displaced), the Nakba and Palestinian refugee crisis (1948, 700,000–750,000 Palestinians displaced).
  • Post-war labor recruitment: European countries devastated by war actively recruited labor migrants — Germany's Gastarbeiter ('guest worker') system recruited over 2.6 million Turkish, Yugoslav, and Italian workers; France recruited from North Africa; the UK recruited from the West Indies and South Asia.
  • Spiegel and Mhlanga (2022) trace the evolution from post-WWII refugee responses focused on European displacement to today's global system, highlighting how the Ubuntu philosophical tradition of African societies offered an alternative model of solidarity that contrasts with the camp-based confinement that became dominant.

Instructor notes

The point about the Convention's origins is crucial for understanding its limitations: it was designed for a specific historical context and reflects the political priorities of Western states in 1951. The inclusion of the non-refoulement principle was partly about addressing what had been done to Jewish refugees who were turned away from multiple countries before the Holocaust. This historical context should make students more analytically empathetic toward both the Convention's achievements and its gaps.

Narration transcript

The international refugee protection system that governs asylum today was built in the aftermath of World War II — a specific historical response to a specific historical catastrophe. Between 40 and 60 million people were displaced in Europe during and after the war; the Holocaust had revealed what happened when states refused protection to persecuted minorities; and the newly formed United Nations needed an institutional response. The result was the International Refugee Organization in 1946 and, ultimately, the 1951 Refugee Convention — initially limited to European refugees and events before 1951, expanded by the 1967 Protocol to universal application. At the same time, postwar decolonization generated massive displacement events that the Convention was poorly equipped to address: the 1947 partition of India and Pakistan, which displaced between 12 and 20 million people; the 1948 Nakba, which displaced 700,000 to 750,000 Palestinians. And even as the refugee regime was being constructed, European governments were actively recruiting labor migrants from their former colonies and from Turkey and Yugoslavia — guest worker programs that created long-term settlement despite the temporary framing. As Spiegel and Mhlanga document, the camp-based containment model that came to dominate refugee response worldwide was never inevitable — African societies offered Ubuntu-based alternatives of integration and solidarity that were structurally marginalized by the Western-dominated humanitarian system.

Full slide detail

  • The direction of contemporary migration flows is not random: colonial ties in language, legal systems, religion, and economic dependency structure which people migrate where, in ways that persist decades after formal independence.
  • Laio et al. (2013) demonstrate statistically that migration destination choices continue to reflect colonial language ties, religious connections, and former empire membership — the Algerian-French corridor, the Jamaican-British corridor, the Filipino-American corridor are not accidents but legacies.
  • The Windrush generation (UK): Caribbean workers recruited under the 1948 British Nationality Act to fill postwar labor shortages were later treated as illegal immigrants when UK policy shifted — a case study in how colonial labor recruitment creates migration obligations that states subsequently disavow.
  • Frankema and de Haas (2025): studying intra-African mobility between 1850 and 1960, challenge the notion that contemporary African mobility patterns are new — colonial labor extraction systems created migration infrastructures that shaped post-independence mobility.
  • Border regimes created by colonialism: many of the world's most conflict-prone borders were drawn by colonial powers without regard for ethnic, linguistic, or ecological communities — the arbitrary borders of the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, and South Asia continue to generate displacement.

Instructor notes

The Windrush scandal is an excellent case for a class discussion: it illustrates the gap between states' historical willingness to recruit colonial labor and their subsequent disavowal of the obligations that created. Graduate students should connect this to Pacciardi's (2024) analysis of EU border externalization as a continuation of colonial-era migration management — outsourcing border control to African states as Europe outsourced labor recruitment from them.

Narration transcript

Colonial relationships continue to structure contemporary migration patterns with remarkable persistence. This is not a matter of cultural nostalgia — it is an empirical finding. Laio and colleagues' network analysis of global migration patterns from 1960 to 2000 demonstrates that colonial language ties, religious connections, and former empire membership are among the strongest predictors of bilateral migration flows. Algerians migrate to France; Jamaicans to the UK; Filipinos to the United States and to Saudi Arabia through labor contracts structured during the period of American colonial influence. Frankema and de Haas, studying intra-African mobility from 1850 to 1960, show that colonial labor recruitment systems created migration infrastructures — routes, networks, urban destinations — that shaped post-independence patterns of African mobility. And the Windrush scandal offers a poignant case study: British Caribbean workers legally recruited under the 1948 British Nationality Act to fill postwar labor shortages spent decades contributing to British society, only to face deportation threats when the Home Office lost their records. The colonial labor contract was honored when labor was needed; disavowed when the workers grew old.

  3. Ubuntu philosophy (Southern Africa) 'I am because we are' — a communal philosophy that grounds human identity in relationship and obligation to others …
  4. Catholic Social Teaching the four verbs of migration (welcoming, protecting, promoting, integrating) articulated by Pope Francis frame migration …
  5. These traditions do not resolve migration policy debates.

Full slide detail

  • Xenia (ancient Greek): the sacred duty of hospitality to strangers and travelers, embedded in Homeric epic and Greek religious practice — violation of xenia was considered an offense against the gods.
  • Abrahamic traditions: Judaism, Christianity, and Islam all contain explicit commandments regarding care for the stranger and the displaced. Leviticus 19:34 (Hebrew Bible): 'The stranger who resides with you shall be to you as the citizen among you; you shall love the stranger as yourself.' The Quran (4:36) commands kindness to neighbors including the 'companion by your side' and the traveler in need.
  • Ubuntu philosophy (Southern Africa): 'I am because we are' — a communal philosophy that grounds human identity in relationship and obligation to others, offering a non-Western framework for solidarity with displaced persons that Spiegel and Mhlanga (2022) argue was marginalized by Western-dominated humanitarian institutions.
  • Catholic Social Teaching: the four verbs of migration (welcoming, protecting, promoting, integrating) articulated by Pope Francis frame migration as a moral obligation of solidarity rooted in the dignity of every human person.
  • These traditions do not resolve migration policy debates — they provide normative resources that have historically motivated hospitality and continue to animate religious civil society responses to contemporary displacement.

Instructor notes

For secular institutions, frame these traditions as cultural and historical resources rather than religious prescriptions. The point is not to tell students what to believe but to demonstrate that the contemporary tension between hospitality and restriction has deep historical roots across many traditions — and that restrictionism is not a 'natural' or inevitable position. Expert MT Davila (Merrimack College) and Colleen Mitchell (Villanova) bring expertise in religious framings of migration.

Narration transcript

Migration policy debates can sometimes be reduced to a tension between security and control on one side and openness and hospitality on the other. What is often missing from these debates is the recognition that the hospitality tradition has its own long history, rooted in some of humanity's deepest moral and religious frameworks. The ancient Greeks embedded the duty of hospitality to strangers — xenia — in religious obligation and epic literature. The Abrahamic traditions — Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — all contain explicit commandments about care for the stranger, the alien, the displaced. Leviticus commands that the stranger living among you shall be treated as a citizen; the Quran commands kindness to the traveler in need. Ubuntu philosophy in Southern African traditions grounds human identity itself in communal relationship and obligation to others. These are not fringe traditions — they are core to the moral heritage of much of humanity. They do not resolve the specific policy questions about how many people to admit or how to process asylum claims. But they do establish a normative baseline: that the humanity of the migrant is not diminished by their status, and that the capacity to welcome is a moral measure of a community's character.

Key pointThe Global Compacts of 2018 — for migration and for refugees — are deliberately non-binding. States' resistance to legally enforceable international migration obligations is itself a policy choice with humanitarian consequences.

Full slide detail

  • Open migration era (pre-1880s): internal borders of empires were porous; passport requirements for international travel were rare; movement across much of Europe and within empires was relatively unconstrained by documentation.
  • First border regime (1880s–1920s): introduction of passports, national origin restrictions, and health inspections at borders; the US Chinese Exclusion Act (1882) was the first significant racially targeted federal immigration restriction in US history.
  • Post-WWII refugee regime (1940s–1960s): construction of the international protection system alongside guest worker programs; tension between humanitarian obligation and labor market management.
  • The managed migration era (1990s–present): the Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (2018) and the Global Compact on Refugees (2018) represent the most recent multilateral attempt to harmonize migration governance — non-binding, reflecting states' resistance to treaty obligations.
  • Ferris and Kerwin (2023): surveying 10 cases of protracted displacement across three continents, find that the three 'durable solutions' — voluntary return, local integration, and third-country resettlement — all remain largely inaccessible, and that this failure is structural rather than exceptional.

Instructor notes

The evolution from open to managed migration frames the current policy environment as historically constructed, not inevitable. Graduate students should engage with Pacciardi's analysis of border externalization as the latest phase of this evolution — the EU outsourcing border control to African states. This creates a bridge to Module 7's borders and sovereignty content.

Narration transcript

The border regime we live within today is not the natural or inevitable way of organizing human mobility. The systematic use of passports, national origin quotas, and border enforcement is largely a twentieth-century construction. Before the 1880s, movement within empires and across many borders was relatively unconstrained by documentation. The 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act in the United States is often marked as a founding moment of racially organized immigration restriction in democratic politics. The twentieth century then saw waves of restriction — after World War I, after World War II with the refugee regime, through the Cold War, and then the managed migration era of the 1990s and 2000s. The 2018 Global Compacts on Migration and on Refugees represent the most recent multilateral effort to harmonize migration governance. But both are deliberately non-binding: states refused to accept legally enforceable international obligations. Ferris and Kerwin, reviewing 10 cases of protracted displacement, find that all three of the official durable solutions — voluntary return, local integration, resettlement — remain largely inaccessible for the vast majority of displaced people. This is not a series of individual failures. It is a structural condition produced by sovereign states' systematic resistance to taking responsibility for the people their policies and their historical actions have displaced.

Full slide detail

  • Many features of contemporary migration politics have close historical parallels: nativist resistance to Southern European immigrants in 1900s America mirrors contemporary resistance to Latin American and Middle Eastern immigrants; the guest worker 'temporary' framing was applied to Turkish workers in Germany in the 1960s and to Gulf kafala workers today.
  • What has changed: the scale and speed of global communication (migrants can maintain real-time contact with communities of origin); the formalization of international law (states have explicit legal obligations they did not have in 1850); the visibility of migrants via media and social media.
  • What has not changed: the structural role of cheap mobile labor in powering economic growth in receiving countries; the use of migrants as political scapegoats during economic downturns; the gap between states' labor market needs and their political rhetoric on migration.
  • The Pacciardi (2024) analysis of EU border externalization traces its genealogy to post-WWII cooperation frameworks — showing that the current EU Trust Fund for Africa strategy is not new but the latest iteration of decades-long European efforts to prevent African migration.
  • Teaching the parallels carefully: historical analogy is a pedagogical tool, not a claim that history repeats exactly; the specific conditions, populations, and political contexts differ, and those differences matter.

Instructor notes

Historical parallels are powerful but require methodological care. The analogy between early twentieth-century nativism and contemporary restrictionism is illuminating — but students should be cautioned against assuming history simply repeats. The specific populations, legal contexts, and global political environments differ in important ways. The comparative essay signature experience for this module should push students to identify both the parallels and the differences.

Narration transcript

One of the most productive — and pedagogically careful — exercises in migration history is the examination of historical parallels with the present. The arguments made against Italian and Jewish immigrants in early twentieth-century America — that they were too different to assimilate, that they brought crime and disease, that they would undermine the cultural fabric of the nation — are structurally nearly identical to arguments made today about Latin American and Middle Eastern immigrants. The guest worker 'temporary' framing that Germany applied to Turkish workers in the 1960s, telling itself that the workers would return home after their contracts expired, was the same framework applied to Gulf kafala workers today — with the same outcome: long-term settlement without legal pathways to permanent residence. Pacciardi's analysis of the EU Trust Fund for Africa shows that the contemporary strategy of outsourcing border control to African states has genealogical roots in decades of European policy aimed at preventing African migration. History does not repeat exactly. The specific populations, the legal frameworks, and the global political environment have changed in important ways. But the structural dynamics — economic need for cheap mobile labor, political resistance to permanent integration, use of migrants as scapegoats during downturns — these have shown remarkable persistence across a century and more.

Narration transcript

Module 4's signature experience is a comparative essay: a structured academic analysis of one historical migration system and one contemporary migration system, examining what persists across time and what has changed. This exercise builds historical thinking as a research competency — and historical thinking is, in the migration field, profoundly important. The displacement of Ukrainians in 2022 cannot be fully understood without understanding the post-WWII population transfers in Europe. The Northern Triangle migration system cannot be understood without the Bracero Program. The kafala labor system in the Gulf cannot be understood without the history of colonial indenture. Good historical comparison does not claim that history simply repeats. It identifies the structural conditions — labor market dynamics, racial hierarchies, political incentives — that produce similar patterns in different times, while remaining attentive to what is genuinely different: the international legal framework that now exists, the technology of communication and surveillance, the global visibility of migration that social media creates. Graduate students are asked to engage explicitly with how colonial history structures current governance — the Pacciardi reading on border externalization provides one empirical handle.

Definition

Defining Migration History: Key Terms and Concepts

Diaspora
a dispersed population that maintains a shared identity connected to a real or imagined homeland.
Chain migration (network migration)
migration that perpetuates itself through the social networks established by earlier migrants.
Colonial migration
movement of people within or as a consequence of colonial systems.
Protracted displacement
a situation in which refugees have been unable to return home or integrate locally for five or more years.
Durable solutions
the three internationally recognized pathways for resolving displacement.
DefinitionDiaspora, chain migration, protracted displacement, durable solutions — understanding historical migration requires a vocabulary that bridges individual experience and structural processes across time.

Full slide detail

  • Diaspora: a dispersed population that maintains a shared identity connected to a real or imagined homeland; the term originated with the Jewish dispersal from ancient Palestine and has since been applied to Irish, Chinese, Indian, African, and many other globally dispersed communities.
  • Chain migration (network migration): migration that perpetuates itself through the social networks established by earlier migrants — new migrants follow established routes, rely on existing community support, and provide information and resources that enable subsequent migration; network theory (developed by Massey et al., 1993) explains why migration flows often outlast the original push conditions.
  • Colonial migration: movement of people within or as a consequence of colonial systems — including settler colonialism (European populations moving to colonized territories), indentured labor migration (South Asian and Chinese workers to colonial plantations after slavery's abolition), and forced relocation of colonized populations.
  • Protracted displacement: a situation in which refugees have been unable to return home or integrate locally for five or more years; UNHCR defines protracted refugee situations using this criterion; Ferris and Kerwin (2023) identify approximately 43 such situations globally as of 2023.
  • Durable solutions: the three internationally recognized pathways for resolving displacement — voluntary return to the country of origin, local integration in the country of first asylum, and resettlement to a third country; all three have become progressively harder to achieve in the current global political environment.

Instructor notes

This definitional slide serves double duty: it provides vocabulary for understanding historical migration patterns AND introduces terms that will be essential for Modules 5, 6, and 7. Network migration theory (chain migration) is particularly important for explaining why historical migration patterns continue to shape contemporary flows — why, for example, Mexicans overwhelmingly migrate to specific US cities where earlier migrants settled. Graduate students should engage with Massey et al. (1993) on network theory as part of this discussion.

Narration transcript

Before we examine the major historical migration waves, let us establish some vocabulary that will recur throughout the course. The concept of diaspora refers to a dispersed population that maintains a shared identity connected to an original homeland — the Jewish diaspora, the Irish diaspora, the African diaspora. Diasporas are created by historical migration events and continue to shape contemporary migration through the networks and cultural ties they maintain. Chain migration — sometimes called network migration — describes how migration perpetuates itself. Once a community establishes a presence in a destination, it creates information networks, social support systems, and reduced transition costs that enable subsequent migrants to follow. Douglas Massey and colleagues documented this mechanism extensively in their 1993 theoretical review, and it helps explain why migration flows often outlast the original push conditions that created them. Colonial migration is a category that encompasses enormous historical diversity: European settler populations moving to colonized territories; indentured labor systems that moved South Asian and Chinese workers across colonial plantations after formal slavery was abolished; and the forced relocation of colonized populations. Understanding these historical flows is essential because, as we will see, they created migration corridors and diaspora communities that continue to structure contemporary flows.

  • South-South migration in Africa (1850–1960): Frankema and de Haas (2025) document extensive intra-African regional mobility between 1850 and 1960, driven by colonial labor demands, including the movement of millions of workers within colonial economic systems — a dimension of African migration history often absent from Eurocentric accounts.
  • Contemporary scale: in 2024, UN DESA estimated 304 million international migrants — but historical analysis by Laio et al. (2013) shows the global migration network became substantially more interconnected between 1960 and 2000, with colonial histories, language, and religious ties continuing to structure destination choices.

Instructor notes

The scale of the transatlantic slave trade deserves emphasis — it is the largest involuntary migration in human history and the foundation of much of the racial and economic geography of the Americas. Faculty should be prepared for students who either know this history well and engage with it critically, or who encounter it with surprise. The juxtaposition of voluntary and involuntary historical migration is important: the Great Atlantic Migration and the slave trade were happening simultaneously, creating the layered demographic complexity of the nineteenth-century Atlantic world.

Narration transcript

The scale of historical migration is staggering when we engage with the actual numbers. The transatlantic slave trade — the most thoroughly documented forced migration in history — transported approximately 12.5 million enslaved Africans to the Americas between 1500 and 1900, according to the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database maintained by scholars at Emory University and other institutions. About 10.7 million survived the Middle Passage; roughly 1.8 million died during the Atlantic crossing alone. This was not merely a migration event — it was the foundational demographic process that shaped the racial, economic, and cultural geography of the Americas. At roughly the same historical moment, the Great Atlantic Migration was also underway: between 1840 and 1920, approximately 60 million Europeans migrated to the Americas and other destinations. The United States alone received roughly 25 million immigrants between 1880 and 1920. Ireland, convulsed by famine and poverty, lost approximately half its pre-famine population in a single decade. These were not distant historical curiosities — they created the diaspora communities and migration networks that structure contemporary flows. The Mexican-American community in the US Southwest, the Italian-American communities in New York and Buenos Aires, the Irish diaspora across English-speaking countries — all trace their origins to these historical migrations.

  • Discussion prompt: What does the historical record suggest about the relationship between migration restriction policies and actual migration outcomes? Does restriction reduce migration, redirect it, or primarily determine who bears its risks?

Instructor notes

The historical parallels discussion is often the most generative in Module 4. Students who may resist direct engagement with contemporary politics can engage with the same dynamics through historical distance. The key analytical move is to trace the mechanisms — not just the rhetoric. Restriction in the 1880s produced irregular channels; the Bracero Program produced the undocumented population when it ended. These are patterns with contemporary relevance. MT Davila (Merrimack College) and Colleen Mitchell (Villanova) are course experts with deep engagement in the hospitality narrative dimension of this module.

Narration transcript

One of the most intellectually productive exercises in migration history is to examine contemporary migration rhetoric against historical examples. When the Irish arrived in the United States in the 1840s and 1850s, they were described in newspapers and political speeches as racially inferior, prone to crime and drunkenness, carriers of disease, threats to Protestant American culture — Catholic conspirators loyal to a foreign religious authority. The same rhetoric, almost word for word, has been applied to successive waves of migrants: the Chinese in the 1870s and 1880s, Italians and Eastern European Jews at the turn of the twentieth century, Mexicans and Central Americans today. The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 was the first US immigration law to exclude an entire nationality on explicitly racial grounds. It was presented as protecting American workers and culture; it was rooted in racial hierarchy ideology. Formally repealed only in 1943. Historical analysis also illuminates the relationship between managed and irregular migration: the US Bracero Program between 1942 and 1964 admitted approximately 4.6 million Mexican agricultural workers through a formal bilateral agreement. When the program ended in 1964, the labor demand it had served did not disappear — and the migration networks it had created did not dissolve. The result was the emergence of the undocumented Mexican agricultural labor force that has characterized US agriculture ever since. Policy creates consequences that outlast the policy.

Full slide detail

  • Human mobility is ancient and global; the restrictive border regime is a recent invention of the nation-state era, not a natural or inevitable organization of human movement.
  • The transatlantic slave trade — 12.5 million forcibly transported Africans — is the foundational historical context for understanding global migration's racial and economic dimensions today.
  • Colonial relationships in language, religion, economics, and legal systems continue to structure contemporary migration corridors in measurable, empirically documented ways (Laio et al., 2013; Frankema & de Haas, 2025).
  • The 1951 Refugee Convention was built for a specific post-WWII European context; its limits in addressing contemporary climate, gang violence, and South-South displacement are structural, not accidental.
  • Religious and cultural traditions of hospitality — Greek xenia, Abrahamic commands to welcome the stranger, Ubuntu philosophy — provide normative resources that have historically motivated solidarity and continue to animate humanitarian response.
  • Policy evolution toward managed migration and externalization is historically traceable to colonial patterns of labor extraction and containment — understanding this genealogy is essential for evaluating current policies critically.

Narration transcript

Module 4 has given us the long view. We have seen that migration is ancient, that it has been organized in radically different ways across historical periods, and that the current border regime is historically recent and contingent. We have confronted the foundational violence of the transatlantic slave trade and colonial forced labor as the structural context for global migration's racial dimensions today. We have seen how colonial relationships continue to channel contemporary migration flows decades after formal decolonization. We have traced the construction of the international refugee protection system from its post-WWII origins to its current limitations. And we have recovered religious and cultural traditions of hospitality — from ancient Greek xenia to Ubuntu philosophy — that offer normative resources for teaching migration with ethical depth. In the next module, we zoom in from this broad historical panorama to the human scale of the migration journey itself: the routes, the dangers, the trauma, and the profound resilience of people on the move.
