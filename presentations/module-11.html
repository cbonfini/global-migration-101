Religion, Ethics, and Migration

Narration transcript

The ethics of migration is not a peripheral concern in academic or policy life — it sits at the center of some of the most heated debates in democratic societies. This module examines the ethical frameworks that inform migration debates, from the philosophical traditions of cosmopolitanism and communitarianism, to the rich religious traditions of hospitality and care for the stranger. We look at Catholic social teaching, Islamic and Jewish perspectives, faith-based organizations in practice, and the contested ethics of open borders.

Full slide detail

  • Migration policy is fundamentally normative: every border policy, asylum system, and integration program embeds assumptions about who matters morally, what obligations states have, and where the limits of political community lie.
  • Descriptive vs. normative analysis: empirical migration studies describe what happens and why; ethical analysis asks what should happen and on what grounds — both are necessary for faculty teaching in professional and policy contexts.
  • The stakes are human: migration policy determines who lives and dies, who is separated from family, who gains opportunity, and who is expelled. These are not merely administrative decisions — they are moral choices.
  • Contested moral terrain: migration ethics spans some of the deepest disagreements in political philosophy — between cosmopolitanism and nationalism, between rights-based and communitarian approaches, between universalism and particularism.
  • Faculty role: presenting multiple ethical frameworks fairly, without imposing a single conclusion, while maintaining scholarly rigor and genuine engagement with the arguments — this is the intellectual challenge.

Instructor notes

Open by establishing why ethics belongs in a social science course on migration. Some faculty from quantitative or legal backgrounds may be skeptical of normative analysis. Make the case that all policy analysis involves normative premises — the question is whether those premises are examined or hidden. This module uses the Villanova / Mother Cabrini Institute context naturally — the initiative is explicitly grounded in ethical and faith commitments, so ethics is not an imposition on the course but one of its animating questions.

Narration transcript

Migration policy cannot be ethically neutral, even when it presents itself as technical or administrative. Every decision about who may enter, who may stay, who receives protection, and who is expelled encodes a set of moral judgments: about the relative weight of national sovereignty versus individual human rights; about who counts as a member of the moral community; about what obligations we have to people who are not our fellow citizens. These judgments are often not made explicit in policy debates, and one of the core tasks of an ethical approach to migration is to surface them. Empirical migration research tells us how many people are moving, why they are moving, and what happens when they arrive.

Key pointCarens (2013): 'Borders are the new feudalism' — birthplace determines life chances more powerfully than any other single factor, yet we treat this as morally natural.

Full slide detail

  • Cosmopolitan open borders position (Carens, 1987; 2013): national borders are morally arbitrary — like feudal birth privileges, they determine life chances without moral justification. From behind a Rawlsian 'veil of ignorance,' free movement would be a basic human right.
  • State sovereignty / communitarian response (Walzer, 1983; Miller, 2016): democratic political communities have legitimate interests in controlling membership; cultural continuity, welfare state sustainability, and democratic self-determination justify immigration restrictions.
  • Rainer Bauböck's mid-level approach (2025, Critical Review of International Social and Political Philosophy): rejects ideal-theory open borders advocacy; argues for promoting expanded international free movement through pragmatic reform of liberal democratic institutions rather than demanding full open borders.
  • Libertarian open borders: some conservatives argue borders restrict free markets and individual liberty — 'there ought to be no borders for goods and capital, so why should there be borders for people?'
  • Key contested questions: Do wealthy countries that contributed to emigration pressures (through colonial history, climate change, military intervention) have special obligations to those displaced as a result? Does affluence create obligation?

Instructor notes

The open borders debate is one of the most intellectually rich debates in political philosophy and connects to students' intuitions about fairness, national identity, and global justice. Present Carens's position fairly and rigorously — it is often caricatured. Bauböck's 2025 mid-level critique provides a sophisticated response that doesn't simply reject open borders but argues for a different political strategy. The libertarian open borders argument (from the Right) is often surprising to students and worth introducing to complicate the political mapping.

Narration transcript

Few questions in political philosophy are more fundamental than: do national governments have the moral right to exclude foreigners? Joseph Carens, in his 1987 article and his 2013 book The Ethics of Immigration, argued that they do not. Carens drew on Rawlsian political philosophy: if we imagined designing a just global social order from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which country we would be born in, we would almost certainly not design a world in which birthplace determines access to opportunity, safety, and rights as dramatically as our actual world does. Borders, Carens argued, are the new feudalism: they lock in privilege based on the accident of birth just as surely as inherited aristocratic rank did in medieval Europe.

DefinitionNon-refoulement: the most fundamental norm in international refugee law — widely regarded as absolute, but states increasingly use legal fictions to circumvent it while technically complying. Understanding the principle is essential for evaluating these evasions.

Full slide detail

  • Cosmopolitanism: the philosophical position that all human beings belong to a single moral community, with obligations that transcend national borders — in migration ethics, supports open borders or at minimum strong obligations toward migrants regardless of nationality.
  • Communitarianism: the position that political communities — nations, states — have genuine moral significance and legitimate interests in self-determination, including control over membership — provides philosophical grounds for immigration restriction.
  • Non-refoulement: the core principle of international refugee law (Article 33 of the 1951 Convention) that prohibits states from returning a refugee to a country where they face serious danger — widely considered jus cogens (a peremptory norm from which no derogation is permitted).
  • Hospitality (xenia in Greek; diyafa in Arabic; hachnasat orchim in Hebrew): the ethical and cultural obligation to welcome and care for the stranger or guest — foundational in multiple religious and philosophical traditions; Derrida's 'unconditional hospitality' is the philosophical extreme.
  • The right not to migrate: the principle that people should have the material, legal, and political conditions necessary to build dignified lives in their places of origin — affirmed in Catholic social teaching and progressive migration justice frameworks as the counterpart to the right to migrate.

Instructor notes

The definitional clarity on cosmopolitanism vs. communitarianism is important for helping students understand that the open-borders debate is not simply progressive vs. conservative — it maps onto deep philosophical traditions in political theory. Derrida's 'unconditional hospitality' is philosophically important but impractical as a policy guide; its value is as a moral limit-case that reveals what is at stake. The right not to migrate is often omitted from migration ethics courses but is central to the Villanova/Mother Cabrini Institute framing.

Narration transcript

Clear conceptual foundations are essential before engaging the substantive debates in migration ethics. Cosmopolitanism, in its migration applications, holds that national borders have limited moral weight: all human beings belong to a single moral community, and obligations do not stop at political borders. Joseph Carens is the most influential contemporary proponent. Communitarianism, developed by Michael Walzer, Alasdair MacIntyre, and others, argues that political communities are not morally arbitrary — they embody shared histories, traditions, and conceptions of the good that give them genuine moral standing and legitimate interests in self-determination, including membership control. Non-refoulement — from the French 'refouler,' to push back — is the foundational principle of international refugee law: no state may return a refugee to a country where they face a serious danger of persecution or death.

Key pointPope Francis's four verbs: Welcome — Protect — Promote — Integrate. A practical ethical checklist for institutions responding to migration.

Full slide detail

  • Catholic social teaching (CST) on migration rests on several foundational principles: human dignity (every person has inherent worth and rights regardless of status); subsidiarity (action should occur at the most local effective level); solidarity (we are all responsible for one another); and the universal destination of goods (the earth's resources belong to all humanity).
  • The right to migrate AND the right not to migrate: CST affirms both — people have a right to seek better lives elsewhere, and a right to the conditions in their home communities that would make migration unnecessary. This framing refuses to treat migration solely as a crisis.
  • Kerwin (2024, Journal of Law and Religion): documents how US faith-based organizations grounded in Catholic and other Christian traditions have provided the majority of resettlement, legal, and social services to migrants and refugees — and face growing political challenges.
  • Pope Francis and Laudato Si'/Laudate Deum: connect climate change, environmental destruction, and forced migration — positioning climate displacement as a moral issue rooted in the same injustice as economic inequality.
  • The four-verb framework (Pope Francis): welcome, protect, promote, integrate — often cited as a practical ethical guide for institutional responses to migration at every level from parishes to national governments.

Instructor notes

This framework is central to the Villanova / Mother Cabrini Institute context and should be given careful attention. Emphasize that CST on migration is not exclusively Catholic in its appeal — the principles of human dignity, solidarity, and the universal destination of goods resonate across traditions. Kerwin's 2024 article on faith-based organizations documents a practical dimension often invisible in policy analysis: who actually delivers migrant services? In the US, it is predominantly faith-based organizations.

Narration transcript

Catholic social teaching has developed one of the most coherent and influential ethical frameworks for migration. Its foundations lie in a cluster of principles: human dignity, which holds that every person possesses inherent worth and rights that no state action can remove; solidarity, which frames humanity as a single interdependent community with shared responsibilities; subsidiarity, which holds that decisions should be made at the most local effective level; and the universal destination of goods, which teaches that the earth's resources are intended for all of humanity, not merely for those who happen to have been born within a particular nation's borders. What is distinctive about the CST framework is its insistence on both the right to migrate and the right not to migrate.

Full slide detail

  • The Hebrew Bible / Torah: the command to welcome the 'ger' (stranger / resident alien) appears 36 times — more frequently than almost any other ethical command — rooted in the memory of Israel's own experience as strangers in Egypt.
  • Islam and the concept of 'hijra' (migration): the Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina is one of Islam's foundational events; Islamic jurisprudence includes strong obligations of hospitality ('diyafa') and protection of the traveler.
  • Braginskaia (2023, Journal of Church and State): compares how Muslim and Jewish communities in Britain interpret and practice hospitality toward migrants — finding both theological grounding and secular humanitarian motivations, with some tensions over which newcomers are prioritized.
  • Kulska (2020, Religions): argues that religious non-state actors are 'trend-setters' on migration ethics, mediating between security-driven state policies and moral obligations — religious organizations often hold more welcoming positions than their state governments.
  • Cross-religious convergences: multiple traditions share themes of the stranger as sacred guest, the moral test presented by the vulnerable other, and obligations that transcend political membership — providing grounds for inter-faith collaboration on migration.

Instructor notes

Presenting multiple religious traditions fairly and accurately requires care. The 36-times figure for the ger in the Torah is verifiable and striking. The Islamic hijra framing is important — migration is embedded in Islam's founding narrative in a way that creates genuine theological resources for welcoming migrants. Braginskaia's comparative study of Muslim and Jewish communities in Britain is valuable for showing that theological hospitality is enacted differently across communities and contexts, with real internal debates.

Narration transcript

The world's major religious traditions contain deep resources for migration ethics — resources that predate and in many ways transcend the contemporary political debate. In the Hebrew Bible, the obligation to protect the 'ger' — variously translated as stranger, sojourner, or resident alien — appears approximately 36 times, making it one of the most frequently reiterated ethical commands in the entire Torah. The command is grounded in historical memory: 'you shall not oppress a stranger, for you know the heart of the stranger, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt.' In Islam, migration is not merely a policy question but a theological event: the Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina — the hijra — is so foundational that the Islamic calendar begins with it.

Full slide detail

  • Faith-based organizations (FBOs) provide a substantial share of refugee resettlement, immigration legal services, emergency housing, food assistance, healthcare, and social integration support worldwide — often filling gaps left by state withdrawal from social services.
  • Kerwin (2024): US faith-based organizations — Catholic Charities USA, LIRS, Church World Service, HIAS, IRC (originally Christian) — collectively resettled tens of thousands of refugees annually, provided legal representation to hundreds of thousands, and operated social service networks reaching millions.
  • Political challenges: US faith-based organizations have faced increasing political pressure when their work conflicts with federal immigration enforcement priorities — including conflict over sanctuary provision, asylum legal aid, and provision of services to undocumented people.
  • Global FBO landscape: CARITAS Internationalis, the Islamic Relief network, World Vision, and many smaller national organizations operate globally — providing humanitarian response at borders, in camps, and in urban contexts, often with more access than government actors.

Instructor notes

This slide makes the ethical discussion concrete: faith-based organizations are not just talking about migration ethics, they are the primary delivery system for migration services in many contexts. Kerwin's 2024 article provides specific empirical grounding. The tensions within faith communities are important to acknowledge — faculty may have students with conservative religious backgrounds who feel their own traditions are being misrepresented. Honor the internal debates while presenting the weight of textual and theological evidence for hospitality traditions.

Narration transcript

Faith-based organizations are not peripheral players in the migration support ecosystem — they are, in many contexts, the central delivery system. In the United States, the federal refugee resettlement system is operated primarily through contracts with faith-based organizations: Catholic Charities USA, Lutheran Immigration and Refugee Service, Church World Service, HIAS — which was founded in 1881 as the Hebrew Immigrant Aid Society and now serves refugees of all faiths — and others collectively resettle tens of thousands of refugees annually, operate immigration legal service programs, and provide social services to millions of migrants and refugees.

Narration transcript

The claim that religious traditions contain resources for welcoming migrants is a normative argument — it says what religious traditions teach. But a separate empirical question asks whether invoking religious obligations actually changes people's attitudes toward refugees in practice. Sam Selsky's 2024 study in the Journal of International Migration and Integration addressed exactly this question in one of the world's most politically complex host contexts: Lebanon, a country that hosts the highest number of refugees per capita in the world, primarily Syrians, and where anti-Syrian sentiment is high among some communities. Selsky's survey experiment tested whether messages invoking sacred texts and religious obligations toward refugees shifted attitudes toward refugee inclusion among Sunni Muslim, Shia Muslim, and Christian respondents — particularly among those with high baseline hostility.

Key pointThe 'bridge or barrier' question has no universal answer — religion's effect on integration is highly context-dependent, mediated by host society attitudes toward religion, the specific faith community, and the dimensions of integration being measured.

Full slide detail

  • Fleischmann (2022, Zeitschrift für Religion, Gesellschaft und Politik): reviews 20 years of European survey research on immigrant religiosity and its relationship to integration — the research has expanded enormously since 9/11 but produced inconclusive findings.
  • The 'bridge or barrier' question: does immigrant religiosity help or hinder integration into secular European host societies? The answer is context-dependent: in some settings, religious community provides social capital; in others, it can reinforce segregation.
  • Muslim immigrant religiosity in Europe has been particularly scrutinized under post-9/11 securitization — producing a research literature that Fleischmann argues has been shaped by the political context in ways that compromise scientific neutrality.
  • Religious community as social capital: immigrant religious communities often provide practical support networks — job referrals, childcare, housing leads, emotional support — that facilitate integration independent of the religious content per se.
  • The secularization gap: highly secular European host societies may misread high immigrant religiosity as a sign of incompatibility, when religiosity among immigrants reflects broadly global patterns rather than specific migration or integration attitudes.

Instructor notes

Fleischmann's 2022 review is valuable for faculty who teach in European contexts where Muslim immigration and integration is politically charged. The key finding — that 20 years of research has produced inconclusive results — is itself significant: it means the politically prevalent assumption that Muslim religiosity is incompatible with European integration is not empirically supported by the research literature. The social capital dimension is important for balance: religious community provides genuine practical integration support regardless of theological content.

Narration transcript

The relationship between immigrant religiosity and integration has been one of the most intensely studied questions in European migration research since the early 2000s — particularly in the context of Muslim immigration. Fenella Fleischmann's 2022 review in Zeitschrift für Religion, Gesellschaft und Politik surveys two decades of this research and reaches a striking conclusion: despite the enormous volume of study, the findings are fundamentally inconclusive. Sometimes immigrant religiosity facilitates integration by providing social networks, community support, shared identity anchors, and practical resources. Sometimes it appears to reinforce separation from host-society institutions and norms. The difference depends enormously on the specific religious community, the host-society context, the dimensions of integration being measured, and the methodology used.

Ethical Frameworks Beyond Religion: Rights, Justice, and Obligation

  1. Human rights approach migrants have inalienable rights by virtue of their humanity, not their citizenship.

  4. Duty of rescue vs. duty to admit: even philosophers skeptical of open borders typically recognize a duty of rescue for those whose lives are at immediate risk.
  5. Bauböck's mid-level approach (2025) rather than advocating ideal-theory open borders or accepting current restrictions, focus on incremental expansion of …
Key pointThe capabilities approach asks not 'do migrants have formal rights on paper?' but 'can they actually live a life of dignity?' — a more demanding ethical test that many migration systems fail.

Full slide detail

  • Human rights approach: migrants have inalienable rights by virtue of their humanity, not their citizenship — grounded in the 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights and subsequent instruments; imposes obligations on all states regardless of treaty commitments.
  • Capabilities approach (Sen; Nussbaum): the ethical test is not whether formal rights exist on paper but whether people have the substantive capabilities to live a life of dignity — migration may be necessary for capability realization when origin societies cannot provide it.
  • Global justice / distributive justice: the global distribution of life chances is profoundly unjust; wealthy countries that benefit from the global economic system that produces migration pressures bear special obligations to those displaced by it.
  • Duty of rescue vs. duty to admit: even philosophers skeptical of open borders typically recognize a duty of rescue for those whose lives are at immediate risk — a minimalist but practically significant norm.
  • Bauböck's mid-level approach (2025): rather than advocating ideal-theory open borders or accepting current restrictions, focus on incremental expansion of free movement rights within and across liberal democracies — a pragmatic strategy for genuine progress.

Instructor notes

The capabilities approach (Sen and Nussbaum) is a powerful alternative to rights-based frameworks for migration ethics because it focuses on substantive freedom rather than formal entitlement. Bauböck's mid-level argument is the most sophisticated recent response to the Carens open borders position and is worth reading carefully with graduate students. The duty-of-rescue point establishes common ground across philosophical positions that is practically significant for policy.

Narration transcript

Religious frameworks provide some ethical resources for migration, but secular philosophical traditions offer others. The human rights approach, grounded in the 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights, argues that migrants have inalienable rights by virtue of their humanity — rights that do not depend on citizenship and that states are obligated to respect regardless of their domestic law or international treaty commitments. This framework has driven many of the most significant developments in international refugee and migration law. Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum's capabilities approach provides a different but complementary lens: rather than asking whether formal rights exist on paper, it asks whether people have the substantive capabilities — the real, effective freedoms — to live a life of dignity.

Debate

Ethical Dilemmas in Migration Policy

  1. The triage dilemma when states cannot admit all who need protection, how should selection be made?
  2. Ethical recruitment in healthcare wealthy countries recruiting nurses and doctors from countries with critical health workforce shortages.
  3. The 'safe third country' rule requiring asylum seekers to claim protection in the first safe country they reach may be legal but is ethically …
  4. Family separation and deterrence policies using family separation as a deliberate deterrence mechanism.
  5. The ethics of returns when is it ethically permissible to return people to their origin countries?
Key pointThe hardest ethical questions in migration policy are not between good and evil — they are between competing goods and competing rights. Ethical analysis must acknowledge this complexity honestly.

Full slide detail

  • The triage dilemma: when states cannot admit all who need protection, how should selection be made? By risk level? By geographic proximity? By historical ties? Each principle privileges different populations and reflects different underlying moral theories.
  • Ethical recruitment in healthcare: wealthy countries recruiting nurses and doctors from countries with critical health workforce shortages — is this mutually beneficial labor mobility or ethically problematic extraction of human capital?
  • The 'safe third country' rule: requiring asylum seekers to claim protection in the first safe country they reach may be legal but is ethically contested — it shifts protection burdens to countries geographically proximate to crisis zones, most of which are lower-income.
  • Family separation and deterrence policies: using family separation as a deliberate deterrence mechanism — as was done at the US-Mexico border — is widely condemned by ethicists, child development scholars, and human rights bodies as a form of torture and a human rights violation.
  • The ethics of returns: when is it ethically permissible to return people to their origin countries? What conditions must be met? The tension between states' interest in enforcement and individuals' safety is one of the most contested in migration ethics.

Instructor notes

This slide introduces ethical dilemmas that have no clean answers but that students need to learn to reason through. The healthcare recruitment case has a scholarly literature and real policy implications. The family separation case is clear-cut from human rights and child development perspectives but politically contested — present the expert consensus clearly. The safe third country rule has been challenged in multiple court systems and is actively contested in international refugee law.

Narration transcript

The most challenging dimension of migration ethics is not distinguishing between clear goods and clear evils — it is navigating genuine moral dilemmas where important values and rights are in tension. Consider the selection problem: when a country cannot admit everyone who needs protection, by what principle should it select? Should priority go to those facing the greatest risk of death? To those with existing family ties in the country? To those who come from countries with historical ties linked to colonialism or military intervention? Each of these principles can be defended — and each implies a different underlying moral theory about obligation, membership, and justice. The healthcare recruitment dilemma presents a conflict between individual freedom of movement, which is a basic right, and collective obligations that origin countries may have claims to enforce.

The Abolition Framework and Migration Justice

  1. Migration justice frameworks (beyond conventional human rights) argue that current border enforcement systems cause systematic harm to racialized populations globally, and that …
  2. No Borders movement radical position that national borders themselves are instruments of racial capitalism and colonial domination, and …
  3. Prison/immigration detention abolition overlap scholars like Ruth Wilson Gilmore and movement organizations link immigration detention to carceral systems.
  4. Critique of reform migration justice advocates argue that incremental reform often legitimizes and extends the system.
  5. Limits and tensions abolition frameworks are morally provocative and intellectually important, but states still face practical governance …
Key pointThe migration justice/abolition framework asks: if we ended mass incarceration because prisons caused systematic harm to Black communities, should we apply the same logic to immigration detention?

Full slide detail

  • Migration justice frameworks (beyond conventional human rights): argue that current border enforcement systems cause systematic harm to racialized populations globally, and that incremental reform is insufficient — abolition of enforcement practices or immigration detention is necessary.
  • No Borders movement: radical position that national borders themselves are instruments of racial capitalism and colonial domination, and that the just response is to abolish border enforcement rather than reform it.
  • Prison/immigration detention abolition overlap: scholars like Ruth Wilson Gilmore and movement organizations link immigration detention to carceral systems — arguing that detention expansion mirrors prison expansion and must be analyzed and resisted as part of the same structure.
  • Critique of reform: migration justice advocates argue that incremental reform often legitimizes and extends the system — new 'humane' detention facilities, reformed deportation procedures — without addressing the fundamental violence of exclusion.
  • Limits and tensions: abolition frameworks are morally provocative and intellectually important, but states still face practical governance questions about undocumented populations, criminal conduct, and border management that require policy responses even in the most progressive contexts.

Instructor notes

This is explicitly flagged in the course as an 'alternative lens' and should be taught as such — it is an important critical perspective, not the consensus position of the field. Present it rigorously and fairly without either dismissing it or adopting it as the course's own normative position. The connection between immigration detention and prison abolition scholarship is intellectually serious and deserves engagement. Faculty should be prepared for this to provoke strong reactions in both directions.

Narration transcript

The frameworks we have examined so far — human rights law, Catholic social teaching, open borders philosophy, capabilities theory — all operate broadly within an assumption that the state and its border architecture is the legitimate starting point for ethical analysis: the question is what limits states should observe, not whether states should control borders at all. The abolition framework challenges this assumption directly. Drawing on the tradition of prison abolition scholarship — particularly the work of Ruth Wilson Gilmore — migration justice advocates argue that immigration enforcement and detention systems are not reformable institutions that merely need better oversight. They are, on this view, instruments of racial capitalism and colonial domination that cause systematic harm to racialized global populations.

Ask the roomThe signature experience: present and defend an ethical analysis of a specific migration policy dilemma, applying at least two distinct ethical frameworks explicitly.

Full slide detail

  • Discussion prompt 1: Bauböck argues against ideal-theory open borders advocacy and for pragmatic mid-level reform. Is pragmatism in ethics a principled position or a concession to injustice? What would you gain and lose by arguing for open borders vs. arguing for incremental reform?
  • Discussion prompt 2: Multiple religious traditions command hospitality to the stranger — yet faith communities are internally divided on migration policy. How should faculty navigate teaching religious ethics to religiously diverse student bodies?
  • Discussion prompt 3: Select one specific migration policy — a safe third country rule, a family separation policy, an immigration detention system — and conduct a brief ethical analysis using two different frameworks (rights-based + capabilities, or CST + cosmopolitan). Do the frameworks converge or diverge in their verdicts?
  • Discussion prompt 4: Selsky's Lebanon experiment suggests religious framing can reduce anti-refugee sentiment. Is it ethical to use religiously-framed messaging to change people's attitudes on political questions — or does this cross a boundary between persuasion and manipulation?

Instructor notes

These prompts are designed to push students beyond 'I agree with the general principle' to 'here is how I reason from principle to specific policy judgment.' Prompt 3 in particular is the template for the module's signature experience. The final prompt about the ethics of religious persuasion is philosophically interesting and connects to broader debates about epistemic autonomy and the ethics of political communication.

Narration transcript

Ethics in the migration classroom works best when it moves from abstract principle to concrete judgment. Bauböck's pragmatism challenge asks students to think about the relationship between moral ideals and political strategy — a question that arises in every applied ethical domain. The religious diversity question is practically important for faculty: how do you teach a tradition's hospitality ethic to students who come from that tradition and find it inspiring, alongside students who are skeptical of religious arguments, alongside students who come from denominations with different interpretations? The policy analysis exercise — applying two frameworks to one specific policy — is the most powerful pedagogical tool in the ethics toolkit.

Full slide detail

  • Migration ethics is not peripheral — it is embedded in every migration policy decision; surfaces the normative premises that empirical analysis often leaves unexamined.
  • Multiple ethical frameworks — human rights, CST, cosmopolitanism, capabilities, communitarianism — reach different conclusions from different foundational premises; students must understand and apply these frameworks, not merely choose one.
  • The world's major religious traditions contain powerful resources for welcoming migrants, grounded in shared themes of the stranger's sanctity and the moral test of care; but faith communities are internally diverse and these traditions are contested.
  • Faith-based organizations are the primary delivery system for migrant services in many contexts — not a niche supplement to state provision but a structural feature of humanitarian response.
  • The hardest migration ethics questions are genuine dilemmas between competing goods — triage selection, healthcare recruitment, safe third country — that require careful reasoning rather than ideological assertion.

Instructor notes

Close by returning to the human dignity standard — which is both CST-grounded and philosophically defensible across traditions. Note that the next module (media and narratives) will examine how ethical language and moral framing are used in public debate — connecting the philosophical analysis here to the rhetorical and political analysis that follows.

Narration transcript

This module has ranged across some of the most challenging terrain in ethical and political philosophy. We have seen that every migration policy decision embeds moral premises — about who matters, what states owe, and what human dignity requires — that deserve explicit analysis. We have examined multiple frameworks: the open borders cosmopolitanism of Carens, the communitarian response of Walzer and Miller, Bauböck's pragmatic mid-level alternative, the Catholic social teaching tradition with its dual commitment to the right to migrate and the right not to migrate, the hospitality traditions found across Jewish, Islamic, and Christian texts, the capabilities approach of Sen and Nussbaum, and the migration justice/abolition framework. These frameworks do not always agree — and faculty must be able to present their genuine disagreements honestly rather than smoothing them over.
