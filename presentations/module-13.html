Climate Change and Future Displacement

How is climate change reshaping who moves, where, and why — and what law and policy are equipped to respond?

Narration transcript

Climate change is already displacing people — and the scale will almost certainly grow. But the relationship between climate and migration is more complex than 'climate disaster causes flight.' This module examines how slow-onset and rapid-onset climate events drive displacement, why the international legal framework has no specific protection for climate-displaced people, and what a just and evidence-based policy response would look like.

Full slide detail

  • Climate change is a migration driver through multiple pathways: extreme weather events (floods, hurricanes, wildfires) that destroy homes and livelihoods; slow-onset changes (sea-level rise, desertification, salinization, glacial retreat) that erode agricultural capacity over years; and temperature increases that make areas uninhabitable or economically unviable.
  • The relationship is not simple or direct: climate rarely causes migration alone — it interacts with poverty, governance quality, conflict, and existing social networks to produce or prevent migration; not everyone exposed to climate change migrates.
  • Internal vs. international climate migration: most climate-related migration is internal — within national borders — not international; this has important implications for the legal framework (which addresses international refugees) and for data collection.
  • Mugambiwa and Sibanda (2025, International Journal of Politics and Security): argue for a risk management and climate justice framework embedded in sustainable development — climate displacement must be addressed within broader development frameworks, not managed separately.
  • The interaction with economic migration: climate-induced livelihood loss often causes migration that looks like 'economic migration' — further blurring the climate/economic/political migration distinction that legal systems try to enforce.

Instructor notes

The complexity point at the top is essential: students often arrive with an oversimplified direct-causation model. Help them see that climate effects on migration are mediated by adaptive capacity, social networks, governance, and economic resources. The internal vs. international distinction is critically important for the legal discussion that follows — most climate displacement does not cross international borders and thus falls outside the 1951 Refugee Convention framework. The interaction with economic migration is important for critiquing oversimplified category distinctions.

Narration transcript

Climate change is increasingly recognized as one of the most significant drivers of human displacement in the 21st century. But the relationship between climate change and migration is not simple or direct, and oversimplified framings — 'climate refugees flooding wealthy countries' — are both empirically misleading and politically unhelpful. Climate change drives displacement through multiple pathways. Rapid-onset events — hurricanes, cyclones, floods, wildfires — can destroy homes, infrastructure, and livelihoods overnight, displacing people from days to permanently. Slow-onset changes — the gradual rise of sea levels, the progressive desertification of formerly productive agricultural land, the salinization of freshwater sources and coastal farmland, the retreat of glaciers that supply mountain communities with freshwater — erode the viability of communities over years and decades, creating conditions that make staying increasingly difficult without making departure feel urgent in.

The Groundswell figures are widely cited but require careful presentation. Narration transcript

The most widely cited projections of future climate displacement come from the World Bank's Groundswell report, published in 2021 in its second edition, which for the first time produced global estimates across six world regions. In the pessimistic reference scenario — no significant climate action, no major development improvements — the report projects up to 216 million people could be internal climate migrants by 2050. Sub-Saharan Africa would face the largest numbers, potentially 86 million people, driven by water stress, crop failure, and coastal flooding. East Asia and the Pacific, particularly delta and coastal regions in Bangladesh, Vietnam, and Pacific island countries, could see 49 million. South Asia could see 40 million. North Africa, where water scarcity is already acute, could see 19 million.

Full slide detail

  • Pacific small island states face existential slow-onset threats: Kiribati, Tuvalu, the Marshall Islands, and the Maldives may become largely uninhabitable this century due to sea-level rise, saltwater intrusion, and increased storm surge frequency.
  • Piggott-McKellar and Vella (2023, Frontiers in Climate): compare planned relocation in Fiji (multiple village relocations conducted) and Australia (Indigenous community relocation from a flood-prone island) — finding key lessons about community consent, cultural continuity, land rights, and governance.

Instructor notes

The Pacific island case is powerful for showing the existential dimensions of climate displacement — this is not about economic opportunity or even safety in a conventional sense, but about the survival of cultures, identities, and nations. Piggott-McKellar and Vella's 2023 case study of Fiji and Australia provides specific, practical lessons about how planned relocation should and should not be done. The preservation of state question — what happens if a state's territory disappears under rising seas — is genuinely unresolved in international law and connects powerfully to the statelessness discussion from Module 9.

Narration transcript

Pacific small island states face the most acute existential version of climate displacement: not merely the displacement of communities within a country, but the potential disappearance of the country itself. Kiribati, Tuvalu, the Marshall Islands, and the Maldives — low-lying atoll nations in the Pacific and Indian Oceans — face a combination of sea-level rise, increasing storm surge intensity, and saltwater intrusion into freshwater lenses and agricultural land that may make permanent habitation impossible within this century. These are not distant scenarios — communities in these nations are already experiencing the effects. Fiji, slightly larger and higher in elevation, has already conducted multiple planned relocations of coastal villages.

Key pointLake Chad: once one of Africa's largest freshwater bodies, it has shrunk by approximately 90 percent since the 1960s due to climate change and water use — disrupting the livelihoods of 30+ million people across four countries.

Full slide detail

  • The Sahel region of Africa — running from Senegal and Mauritania in the west through Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad, and Sudan to Ethiopia and Eritrea in the east — is among the world's most climate-vulnerable regions.
  • Desertification, declining rainfall, and Lake Chad shrinkage (approximately 90 percent since the 1960s) have reduced agricultural land, destroyed pastoral livelihoods, increased competition for resources, and fueled conflicts between farming and herding communities.
  • Climate-conflict-migration nexus: the World Food Programme and academic researchers have documented how resource competition exacerbated by climate stress contributes to communal violence in the Sahel — the Boko Haram insurgency and Sahel jihadist movements have recruited from climate-displaced youth.
  • Climate displacement in the Sahel is primarily internal: most displaced Sahelians migrate to regional cities — Niamey, N'Djamena, Ouagadougou — or to coastal West African cities, not to Europe; the narrative that Sahel climate migration goes to Europe is exaggerated.
  • Arce et al. (2024, iScience): quantifies responsibility for climate-induced displacement by tracing consumption-based emissions in wealthy nations through global supply chains to displacement vulnerability in the Global South — an environmental justice framework with global supply chain analysis.

Instructor notes

The Sahel case grounds the abstract climate-migration relationship in a specific, well-documented regional context. The climate-conflict-migration nexus is important but must be handled carefully: the relationship between climate stress and conflict is real but probabilistic and mediated by governance, inequality, and other factors — presenting it as deterministic oversimplifies. The internal migration point challenges the political narrative that Sahel climate migration is a driver of irregular migration to Europe — the research shows this is significantly overstated. Arce et al.'s supply chain responsibility attribution is cutting-edge environmental justice scholarship worth introducing to graduate students.

Narration transcript

The Sahel region of Africa provides one of the most extensively documented cases of climate stress combining with economic and political fragility to drive displacement. The region stretches across the southern edge of the Sahara, and has experienced decades of declining rainfall, advancing desertification, and intensifying droughts that have progressively undermined agricultural and pastoral livelihoods. Lake Chad — once one of Africa's largest freshwater bodies and the source of livelihood for an estimated 30 million people across Nigeria, Niger, Chad, and Cameroon — has shrunk by approximately 90 percent since the 1960s, a contraction driven by both climate change and increasing water extraction. This collapse has destroyed fishing industries, made previously productive agricultural land arid, and forced competition for remaining resources between farming communities and herders whose traditional pastoral routes have been disrupted.

DefinitionThe Teitiota case (2020): the UN Human Rights Committee ruled that states cannot return people to countries where climate change creates a real risk to the right to life — the first ruling to connect climate change and non-refoulement.

Full slide detail

  • The 1951 Refugee Convention does not cover climate-displaced people: its definition requires persecution by a human agent based on specific grounds (race, religion, nationality, political opinion, social group membership) — natural processes and climate change do not meet this definition.
  • IDP frameworks (UN Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement, 1998) cover internal displacement including from natural disasters, but they are soft law — non-binding on states and inconsistently applied.
  • Chotouras, Fragkou, and Tsadiras (2024, Human Rights Law Review): argue for a human rights-based approach to fill this gap — recognizing a binding right to a healthy environment as the legal foundation for climate-displaced persons' claims.
  • Arumbinang (2025) examines how Indonesia — one of the world's most climate-vulnerable countries — faces critical legal gaps in protecting climate-displaced communities, and argues for digital governance tools to bridge them.
  • The Teitiota case (2015 New Zealand; 2020 UN Human Rights Committee): a Kiribati national who sought asylum in New Zealand on climate grounds was denied but established the principle that returning someone to life-threatening climate conditions could, in principle, violate the right to life — a significant opening in international law.

Instructor notes

The legal gap is one of the most practically significant topics in this module. The Teitiota case is important and often unknown to students — it represents the most significant international legal development on climate displacement to date and should be explained carefully. Chotouras et al.'s human rights-based approach argument is the most compelling pathway identified in the scholarly literature for filling the legal gap. The distinction between hard law (the 1951 Convention) and soft law (the IDP Guiding Principles) matters for understanding why protection remains inconsistent.

Narration transcript

The international legal framework for protecting displaced people has a critical gap when it comes to climate change. The 1951 Refugee Convention, which remains the foundational international instrument, defines a refugee as someone who faces a well-founded fear of persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group. Climate change does not fit this definition: it is not persecution, it is not directed at specific individuals by human agents, and its effects are not neatly bounded by the listed grounds. The result is that hundreds of thousands, and potentially millions, of people displaced by climate events have no specific legal protection status under international law.

Key point26.4 million: people internally displaced by weather-related disasters in 2023 alone (IDMC) — more than conflict displacement, yet far less covered by media and policy attention.

Full slide detail

  • Rapid-onset events (floods, cyclones, droughts, wildfires, glacial lake outburst floods) are the most visible drivers of climate displacement — but most rapid-onset displacement is temporary and internal, with people returning after emergency conditions pass.
  • The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) estimates that weather-related disasters displaced approximately 26.4 million people internally in 2023 alone — a figure larger than conflict-related internal displacement in most years.
  • The Bangladesh case: the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna delta — home to 170 million people — faces compounding rapid-onset flooding and slow-onset salinization; internal climate migration to Dhaka is already reshaping the city, with climate-displaced families identifiable in urban slums.
  • Cascading effects: rapid-onset climate events can trigger secondary displacement through conflict over remaining resources, infrastructure collapse, and economic disruption — making recovery and return more difficult even after immediate emergencies pass.
  • The distinction between temporary and protracted displacement: rapid-onset events initially displace people temporarily; but when underlying conditions (sea-level rise, desertification, degraded livelihoods) make return impossible, temporary displacement becomes protracted — a transition that current humanitarian systems handle poorly.

Instructor notes

This slide distinguishes the rapid-onset from the slow-onset climate displacement dynamics covered in the Pacific and Sahel case studies. The IDMC figure is important and often surprises students — weather disasters displace more people annually than conflict in most years, yet receive far less sustained governance attention. The Bangladesh case is useful for connecting rapid and slow-onset dynamics: the same delta communities face both annual flooding and progressive salinization.

Narration transcript

Beyond the slow-onset processes we examined in the Pacific and Sahel cases, rapid-onset weather events are already the single largest driver of internal displacement globally. The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre estimates that weather-related disasters displaced approximately 26.4 million people internally in 2023 alone — a figure that in most years exceeds conflict-related internal displacement. Floods were responsible for the majority of these displacements, particularly in South and Southeast Asia. The Bangladesh delta provides an exceptionally important case: home to approximately 170 million people, the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna delta faces compounding threats from annual monsoon flooding that is intensifying with climate change, combined with the slow-onset salinization of agricultural land from rising sea levels and saltwater intrusion.

Full slide detail

  • The environmental justice dimension of climate displacement: the populations most vulnerable to climate displacement — subsistence farmers in the Sahel, coastal communities in Bangladesh and Vietnam, island communities in the Pacific — are among the least responsible for the greenhouse gas emissions driving climate change.
  • Historical emissions responsibility: the United States and European nations are responsible for the majority of cumulative historical greenhouse gas emissions that have caused current climate conditions — yet the populations they displaced are primarily in the Global South.
  • Arce et al. (2024, iScience): novel methodology attributing responsibility for climate-induced displacement along global production chains — wealthy consumers' purchasing decisions are traced through supply chains to emissions and ultimately to displacement vulnerability.
  • Loss and damage: the concept, established at COP27 in 2022, involves wealthy countries compensating developing countries for losses they cannot adapt to — and was the basis for establishing a new Loss and Damage fund, though capitalization and governance remain contested.
  • The just transition: policy frameworks that address climate change must account for displacement impacts in the Global South — 'green' transitions that reduce emissions without compensating for climate displacement harm are incomplete from a justice perspective.

Instructor notes

The environmental justice dimension is critical for connecting climate migration to global economic and political systems rather than treating it as a purely 'natural' phenomenon. Arce et al.'s supply chain attribution is methodologically innovative and conceptually important for graduate students. The Loss and Damage fund from COP27 is a significant recent policy development — note that initial pledges were modest and governance arrangements remain unresolved. Faculty should emphasize that climate displacement is not a 'natural disaster' but the result of human choices, creating genuine questions of moral and potentially legal responsibility.

Narration transcript

Climate displacement is not a natural disaster in the politically neutral sense — it is the consequence of human decisions about energy systems, industrial production, and economic organization. The populations most vulnerable to climate displacement are overwhelmingly in the Global South: subsistence farming communities in the Sahel, low-lying delta communities in Bangladesh, coastal villages in Vietnam and the Pacific, Arctic indigenous communities whose ice-based livelihoods and cultural practices are being destroyed. These populations contributed minimally to the cumulative greenhouse gas emissions that have produced current climate conditions. The United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, Japan, and other wealthy industrialized nations are responsible for the great majority of historical emissions. The environmental justice argument is straightforward: those who caused the harm bear obligations to those who suffer it.

Key pointThe honest answer to 'how many people will climate change displace?' is: it depends enormously on how much we reduce emissions, how much we invest in adaptation, and what we count — but across all scenarios, the direction is clear.

Full slide detail

  • Milan, Madson, Farbotko et al. (2024, Frontiers in Climate): systematic review of 30 climate migration forecasting models — finding enormous variation in projections, methods, spatial scales, and what 'climate migration' means.
  • Sources of uncertainty: different climate pathway assumptions (RCP 2.6 vs. RCP 8.5); different definitions of climate migration (direct displacement vs. livelihood loss vs. income effects); different adaptive capacity assumptions; different migration cost assumptions — all compound.
  • The macro-model vs. micro-model debate: large macro-models (like Groundswell) produce aggregate regional projections but cannot capture local decision-making; household-level micro-studies provide behavioral realism but cannot scale up to global projections.
  • What models consistently agree on: climate change will increase displacement, particularly in South and Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and coastal and small-island settings; slower climate action produces higher displacement; the poorest populations are most exposed.
  • Policy implications of uncertainty: uncertainty about scale is not an argument against action — it is an argument for adaptive governance, scenario planning, and investing in social protection and migration infrastructure now.

Instructor notes

This slide is important for scientific literacy: students should understand what climate migration projections are actually measuring and what their limitations are. Milan et al.'s 2024 review is the most rigorous recent attempt to synthesize the forecasting literature and its finding of enormous model variation is important. Stress the policy implication: uncertainty about exact numbers does not justify inaction — the direction of effects is consistent across models, and the conditions for minimizing displacement are clear.

Narration transcript

One of the most common questions faculty and students encounter is: how many people will climate change displace? The honest answer requires understanding both what forecasting models can do and what they cannot. Andrea Milan and colleagues' 2024 review in Frontiers in Climate examined 30 different climate migration forecasting models and found that projections vary enormously — from tens of millions to over a billion — depending on the assumptions embedded in each model. Different climate pathway scenarios produce different baseline temperatures and precipitation changes. Different definitions of 'climate migration' — direct displacement from extreme events versus gradual livelihood loss versus wage effects that make staying uneconomic — produce very different numbers.

Key pointManaged retreat done well: community consent, preserved land rights, livelihood support, genuine power-sharing. Managed retreat done badly: colonial-style displacement with new infrastructure and broken promises.

Full slide detail

  • Managed retreat / planned relocation: proactive movement of communities from high-risk areas to safer locations — contrasted with involuntary displacement and reactive emergency evacuation; can preserve cultural community and social cohesion if done well.
  • Piggott-McKellar and Vella (2023) lessons from Fiji and Australia: (1) genuine community consent is non-negotiable; (2) land rights and cultural site access at both origin and destination must be addressed; (3) livelihood support at destination is essential; (4) governance processes must be transparent and accountable; (5) timelines must be realistic.
  • The community-led vs. state-led tension: state-led relocation programs have a poor historical record (colonial relocations, dam displacement, urban clearance) — community trust requires genuine power-sharing, not just consultation.
  • Climate adaptation finance: the UNFCCC Adaptation Fund, the Green Climate Fund, and bilateral adaptation financing are supposed to support vulnerable communities — but disbursement is slow, conditionalities are high, and the most vulnerable communities often have the least capacity to access it.
  • Preventive vs. reactive: current migration governance systems are predominantly reactive — responding to displacement after it occurs — while the research evidence strongly favors preventive investment in climate resilience and managed relocation before crisis develops.

Instructor notes

The distinction between managed retreat done well and the long history of poorly managed state-led relocations is critical for faculty to help students navigate. Piggott-McKellar and Vella's five lessons provide a practical framework. The climate adaptation finance gap is an important structural problem — the funds exist in principle but accessing them is a barrier for exactly the most vulnerable communities. The preventive vs. reactive framing connects to the broader theme of anticipatory governance.

Narration transcript

Rather than waiting for climate displacement to happen and then responding with emergency humanitarian assistance, a growing body of research advocates for managed retreat — proactive, planned relocation of communities from high-risk areas to safer locations before a crisis occurs. The historical record of state-led relocations, however, is not encouraging: colonial-era relocation programs, dam displacement, urban clearance — all have left legacies of broken communities, lost livelihoods, and deep distrust of government promises. What distinguishes successful managed retreat from this history? Piggott-McKellar and Vella's analysis of relocations in Fiji and Australia identifies five conditions that seem to be necessary: genuine community consent through meaningful decision-making processes, not nominal consultation; attention to land rights and access to cultural sites both at the origin location and the destination; livelihood support that allows communities to rebuild economic security.

Debate

Debate: Climate Migrants — New Legal Category or Extension of Existing Frameworks?

  1. Position 1 — New binding instrument a dedicated Climate Displacement Convention would provide specific, comprehensive protection tailored to climate …
  2. Position 2 — Extend existing frameworks creating new instruments risks diluting protections for existing categories of refugees.
  3. Position 3 — Regional frameworks regional agreements (African Union, Pacific regional frameworks) may be more feasible than global instruments, given …
  4. The political feasibility problem any new binding instrument on climate displacement would require ratification by wealthy countries.
  5. The 'climate refugee' label debate some scholars argue the term 'climate refugee' is counterproductive.
Key pointThe core dilemma: the populations most exposed to climate displacement need new legal protection now — but new binding instruments face the same political obstacles that have limited existing ones. What is the most effective path forward?

Full slide detail

  • Position 1 — New binding instrument: a dedicated Climate Displacement Convention would provide specific, comprehensive protection tailored to climate displacement dynamics — including slow-onset events, temporary and circular migration, and group-based rather than individual protection.
  • Position 2 — Extend existing frameworks: creating new instruments risks diluting protections for existing categories of refugees; better to extend the 1951 Convention's 'particular social group' grounds or develop authoritative interpretation of 'environmental persecution' through existing mechanisms.
  • Position 3 — Regional frameworks: regional agreements (African Union, Pacific regional frameworks) may be more feasible than global instruments, given the geographically concentrated nature of climate displacement vulnerability — and more adaptable to regional contexts.
  • The political feasibility problem: any new binding instrument on climate displacement would require ratification by wealthy countries — the same countries that have been reluctant to ratify the ICMW and have recently restricted asylum systems. Current political conditions are not favorable.
  • The 'climate refugee' label debate: some scholars argue the term 'climate refugee' is counterproductive — it implies a clear causal chain that is empirically contested, and may generate backlash from states that feel their legal obligations are being expanded by definitional creep.

Instructor notes

This debate slides connects the legal gap analysis to genuine policy debate. Present all three positions fairly — each has scholarly backing and practical arguments. The political feasibility point is important for grounding idealistic proposals in reality. The 'climate refugee' label debate is a good place to apply the categorical politics analysis from Module 9 and Module 12 — labels have political consequences.

Narration transcript

The recognition that international law provides no specific protection for climate-displaced people leads naturally to a debate about the most effective legal response. Three broad positions have emerged in the scholarly and policy literature. The first argues for a new dedicated instrument — a Climate Displacement Convention — that would provide comprehensive, tailored protection acknowledging the specific characteristics of climate displacement: its slow-onset nature, its often non-individualized group character, the role of circular and temporary migration as adaptation strategies, and the special situation of small island states. A dedicated instrument could address all these features in ways that existing refugee law cannot.

  • Scenario planning: a strategic planning tool that develops multiple plausible future trajectories rather than a single prediction — appropriate when uncertainty is high and decisions must be made now that will affect outcomes across scenarios.
  • Scenario 1 — High emissions, low adaptation: worst-case climate trajectory combined with slow development progress — 216 million+ internal climate migrants by 2050, increased international climate displacement, governance systems overwhelmed.
  • Scenario 2 — Low emissions, high adaptation: ambitious climate action (Paris Agreement targets achieved or exceeded) combined with robust adaptation investment — 44 million internal climate migrants by 2050; managed retreat programs in operation; loss and damage compensation flowing.
  • Scenario 3 — High emissions, high governance: rapid climate change but strong institutional responses — managed retreat programs, climate migration legal frameworks, adaptation finance at scale; displacement occurs but is addressed systematically.
  • Policy design implications: migration governance systems, legal frameworks, and adaptation programs should be designed now to be effective across multiple scenarios — building in flexibility to scale up and down as climate projections become clearer.

Instructor notes

Scenario planning connects directly to the module's signature experience. Present the three scenarios not as predictions but as planning frameworks — each implies different priorities for investment, legal reform, and governance capacity. The key policy design implication is 'no-regrets' investments: measures that reduce displacement and improve adaptive capacity across all scenarios (e.g., reducing poverty, improving early warning systems, building managed retreat capacity) are justified regardless of which climate pathway materializes.

Narration transcript

Given the uncertainty in climate migration projections, scenario planning provides a more useful policy framework than single-point forecasts. Scenario planning acknowledges that we cannot know exactly what will happen, but we can develop multiple plausible futures and design systems that perform reasonably well across all of them. The World Bank Groundswell provides the framework for two extreme scenarios: a pessimistic reference scenario, in which no significant climate action is taken and development continues on its current trajectory, projecting up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050; and a more climate-friendly scenario, in which rapid emissions reductions and ambitious adaptation investment are pursued, which could reduce internal climate migration to approximately 44 million by 2050.

Instructor notes

The scenario planning prompt (Prompt 4) is the module's signature experience and should take significant class time. Consider breaking students into groups, each assigned a different vulnerable region. The Kiribati question (Prompt 3) is particularly evocative — it forces students to think about migration not as a policy problem but as a human rights and cultural rights issue of the most fundamental kind. The responsibility attribution question (Prompt 2) is philosophically challenging because it asks students to think about diffuse causal contributions to harm and what obligations they generate.

Narration transcript

The discussion questions for this module ask students to reason through some of the most difficult governance challenges of the coming decades. The legal gap question forces a choice between incremental adaptation of existing frameworks — which has the advantage of building on established institutions — and a new binding instrument, which would be more comprehensive but faces the political obstacle that many states are resistant to new binding obligations. The consumption responsibility question is philosophically important: most legal frameworks treat climate change as a collective action problem without specific attribution of individual or national obligation. Arce et al.'s supply chain attribution methodology begins to change this, but translating causally attributable harm into specific legal or political obligations is complex.

Key pointCore challenge: climate displacement is accelerating faster than governance frameworks can respond. Building the legal, institutional, and financial architecture for climate migration is one of the most urgent governance tasks of this decade.

Full slide detail

  • Climate change drives displacement through multiple pathways — slow-onset and rapid-onset — with most climate-related displacement occurring internally, not across international borders; the legal framework does not protect international climate migrants.
  • The World Bank Groundswell projects up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 in a worst-case scenario, reducible to 44 million by ambitious climate and development action — the scale of future displacement is a policy choice being made now (World Bank, 2021).
  • Planned relocation can preserve cultural community and social cohesion when done with genuine consent and adequate support; the historical record of state-led relocations without these elements is largely negative.
  • Environmental justice requires addressing responsibility: the populations most exposed to climate displacement are least responsible for causing it; the Loss and Damage framework represents the first formal international acknowledgment of compensatory obligations.
  • Uncertainty about scale is not an argument against action — robust, adaptive governance frameworks, legal protection for climate-displaced persons, and pre-positioned adaptation finance are 'no-regrets' investments under all plausible climate scenarios.

Instructor notes

Close by connecting climate displacement to the governance themes of Module 14: the institutional architecture needed for climate migration governance does not yet exist, and designing it is one of the most important challenges for the international community in this decade. The uncertainty framing — 'we don't know exactly how bad it will be, but all scenarios require preparation now' — is important for students who may use uncertainty as a reason for policy passivity.

Narration transcript

This module has examined climate change as a migration driver with a scope and character that existing governance systems are poorly equipped to address. Climate displacement is not a future problem — it is happening now in the Sahel, in the Pacific, in coastal Bangladesh and Vietnam, in the Arctic. But its scale will almost certainly grow, and the governance framework needed to respond to that growth does not yet exist. The World Bank Groundswell projections are the most widely cited numbers, but Milan et al.'s review reminds us that all forecasts carry significant uncertainty — what they agree on is the direction.
