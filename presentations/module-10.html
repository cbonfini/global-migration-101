Cities, Communities, and Integration

How do cities absorb, welcome, or exclude migrant populations — and what determines which?

Module 10 of 14AppliedSystems

Narration transcript

Cities are where migration happens. More than half of all migrants worldwide live in urban areas — not in camps, not in rural regions, but in cities that must respond to their presence through housing, schools, health services, and labor markets. This module examines how cities and communities shape integration outcomes, what distinguishes welcoming cities from exclusionary ones, and how refugee-led and community-led solutions challenge top-down institutional approaches. We look at sanctuary cities, local governance innovations, and the urban infrastructures of belonging.

Narration transcript

When we picture where refugees and migrants live, we often imagine camps — large, organized settlements in remote areas. This mental image is increasingly inaccurate. According to UNHCR data, approximately 76 percent of refugees now live outside formal camps — in cities, towns, and rural communities. The majority of the world's displaced people are urban dwellers, navigating the same rental markets, school systems, and labor markets as everyone else. This shift has profound implications for how we think about humanitarian response and integration policy. Camp-based systems — built around food rations, non-food item distributions, and managed settlements — are poorly adapted to urban contexts where the key challenges are access to housing, employment, documentation, and services. The urban transition is happening primarily in the Global South.

DefinitionIntegration is bidirectional: the host society must also change — in its services, institutions, narratives, and attitudes — for integration to be genuinely successful. Policies that demand adaptation only from migrants miss half the equation.

Full slide detail

  • Integration: a multidimensional process through which migrants achieve participation in the economic, social, civic, and cultural life of the host society — not assimilation (which implies one-directional cultural change) but a bidirectional process involving both migrants and host society.
  • Sanctuary city: a municipal jurisdiction that limits its cooperation with national immigration enforcement, typically through non-enforcement policies, non-sharing of information, or explicit declarations of welcome — the term covers a wide range of specific policies.
  • Refugee-led organization (RLO): an organization founded, primarily governed, and substantially staffed by refugees or displaced people to serve their own community — distinguished from NGOs that serve refugees but are led by non-refugees.
  • Social cohesion: the bonds of trust, reciprocity, and shared norms within and across social groups in a community — integration research increasingly measures social cohesion between migrant and host populations as a key outcome, not just individual migrant 'success'.
  • Local integration (as durable solution): the process by which a refugee acquires permanent legal status and ultimately citizenship in the country of first asylum — one of UNHCR's three durable solutions, rarely achieved in practice for most protracted refugee situations.

Instructor notes

Definitional precision matters here because the same word — 'integration' — is used to mean very different things in policy, popular discourse, and academic literature. In policy contexts it often means 'become like us'; in academic research it is measured as multidimensional participation. The RLO definition is important for Module 10's discussion of refugee-led solutions: the governance question of what makes an organization genuinely refugee-led matters for both accountability and effectiveness.

Narration transcript

Before examining integration policy in depth, it is important to establish what these terms actually mean, because they are used loosely and sometimes misleadingly in public discourse. Integration, in the scholarly literature, is understood as a multidimensional and bidirectional process. It is not synonymous with assimilation — which implies that migrants should shed their own cultural identities and take on those of the host society. Integration involves achieving meaningful participation in the economic, social, civic, and cultural dimensions of host-society life while retaining cultural identity. Critically, it is bidirectional: it requires changes in host-society institutions — accessible services, anti-discrimination measures, welcoming public attitudes — not just adaptation by migrants.

Full slide detail

  • Housing is typically the first and most acute integration challenge: migrants and refugees face discrimination from landlords, higher costs, crowding, segregation into disadvantaged neighborhoods, and lack of access to social housing in many countries.
  • Residential segregation: the concentration of migrant populations in specific urban neighborhoods ('ethnic enclaves') has complex effects — can provide social support and cultural community, but may also limit social contact with host populations and perpetuate disadvantage.
  • Education is the most powerful long-term integration tool: access to quality education for migrant children — with appropriate language support, qualified teachers, and anti-bullying frameworks — is the strongest predictor of successful long-term integration.
  • Healthcare access for migrants: irregular migrants often avoid healthcare settings out of fear of deportation; language barriers, cultural incompetence, and lack of documentation create access barriers for documented migrants as well.
  • Gillespie et al. (2024, International Journal of Qualitative Methods): photovoice research with adolescent refugee girls in urban resettlement finds that schools and public spaces are central to well-being, belonging, and peer connection — centering girls' own voices on integration.

Instructor notes

This slide covers concrete, practical policy terrain that faculty can bring into teaching about specific local contexts. The residential segregation debate is genuinely two-sided — ethnic enclaves provide real support and cultural community, not just disadvantage. Gillespie et al.'s photovoice methodology is a good opportunity to discuss participatory research ethics — centering the voices of adolescent refugee girls rather than having researchers speak for them.

Narration transcript

Integration does not happen through policy statements — it happens through housing, schools, healthcare systems, and public spaces. Housing is almost universally the first and most acute challenge for newly arrived migrants and refugees. Discrimination by private landlords is well-documented across Europe and North America. In many countries, social housing systems have long waiting lists that exclude recent arrivals. Migrants are often channeled into the most expensive, most crowded, and most physically deteriorated parts of the housing stock — which in turn shapes children's school quality, commute times, and exposure to environmental hazards. The concentration of migrants in specific urban neighborhoods — commonly called ethnic enclaves — has complex effects that scholarship has debated for decades. Such concentration provides social support, cultural familiarity, access to community networks, and businesses serving the community.

Full slide detail

  • The 'sanctuary city' concept encompasses a range of policies: non-cooperation with federal immigration enforcement, extending local services regardless of immigration status, public declarations of welcome — but the term means very different things in different contexts.
  • Rachel Humphris (2023, JEMS): a long-term ethnographic study of Sheffield, UK, the self-declared first 'City of Sanctuary,' reveals that sanctuary discourse is often symbolic and can be co-opted by governance actors without producing structural change — the gap between symbolic inclusion and material transformation.
  • Delclós, Ribera-Almandoz, and Garcés-Mascareñas (2025, Urban Studies): Barcelona's 'City of Refuge' programme shows municipal actors can build real inclusion for undocumented residents, but face hard limits when national and EU migration frameworks constrain local action.

Instructor notes

The sanctuary city debate is politically charged in many contexts — in the US, UK, and EU it is actively contested. Humphris's ethnographic critique of Sheffield is valuable precisely because it comes from a researcher who took the concept seriously and found it wanting on structural grounds. Barcelona provides a contrasting case where municipal action has achieved more material results — but also hit hard legal limits from national governance. Use these two cases comparatively.

Narration transcript

The sanctuary city concept has become one of the most debated frameworks in urban migration governance. In the United States, sanctuary cities are cities and counties that limit cooperation with federal immigration enforcement — including not honoring voluntary immigration detainers, not sharing information about undocumented residents, and not deploying local police to enforce immigration law. In the United Kingdom, Sheffield made history by declaring itself the country's first City of Sanctuary in 2005 — a city-wide commitment to welcome and include refugees and asylum seekers. In Spain, Barcelona launched its City of Refuge program to extend services and protections to undocumented residents. In cities across East Africa, municipal authorities have experimented with local frameworks for managing urban refugee populations outside of the formal camp system.

Full slide detail

  • Poland received over 1 million Ukrainian refugees within weeks of Russia's February 2022 invasion — the fastest large-scale refugee absorption in post-World War II Europe.
  • Lyubashenko and Wojtowicz (2026, Urban Affairs Review) use Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) across Polish cities to identify the configurations of factors that drove proactive municipal reception: political leadership, institutional capacity, and community attitudes were necessary in combination — no single factor was sufficient.
  • Cities with progressive political leadership, prior experience with immigration, and existing NGO and civil society networks responded most effectively — suggesting that institutional memory and civil society infrastructure matter as much as available funding.
  • A paradox: Poland had some of the most restrictive immigration policies in the EU for non-Ukrainian migrants before 2022 — the rapid mobilization for Ukrainians illustrates the racialized and political contingency of reception policies.
  • Implications for policy: proactive local reception requires building institutional capacity and civil society infrastructure in advance — 'crisis' moments test pre-existing systems, not just emergency response.

Instructor notes

The Polish/Ukrainian case is remarkable in scale and speed and offers rich comparative material. Lyubashenko and Wojtowicz's QCA methodology is worth explaining briefly for graduate students — it allows identification of necessary and sufficient configurations of conditions rather than simple correlations. The paradox of Poland's restrictive policies toward non-Ukrainian migrants is important to name explicitly — it grounds the reception response in political and racial context rather than presenting it as purely a capacity story.

Narration transcript

When Russian forces invaded Ukraine on February 24, 2022, Poland absorbed more than one million Ukrainian refugees within just a few weeks — the most rapid large-scale urban reception of forced migrants in post-World War II European history. How did Polish cities do this? Lyubashenko and Wojtowicz's 2026 study, published in Urban Affairs Review, used Qualitative Comparative Analysis — a method that identifies which combinations of factors are necessary and sufficient to produce a specific outcome — to examine what drove proactive municipal reception across Polish cities. Their findings were revealing: no single factor was sufficient on its own.

Key pointRefugee-led organizations are not supplementary to formal systems — they are often the first, most trusted, and most culturally competent sources of support that displaced people access.

Full slide detail

  • Refugee-led organizations (RLOs): organizations founded and run by refugees and displaced people to serve their own communities — providing social support, legal navigation, livelihood assistance, and community organizing outside of (and sometimes in tension with) formal humanitarian systems.
  • Kihato and Landau (2024, Urban Studies): in secondary cities across the Horn of Africa (Somaliland, Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia), a Cities Alliance initiative reduced host-refugee tensions by integrating forced migrants into urban planning processes — using 'frontstage' visibility to funders and 'backstage' structural integration.
  • Every Campus a Refuge (Diya Abdo, Guilford College): US model in which college campuses provide temporary housing to refugee families — demonstrating how institutional resources can be mobilized for reception without waiting for government programs.
  • Community co-production: the most effective integration programs are designed and delivered with migrant and refugee input, not simply for them — participatory approaches produce better outcomes and respect dignity.
  • Limits and tensions: RLOs often operate with limited funding, depend on charismatic founding individuals, and can be co-opted or crowded out by larger NGOs seeking to claim RLO status for funding purposes ('NGO-ization' problem).

Instructor notes

The 'Every Campus a Refuge' model is a practical, actionable innovation that faculty can discuss as potentially relevant to their own institutions. Kihato and Landau's Horn of Africa case is important for centering non-Western urban contexts. The NGO-ization problem — where larger organizations adopt RLO language to capture funding without genuine refugee leadership — is an important critical lens. Ask: how would you distinguish a genuine refugee-led organization from one that claims the label for funding purposes?

Narration transcript

One of the most significant shifts in humanitarian and integration thinking over the past decade has been the growing recognition of refugee-led and community-led solutions. Refugee-led organizations — organizations founded, governed, and run by refugees and displaced people — have always existed, often informally, in diaspora communities and displacement contexts. But they are increasingly being recognized as primary actors in their own right, not just recipients of assistance. Kihato and Landau's 2024 study in Urban Studies examined a Cities Alliance initiative across secondary cities in the Horn of Africa — Hargeisa in Somaliland, Nairobi, Kampala, and Addis Ababa. What made the initiative effective was a dual strategy: maintaining visible, formal engagement with international funders and government authorities while working to structurally integrate displaced populations into urban planning processes, housing registration, and service provision.

  4. Local vs. national integration: national integration policy sets the legal framework, but local government, schools, employers, and communities determine actual …
  5. What works research consistently shows that early labor market access, early language instruction, credential recognition, and …
Key pointIntegration is bidirectional: successful integration requires changes in host society institutions — not just migrants' adaptation to host society norms.

Full slide detail

  • Integration is multidimensional: economic integration (labor market participation, income), social integration (social networks, community participation), civic/political integration (citizenship, voting, civic engagement), and cultural integration (language, norms) are distinct dimensions that do not move in parallel.
  • Civic integration requirements: many European countries now require migrants to pass language and civic knowledge tests, attend integration courses, and demonstrate 'civic values' as conditions of long-term residence or naturalization — with contested evidence on effectiveness.
  • Country-of-origin effect: integration outcomes are shaped by migrants' education, prior language exposure, and cultural proximity to host country — not just host-country policies; selectivity of migration systems also matters.
  • Local vs. national integration: national integration policy sets the legal framework, but local government, schools, employers, and communities determine actual outcomes — the 'implementation gap' is large.
  • What works: research consistently shows that early labor market access, early language instruction, credential recognition, and welcoming community attitudes are the strongest predictors of successful integration outcomes across all dimensions.

Instructor notes

The multidimensionality of integration is critical for faculty to convey. Economic, social, civic, and cultural integration do not automatically go together: someone can be economically integrated (working, paying taxes) while socially excluded (no friendships with host-country nationals) or civically disengaged. The civic integration requirement debate is politically live — present both the evidence of some effectiveness and the critiques about coercive integration and the barriers these requirements create.

Narration transcript

Integration is not a single thing — it is multiple simultaneous processes that policy-makers often conflate. Economic integration means participation in the labor market and access to economic opportunity. Social integration means building relationships across community lines — between migrants and host-country nationals, between communities. Civic integration means participation in democratic institutions — voting where permitted, engaging in civic organizations, understanding how political systems work. Cultural integration involves language acquisition and navigation of cultural norms. These dimensions do not automatically move together. A migrant who is economically well-integrated — employed in a skilled position, paying taxes, contributing economically — may remain socially isolated, with no meaningful relationships with host-country nationals, and culturally excluded.

Key pointThe political backlash against multiculturalism in Europe conflated two distinct questions: whether cultural diversity is valuable (a normative question) and whether specific multicultural policies produced integration failures (an empirical question). Disentangling these is essential for honest analysis.

Full slide detail

  • The multiculturalism backlash: in the 2000s, British Prime Minister Cameron, German Chancellor Merkel, and French President Sarkozy all declared that multiculturalism had 'failed' — yet the empirical evidence for multiculturalism's failure was mixed and the diagnosis obscured real integration failures rooted in discrimination and economic exclusion.
  • Civic integration requirements (EU): since the 2000s, most EU member states have introduced mandatory integration contracts, language tests, and civic knowledge requirements as conditions for residence or citizenship — with contested evidence that these requirements improve integration outcomes vs. screening out poorly-educated applicants.
  • The interculturalism alternative (Cantle, 2001; Council of Europe): proposes structured interaction between cultural communities as the mechanism for integration — 'contact' across cultural lines, not mere coexistence in separate communities.
  • Religious accommodation debates: accommodation of religious practice (headscarves in schools/workplaces, prayer times, halal food) is a flashpoint in European integration debates — raising questions about the limits of secular constitutional frameworks in religiously diverse societies.
  • The evidence on multiculturalism: Kymlicka and others argue that the policy record of multicultural states (Canada, Australia pre-2001) was significantly better than European critics claimed — and that what 'failed' was not multiculturalism but inadequate labor market and housing integration policies.

Instructor notes

This debate is politically live in many countries represented in the faculty audience. The key analytical move is to separate the normative question (should diversity be valued and accommodated?) from the empirical question (did specific multicultural policies produce integration failures?). The evidence for multiculturalism 'failing' is much weaker than political rhetoric suggests — what the research shows is that integration failures in European cities were primarily driven by labor market exclusion and residential segregation, not by multicultural policies per se. Kymlicka's defense of the Canadian multicultural record is an important corrective.

Narration transcript

The multiculturalism debate is one of the most heated in European politics, and it directly shapes integration policy in every country where faculty in this course will teach. In the 2010s, several major European leaders declared that multiculturalism had failed — that policies of accommodating cultural difference had prevented integration and produced parallel societies. This political narrative was enormously influential. But the empirical evidence is considerably more complicated. The integration failures that critics attributed to multiculturalism — ethnic enclaves, low labor market participation, weak civic engagement — were concentrated in specific communities and specific countries, and their causes are empirically contested.

Key pointCities absorb the human complexity of migration policy — but national governments control the legal framework and, often, the funding. This asymmetry is a structural challenge for urban integration governance.

Full slide detail

  • Migration governance is inherently multi-level: national governments set immigration law and refugee policy; regional/state governments may have their own responsibilities for health, education, and social services; local governments deliver the actual services.
  • Vertical incoherence: when national policy is restrictive and local governments want to be welcoming — or vice versa — the result is governance incoherence that migrants navigate at personal cost.
  • Mayors Migration Council (MMC) and ICMC: city-level networks that advocate for local governments' role in global migration governance, arguing that cities that bear the costs and complexity of integration should have voice in international migration policy.
  • Delclós et al. (2025) on Barcelona: the city's ambitious inclusion program for undocumented residents ran repeatedly into the limits of what municipal law permitted — showing that even the most progressive cities are constrained by national and EU frameworks.
  • Local integration financing: cities often receive less national and EU funding for integration than the scale of their responsibilities requires — a resource-responsibility mismatch that is one of the most persistent structural problems in urban integration governance.

Instructor notes

The multi-level governance dimension is often underanalyzed in migration courses. The Mayors Migration Council is a real and increasingly significant institutional actor in global migration governance — connect this to Module 14 on global governance. The resource-responsibility mismatch is important for policy design: cities cannot integrate migrants effectively if national governments set unfunded mandates. The Barcelona case from Delclós et al. illustrates these tensions with empirical specificity.

Narration transcript

Migration is governed at multiple levels simultaneously, and the tensions between those levels are one of the most important structural features of urban integration policy. National governments control border enforcement, immigration law, refugee determination procedures, and the legal status frameworks within which migrants live. Regional governments in federal systems may control health, education, and social services. And local governments — mayors, city councils, municipal agencies — are the ones who actually deliver the schools, health clinics, housing programs, transportation systems, and community centers that migrants interact with daily. When these levels of government are not aligned — when a progressive city wants to be welcoming but national law restricts what it can do, or when a national government wants integration but local communities resist — migrants are caught in the resulting governance incoherence.

Full slide detail

  • Most of the world's displaced people live in the Global South, often in secondary cities — not in the European and North American cities that dominate migration policy discourse.
  • Secondary cities in Sub-Saharan Africa — Arua (Uganda), Kakuma/Turkana (Kenya), Hargeisa (Somaliland) — have absorbed hundreds of thousands of displaced people with minimal international support, relying on local government improvisation and community resilience.

Instructor notes

Deliberately decentering the European frame is important here. The Uganda self-settlement policy is an under-discussed policy innovation that works in practice despite resource constraints. The Kihato and Landau research on Horn of Africa cities is valuable for showing that host-community benefit is a necessary condition for durable urban integration — not just a nice-to-have. Faculty should challenge students: why does global media and policy attention so strongly disproportionately focus on European migration contexts?

Narration transcript

When global media and policy-makers discuss migrant integration in cities, the conversation almost invariably centers on European or North American cities — Berlin, Paris, London, New York, Toronto. Yet the majority of the world's displaced people live in cities of the Global South, often in secondary cities that are not capitals, not major media centers, and not connected to the international humanitarian system in the way that formal camp settings are. Secondary cities in Uganda, Kenya, South Sudan, Somalia, and Ethiopia have absorbed hundreds of thousands of displaced people through improvised local responses, community resilience, and in some cases remarkably progressive national policies. Uganda's self-settlement approach deserves particular attention. Uganda permits refugees to settle anywhere in the country, allocates small plots of land to rural refugees, and allows all refugees to work and move freely.

Key pointThe most common failure mode in integration programming: designing a program that looks good in a funding proposal but doesn't fit the actual community context. Context is everything.

Full slide detail

  • Theory of change: an effective community intervention begins by mapping the specific integration gaps, barriers, and assets in a particular community — context-specific design, not transplantation of programs from elsewhere.
  • Stakeholder mapping: who are the actors — local government, civil society, faith communities, employers, schools, healthcare providers, migrant-led organizations, host community leaders — and how can they be engaged as co-designers?
  • Participatory design: migrant and refugee community members must be involved in designing interventions that affect them — not just as service recipients but as agents who understand their own needs and resources.
  • Multi-sector coordination: the most effective integration programs coordinate across education, housing, healthcare, employment, and social services — siloed single-sector interventions consistently underperform.
  • Monitoring, evaluation, and learning: integration programs must track not just outputs (number of people trained) but outcomes (employment rates, social network diversity, self-reported belonging) — and build in learning loops to adapt.

Instructor notes

This slide prepares students for the module's signature experience — the community intervention design challenge. Ground the framework in real examples: what does a good theory of change look like for a specific context? The participatory design principle is especially important — many well-intentioned programs fail because they design for communities rather than with them. Challenge students to think about how they would engage migrant communities as co-designers in their own institutional context.

Narration transcript

Understanding the research on urban integration is necessary but not sufficient — faculty must also be able to help students design practical interventions. A community intervention design framework begins with a fundamental principle: there is no universal model of integration that can be copied from one context to another. What works in Cologne does not necessarily work in Karachi. What works for Syrian refugees in Germany may not work for Venezuelan migrants in Bogotá. Effective design starts with rigorous analysis of the specific community context: Who are the migrants? What are their legal statuses, languages, skills, and social networks? What integration gaps exist — employment, housing, education, social connection, legal documentation? What assets exist in the community — existing civil society organizations, employer networks, faith communities, educational institutions?

Narration transcript

Urban integration raises some of the most practically urgent questions in the entire course. Humphris's finding about Sheffield invites us to think carefully about what symbolic inclusion can and cannot achieve — whether it is a substitute for structural change or a necessary precondition for it. The Every Campus a Refuge question is particularly relevant for the faculty in this course: your own institutions have resources that could be mobilized. What would it take? What would the risks be? Who would need to be involved? The Uganda question is one of the most morally significant in global refugee policy: this is a relatively poor country hosting nearly 1.7 million refugees under a genuinely progressive policy, and it receives less international financial support than much wealthier European countries that host far fewer refugees.

Full slide detail

  • Cities are the primary sites of migration and integration: 76 percent of refugees live outside camps, most in urban settings; effective integration policy must be urban policy (UNHCR, 2023).
  • The infrastructure of integration — housing, education, healthcare, social services — determines outcomes more powerfully than national integration rhetoric; local government is the key delivery actor.
  • Sanctuary city initiatives demonstrate both the possibilities and limits of local action: cities can build inclusion within their competences, but are fundamentally constrained by national immigration law frameworks.
  • Refugee-led and community-led solutions are not supplementary — they are often the most effective, most trusted, and most sustainable integration actors available; effective programming is co-produced with communities.
  • Integration policy must center the Global South: the majority of the world's displaced people live in under-resourced cities in low-income countries, and international support must be proportionate to where people actually are.

Narration transcript

This module has moved from the broad patterns of urban migration to concrete governance questions about what makes cities welcoming or exclusionary. The headline finding from the research is deceptively simple: cities are where integration happens, and the infrastructure of cities — housing, schools, health clinics, public spaces, employment systems — determines outcomes more powerfully than any national integration policy document. Sanctuary cities demonstrate both the potential and the limits of local ambition: they can build real inclusion within their competences but cannot substitute for the legal framework that national governments control. Refugee-led organizations and community-led solutions are not peripheral — they are often the most trusted, most effective, and most culturally competent integration actors available, and the shift toward co-production and refugee leadership represents a genuine paradigm change in humanitarian practice.
