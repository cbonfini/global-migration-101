Narration transcript

We close this course not with despair but with the hard-won clarity that comes from understanding a complex system. Global migration governance is fragmented, underfunded, and often fails the people it is supposed to serve — but it is not fixed. This module maps the institutional architecture of international migration governance, examines the Global Compacts and their limitations, explores the emerging role of cities and non-state actors, and asks students to design their own intervention.

Key pointUnlike trade (WTO) or climate (UNFCCC), migration has no overarching multilateral body with binding enforcement authority. This structural gap is the fundamental challenge of global migration governance.

Full slide detail

  • Global migration governance is not a system — it is a fragmented collection of international organizations, legal instruments, bilateral agreements, regional frameworks, and national policies with no single overarching authority.
  • Key international organizations: UNHCR (refugees and stateless persons, established 1950); IOM (International Organization for Migration, 1951, now a UN-related organization since 2016); ILO (labor standards and workers' rights); UNICEF (children); WHO (health in migration).
  • Legal instruments: 1951 Refugee Convention and 1967 Protocol; ILO Conventions 97, 143, 189; the International Convention on the Protection of Rights of All Migrant Workers (1990, ratified by ~58 states); 2018 Global Compact on Refugees; 2018 Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration.
  • The absence of a central governing body: unlike trade (WTO) or climate (UNFCCC), migration has no overarching multilateral body with binding authority over states — this creates protection gaps, coordination failures, and race-to-the-bottom dynamics.
  • Moretti (2020, The Pacific Review): analyzes UNHCR-IOM coordination in the Asia-Pacific, revealing mandate tensions between protection and migration management that produce confused governance on the ground.

Instructor notes

The architectural fragmentation point is essential for students who assume that 'international law' means there is a clear system with clear authority. The WTO/UNFCCC comparison is powerful for illustrating what is missing. Moretti's UNHCR-IOM tension case is a good empirical anchor for the institutional fragmentation argument. Note that IOM became a UN-related organization only in 2016 — it was previously an independent intergovernmental organization with a distinctly management rather than protection mandate.

Narration transcript

One of the most important things for students of migration governance to understand is that there is no 'global migration governance system' in the way that there is a global trade governance system, with the World Trade Organization at its center. There is no overarching international body with authority to set binding rules for migration, enforce protection standards, or coordinate state behavior. What exists instead is a dense but fragmented ecology of international organizations, legal instruments, regional frameworks, bilateral agreements, and national policies that interact in partially coordinated and partially contradictory ways.

DefinitionBurden-sharing vs. responsibility-sharing: the shift in terminology reflects a fundamental normative argument — refugees are not a 'burden' to be redistributed but a global responsibility to be shared, and the framing we use shapes the politics that follows.

Full slide detail

  • Global governance: the complex of formal and informal institutions, norms, rules, and processes by which global affairs are managed — migration global governance is characterized by fragmentation, non-binding instruments, and the primacy of state sovereignty.
  • Burden-sharing: the principle and practice of distributing the costs, responsibilities, and benefits of refugee and migrant reception among multiple states — the central operational principle of the Global Compact on Refugees, deeply contested in practice.
  • Responsibility-sharing (preferred term): UNHCR now uses 'responsibility-sharing' rather than 'burden-sharing' to shift the framing from migration as an unwanted cost to be redistributed, to a shared global responsibility — a normative shift with practical implications.
  • Durable solutions: the three UNHCR-recognized pathways for ending refugee displacement — voluntary repatriation (returning home safely), local integration (gaining permanent status in country of first asylum), and resettlement (transfer to a third country).
  • Polycentric governance: governance systems characterized by multiple centers of authority and decision-making at different scales — local, national, regional, international — operating simultaneously, often without hierarchical coordination; increasingly proposed as the appropriate model for migration governance.

Instructor notes

Precise conceptual vocabulary is important for the capstone module, where students must navigate complex institutional terrain. The burden vs. responsibility-sharing distinction is a good example of how language encodes political assumptions — a point that connects back to Module 12's media framing analysis. Polycentric governance is the key theoretical concept introduced in Thouez and Schweiger (2026) and is worth explaining carefully.

Narration transcript

As we build toward the capstone analysis and design challenge, it is important to establish the conceptual vocabulary that allows us to analyze global migration governance with precision. Global governance refers to the complex ecology of formal and informal institutions, legal norms, and political processes through which global affairs are managed in the absence of a world government. In migration, this ecology is fragmented and primarily non-binding — no overarching authority, no enforcement mechanism, primacy of state sovereignty at every level. Burden-sharing is the operational principle that has organized international refugee response since 1951: the idea that no single country should bear the entire cost of refugee reception, and that the international community should distribute responsibilities equitably.

To close the course with appropriate grounding in reality, it is worth assembling the key numbers that define the scale of the challenge. According to UNHCR's Global Trends 2024 report, there were 123.2 million forcibly displaced people worldwide at the end of 2024 — including approximately 43.7 million refugees, 68.3 million internally displaced persons, and 6.9 million asylum seekers. More than 2.7 million identified cases went unaddressed, not because resettlement pathways do not exist, but because states did not make the political and financial commitments to provide them.

Key pointThe Global Compacts (2018): the first comprehensive international frameworks on refugees and migration in generations — genuinely significant political achievements, but non-binding and inadequately funded.

Full slide detail

  • The Global Compact on Refugees (GCR, 2018): non-binding political declaration adopted by the UN General Assembly (with two dissenting votes — the US and Hungary) — aims to improve burden and responsibility sharing, expand solutions, and strengthen support for refugees.
  • The Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (GCM, 2018): also non-binding; first comprehensive intergovernmental agreement on migration — sets 23 objectives covering safe migration pathways, migrant rights, and return.
  • Triggs and Wall (2020, International Journal of Refugee Law): assess the GCR and inaugural Global Refugee Forum (2019) — finding it the first 'States-plus' multilateral mechanism for burden-sharing, a genuine advance despite non-binding status and inadequate pledges.
  • The non-binding problem: both compacts are explicitly non-binding — states may selectively implement, ignore, or withdraw from them without legal consequence; by 2020, the US had withdrawn from the GCM process under the Trump administration.
  • The first Global Refugee Forum (2019): produced USD 2.2 billion in pledges — significant but far below the estimated USD 65 billion annual financing gap for refugee response — illustrating the limits of voluntary burden-sharing mechanisms.

Instructor notes

The non-binding nature of the Compacts is the most important legal feature for faculty to explain carefully. Students may assume that UN adoption means binding obligation — it does not. The Triggs and Wall assessment is the most authoritative scholarly analysis of the GCR and the 2019 Global Refugee Forum. The financing gap figure — USD 2.2 billion pledged against a USD 65 billion+ estimated need — powerfully illustrates the gap between political statement and material commitment.

Narration transcript

The year 2018 marked a significant milestone in global migration governance: the adoption of two unprecedented political frameworks — the Global Compact on Refugees and the Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration. These were the first comprehensive intergovernmental agreements on refugees and migration since the 1951 Refugee Convention, negotiated through years of multilateral diplomacy and representing an acknowledgment by the international community that existing frameworks were inadequate. But there is a critical feature of both Compacts that determines their practical significance: they are explicitly non-binding. States that adopt them are making political commitments, not legal obligations. They may implement, partially implement, or entirely ignore the Compacts without legal consequence. The United States under the Trump administration withdrew from the GCM process entirely in 2017, before it was finalized, and was not a signatory.

Debate

Burden-Sharing, Durable Solutions, and Their Limits

  1. The three durable solutions framework voluntary repatriation (returning home when safe).
  2. Maple and Hovil2025, Journal of Refugee Studies argue that all three durable solutions are out of reach for the majority of refugees.
  3. Resettlement is quantitatively marginal fewer than 200,000 refugees are resettled annually worldwide (UNHCR).
  4. Protracted situations approximately two-thirds of refugees are in protracted displacement situations.
  5. Vanyoro, Maple, and Vearey2024, AHMR at the Zimbabwe-South Africa border, the 'social life of vulnerability'
Key pointAverage duration of refugee displacement: over 20 years (UNHCR). The 'temporary' nature of refugee status has become a legal fiction for millions of people.

Full slide detail

  • The three durable solutions framework: voluntary repatriation (returning home when safe); local integration (becoming a permanent resident or citizen of the host country); and resettlement to a third country — established under UNHCR's mandate.
  • Maple and Hovil (2025, Journal of Refugee Studies): argue that all three durable solutions are out of reach for the majority of refugees — unsafe home countries, reluctant host states, and tiny resettlement quotas — and propose recalibrating around refugees' own ground-level coping strategies and the goal of citizenship restoration.
  • Resettlement is quantitatively marginal: fewer than 200,000 refugees are resettled annually worldwide (UNHCR) — a tiny fraction of 35+ million refugees globally. The US historically resettled 50,000-120,000 annually; this was reduced to 18,000 in 2017, 45,000 in 2021, raised to 125,000 in 2022.
  • Protracted situations: approximately two-thirds of refugees are in protracted displacement situations — living in displacement for 5+ years with no durable solution in sight; the average duration of refugee displacement is now over 20 years.
  • Vanyoro, Maple, and Vearey (2024, AHMR): at the Zimbabwe-South Africa border, the 'social life of vulnerability' — fluid, context-dependent protection needs — challenges the neat refugee/migrant categories the Global Compacts depend on.

Instructor notes

The 20-year average duration figure is particularly striking and important. Maple and Hovil's critique of durable solutions is a rigorous and important contribution — it challenges what has been the foundational framework of refugee response since 1950. Vanyoro et al.'s Zimbabwe-South Africa border study shows how the category distinctions embedded in the Global Compacts fail in practice. The resettlement numbers are important for scale: when students realize that fewer than 200,000 people are resettled annually from a global refugee population of 35+ million, the marginal role of resettlement becomes clear.

Narration transcript

The international refugee system is built around three 'durable solutions': voluntary repatriation to the country of origin, local integration in the country of asylum, and resettlement to a third country. These solutions have been the organizing framework of UNHCR's work for decades. But Nicholas Maple and Lucy Hovil's 2025 critical analysis in the Journal of Refugee Studies argues that all three solutions are effectively inaccessible for the majority of the world's refugees. Voluntary repatriation requires that the country of origin become safe — but many refugee situations are protracted precisely because conditions at home do not improve: Somalis have been displaced since the early 1990s, Afghan refugees since the Soviet invasion in 1979, with large new waves in 2021.

Full slide detail

  • Cities have emerged as significant actors in global migration governance — not merely as implementers of national policy but as agenda-setters, innovators, and advocates in international forums.
  • Thouez and Schweiger (2026, Social Sciences): document how cities have shaped Global Compact for Migration negotiations, supported Ukrainian forced migrants, and advanced the climate mobility agenda through transnational city networks — arguing for a 'polycentric' governance model.
  • Mayors Migration Council (MMC): transnational network of mayors advocating for cities' role in global migration governance — arguing that cities that bear integration costs should have voice in international policy-making.
  • City of Sanctuary networks (UK, Ireland), Solidarity Cities (EU), Welcome Cities (Germany): networks of cities that have adopted explicit inclusion commitments and share practices — creating governance innovation through horizontal peer learning rather than vertical national mandates.
  • The polycentric governance argument: global challenges that cannot be managed by national governments alone — climate, migration, pandemic response — require a polycentric system in which cities, regions, civil society, and international organizations all have legitimate governance roles.

Instructor notes

The city governance point connects back to Module 10 but now places it within the global governance framework. Thouez and Schweiger's 2026 paper is the most recent and comprehensive analysis of cities in global migration governance. The polycentric governance concept is theoretically important for graduate students — it provides an alternative to the dominant state-centric model of international relations. The sanctuary and solidarity city networks show that horizontal learning between cities is producing governance innovation that national governments have been slow to adopt.

Narration transcript

One of the most significant developments in global migration governance over the past decade has been the emergence of cities as genuine actors — not merely administrative units implementing national policy, but active advocates and innovators in international governance frameworks. Colleen Thouez and Raphaela Schweiger's 2026 paper in Social Sciences documents how cities made their presence felt in the negotiations over the Global Compact for Migration, successfully inserting language about local and municipal governance that was not in early drafts. Through networks like the Mayors Migration Council, the Solidarity Cities network in Europe, and City of Sanctuary movements in the UK and Ireland, municipalities have created horizontal peer learning structures that produce governance innovation independently of national mandates.

Case study

The ILO-IOM Partnership: Rights vs. Management

  1. Foley and Piper2021, Global Public Policy and Governance examine the formal partnership between the ILO (labour standards, workers' rights, decent work) and IOM (migration …
  2. Mandate tension the ILO's 'decent work' mandate requires prioritizing migrant workers' rights.
  3. Partnership in practice joint programs have produced some positive outcomes in specific corridors.
  4. Governance gap migrant workers in the informal economy, undocumented workers, and domestic workers.
  5. The 'decent work for migrants' agenda five pillars — employment creation, social protection, rights at work, social dialogue, and gender equality …
Key pointThe ILO-IOM tension: rights-based protection and state-centric migration management can pull in opposite directions — and the organization with more resources and diplomatic access tends to dominate.

Full slide detail

  • Foley and Piper (2021, Global Public Policy and Governance): examine the formal partnership between the ILO (labour standards, workers' rights, decent work) and IOM (migration management, state services) in governing global labor migration.
  • Mandate tension: the ILO's 'decent work' mandate requires prioritizing migrant workers' rights; IOM's 'migration management' mandate is organized around states' interest in managing migration flows — the two can conflict when migrant workers' rights are inconvenient for states.
  • Partnership in practice: joint programs have produced some positive outcomes in specific corridors; but Foley and Piper find that IOM's larger budget, more diplomatic access, and operational capacity give it structural advantages that can subordinate the rights-based approach.
  • Governance gap: migrant workers in the informal economy, undocumented workers, and domestic workers — the most vulnerable categories — are least well-served by the ILO-IOM partnership, which is primarily designed for regular, documented labor migration.
  • The 'decent work for migrants' agenda: five pillars — employment creation, social protection, rights at work, social dialogue, and gender equality — articulate what labor migration governance should aim at; the gap between aspiration and implementation is large.

Instructor notes

Foley and Piper's analysis is important for students who assume that international organizations with complementary mandates naturally cooperate effectively. The structural power asymmetry — IOM's larger budget and operational capacity vs. ILO's rights-based mandate — is a critical institutional dynamic. The governance gap for informal and undocumented workers connects back to Module 8's discussion of the limits of the international labor rights framework.

Narration transcript

The governance of global labor migration is a particularly revealing case study in institutional fragmentation and mandate conflict. The ILO, founded in 1919 and one of the oldest international organizations, has a mandate grounded in workers' rights, labor standards, and the 'decent work' agenda. The IOM, founded in 1951, has a mandate centered on migration management — assisting states in managing migration flows, providing migration services, and facilitating returns. These mandates overlap on labor migration but pull in different directions when migrant workers' rights conflict with states' preferences for flexible, easily controllable labor supplies.

Key pointNo single intervention is sufficient: addressing only language, or only legal status, or only employment consistently underperforms. Effective integration requires simultaneous, multi-domain action.

Full slide detail

  • The intervention landscape: effective migration governance is not only about international law and intergovernmental frameworks — it requires practical interventions at the community, city, national, and international levels simultaneously.
  • Multi-level intervention framework: (1) international — legal standards, burden-sharing mechanisms, financing; (2) national — immigration law reform, labor rights, social protection access; (3) city/local — integration services, housing, education, social cohesion; (4) community — refugee-led organizations, host community engagement, social network building.
  • Evidence-based intervention design: the strongest interventions combine early labor market access, language instruction, credential recognition, social network bridging, and legal status stability — addressing multiple barriers simultaneously.
  • Humanitarian innovation: organizations like UNHCR's Innovation Service, IRC's Airbel Impact Lab, and the IKEA Foundation's refugee employment programs are developing evidence-based interventions tested through randomized controlled trials and implementation research.
  • Stakeholder mapping: effective intervention requires identifying all relevant actors — government agencies, civil society, diaspora organizations, faith communities, private sector employers, academic institutions, media — and designing engagement strategies for each.

Instructor notes

This slide bridges from analysis to the capstone design challenge. The humanitarian innovation point is important for giving students a sense of what evidence-based practice in migration looks like — the IRC's Airbel Impact Lab and similar organizations have produced important research on what actually works. The multi-level framing directly prepares students for the capstone project, which asks them to integrate multiple governance levels in their intervention design.

Narration transcript

Effective migration governance is not only about international law and intergovernmental agreements — it requires well-designed practical interventions at every level simultaneously. At the international level, what is needed are binding legal standards, equitable burden-sharing mechanisms, and adequate financing. At the national level, immigration law reform, labor rights extension to migrant workers, and social protection portability. At the city and local level, integration services, housing, education, and social cohesion programs designed with migrant and refugee communities. And at the community level, refugee-led organizations, host community engagement, and social network building that creates the human connections through which belonging is actually experienced.

Full slide detail

  • Demographic transition and labor demand: aging populations in Europe, East Asia, and North America create structural demand for migrant workers in care, healthcare, and other sectors — migration is not a problem to be eliminated but a demographic reality to be managed well.

Instructor notes

This slide introduces the forward-looking dimension necessary for a capstone module. The AI/digital governance point is increasingly urgent — automated decision-making in asylum adjudication is being implemented without adequate scrutiny or rights frameworks in several countries. The demographic transition point is important for students who hold a 'zero-sum' view of labor market effects — demographic realities in aging societies make migration governance not a choice but a necessity. The South-South growth trend is underemphasized in most migration courses.

Narration transcript

Looking forward, the governance systems we design in the coming decade will be tested by several converging trends. Climate displacement will increase pressure on migration governance at a scale that current systems are not designed to handle — building the legal, institutional, and financial architecture now is essential. Demographic transitions in wealthy host societies — aging populations with shrinking working-age cohorts in Europe, Japan, South Korea, and eventually China — create structural demand for migrant workers that will only grow. Countries that frame migration exclusively as a security problem are simultaneously dealing with labor shortages, care crises, and pension system sustainability challenges that labor migration could address. Digital technology is transforming migration governance in ways that are both promising and alarming.

Designing Migration Solutions: A Capstone Framework

  1. The capstone challenge design a comprehensive migration solution.
  2. Problem statement clearly define the specific migration governance failure or gap being addressed.
  3. Theory of change how does your proposed intervention address the identified problem?
  4. Stakeholder analysis who must be involved in implementing this intervention?
  5. Feasibility and constraints what legal, financial, political, and institutional constraints must the intervention navigate?
Key pointGood policy design is not about proposing what would be ideal — it is about proposing what is effective, achievable, and just given real constraints. The capstone tests all three dimensions.

Full slide detail

  • The capstone challenge: design a comprehensive migration solution — policy, program, or intervention — for a specific population and context, integrating multi-level governance thinking, evidence-based design, and ethical grounding.
  • Problem statement: clearly define the specific migration governance failure or gap being addressed — who is affected, in what context, with what consequences? Precision here drives everything else.
  • Theory of change: how does your proposed intervention address the identified problem? What are the causal mechanisms? What assumptions does the theory depend on? What could falsify it?
  • Stakeholder analysis: who must be involved in implementing this intervention? Who has power to block it? Who should be centered in its design? Who bears its costs and who receives its benefits?
  • Feasibility and constraints: what legal, financial, political, and institutional constraints must the intervention navigate? Design within real constraints, not an ideal world.
  • Monitoring and evaluation: how will you know if the intervention is working? What are the key outcome metrics? How will you build in learning loops?

Instructor notes

This slide provides the explicit framework for the module's signature experience and the course capstone. Walk through each component slowly. The theory of change question is critical — many student policy proposals are intuitions without causal mechanisms. The feasibility question is important for connecting good analysis to the real world rather than producing 'academic' proposals that have no pathway to implementation. Encourage students to choose a problem and context they genuinely care about — the proposals will be better.

Narration transcript

The course concludes with a capstone: a policy, program, or intervention proposal that integrates the knowledge and analytical frameworks built across all 14 modules. This is not a theoretical exercise — it is a test of whether students can translate understanding into action. The problem statement is the foundation: precisely defining the specific migration governance failure or gap being addressed. Who is affected? In what geographical and political context? With what consequences for those affected and for the broader society? Imprecision in the problem statement produces vague and unactionable proposals.

Case study

Case: What Better Governance Looks Like — The Global Refugee Forum

  1. The Global Refugee Forum (GRF) convened every four years under the GCR framework, with the inaugural forum in December 2019 in Geneva.
  2. Triggs and Wall's assessment the 2019 GRF was the 'makings of a success' — first true multi-actor burden-sharing event.
  3. Gaps and critiques pledges were voluntary and unverified.
  4. The 2023 GRF pledges totaled over USD 2.4 billion.
  5. What genuine progress looks like moving from voluntary pledges to binding commitments.
Key pointThe Global Refugee Forum: the most significant multilateral refugee burden-sharing event in history — and still only a beginning. Progress is real but inadequate relative to the scale of need.

Full slide detail

  • The Global Refugee Forum (GRF): convened every four years under the GCR framework, with the inaugural forum in December 2019 in Geneva — brought together states, international organizations, NGOs, and refugee-led organizations for burden-sharing pledges.
  • Triggs and Wall's assessment: the 2019 GRF was the 'makings of a success' — first true multi-actor burden-sharing event; USD 2.2 billion in financial pledges plus significant non-financial commitments; genuine refugee participation for the first time.
  • Gaps and critiques: pledges were voluntary and unverified; refugee participation was still tokenistic for many organizations; the US was absent; wealthy countries with low resettlement quotas made no commitments to increase them; climate displacement was not adequately addressed.
  • The 2023 GRF: pledges totaled over USD 2.4 billion; 1,600+ pledges from 1,500+ entities; refugee-led organizations made their own pledges as actors, not just recipients; climate displacement featured more prominently.
  • What genuine progress looks like: moving from voluntary pledges to binding commitments; increasing resettlement quotas; establishing predictable financing mechanisms; centering refugee-led organizations as governance actors; developing a legal framework for climate displacement.

Instructor notes

The GRF case makes the abstract governance discussion concrete and shows genuine progress alongside continuing limitations. The shift from the 2019 to 2023 forums is worth noting — the increasing inclusion of refugee-led organizations as pledging actors (not just recipients) is a significant normative shift. Use this case to illustrate what incremental progress in global governance looks like, and what the gap between political achievement and material need still requires.

Narration transcript

The Global Refugee Forum provides a useful concrete case for examining what progress in global migration governance looks like and where it still falls short. The inaugural Forum in December 2019 was described by scholars Triggs and Wall as the 'makings of a success': it brought together states, international organizations, NGOs, the private sector, and for the first time at this scale, refugee-led organizations as active participants. The USD 2.2 billion in financial pledges was significant — representing the first operationalization of the burden-sharing principle that the Global Compact on Refugees had articulated in 2018.

Prompt 4 is the bridge to the capstone submission. The final capstone prompt is for personal reflection — it is appropriate to give students time to write individually before group discussion. The binding convention question (Prompt 1) is important for connecting legal and political feasibility analysis. Maple and Hovil's challenge to durable solutions (Prompt 3) invites genuinely creative thinking about what alternatives might look like — encourage students to think beyond the existing framework rather than merely improving it.

Narration transcript

The discussion questions for this final module are designed to synthesize the entire course and bridge from analysis to the capstone design challenge. The binding convention question asks students to think about the political economy of international law-making: why have states been unwilling to accept binding obligations on migration when they have done so for trade, climate, and human rights? What would it take to change this? The cities governance question connects back to the multi-level governance themes of Modules 10 and 14 and raises profound questions about democratic legitimacy: when a city government adopts policies that protect undocumented residents against national enforcement, to whom is it accountable — the national government, or the residents it is trying to protect?

Key pointThe course closes where it began: with the recognition that migration is a fundamental feature of human experience — not a crisis to be solved but a permanent reality to be governed justly, humanely, and effectively.

Full slide detail

  • Global migration governance is institutionally fragmented: no overarching authority, non-binding Compacts, mandate conflicts between organizations — the fundamental challenge is building legitimate, effective, and adequately financed governance across these gaps.
  • The Global Compacts on Refugees and Migration (2018) are the most significant multilateral agreements in decades — genuinely advancing burden-sharing and norm-setting — but non-binding status and financing gaps severely limit their impact.
  • Durable solutions require rethinking: the three traditional solutions are inaccessible to most refugees; 20-year average displacement durations make 'temporary protection' a fiction; new frameworks centered on citizenship, belonging, and refugees' own strategies are needed.
  • Cities are governance actors, not just implementers — their growing role in global migration governance through networks, advocacy, and innovation must be formalized in governance frameworks that currently exclude them.
  • The most urgent near-term governance tasks: developing legal protection for climate-displaced persons; reforming adaptation finance to reach vulnerable communities; building managed retreat capacity; extending labor rights to informal and undocumented workers — all require political will that is currently absent but that education and advocacy can build.

Instructor notes

Close the course by returning to the foundational themes: migration is not new, not stopping, and not a problem to be eliminated. The governance challenge is how to manage it in ways that respect human dignity, distribute burdens fairly, and build societies that work for everyone. The capstone — whatever form students' proposals take — is the practical expression of this course's ambition: faculty who can teach migration with rigor and complexity, and students who can design real-world interventions informed by evidence and ethics.

Narration transcript

Fourteen modules ago, we began with the recognition that migration is a fundamental, ancient, and permanent feature of human experience. We have moved through conceptual foundations and historical context, through systems and governance, through the human experience of migrants themselves, through narratives and ethics, through future challenges and the governance frameworks needed to address them. What have we built? A comprehensive, interdisciplinary, evidence-based understanding of global migration — one that equips faculty to teach it with the complexity and rigor it deserves. We understand now that migration is driven by a web of forces — economic, political, environmental, social — that interact in ways that no single theory fully captures.
