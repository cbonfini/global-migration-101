Media, Narratives, and Public Perception

How does the story of migration get told — and what happens when it is told badly?

Module 12 of 14AnalyticalCritical Thinking

Narration transcript

The way migration is covered in the news, discussed on social media, and represented in political speech has enormous consequences for policy, for social cohesion, and for the people being discussed. This module examines how media frame migration, what research shows about the effects of negative imagery and securitizing language on public attitudes, how misinformation circulates, and what better alternatives look like. We look at racialized hierarchies in refugee representation, the psychology of selective exposure to hostile media, and the relationship between narrative and policy.

DefinitionEntman (1993): Framing is the selection and salience of certain aspects of reality — making them more or less important, defining a problem, diagnosing causes, and suggesting remedies. It is a form of power.

Full slide detail

  • Media framing (Goffman, 1974; Entman, 1993): the way information is presented — what is emphasized, what is omitted, what metaphors are used, what voices are heard — shapes how audiences interpret and respond to events.
  • Migration has been consistently framed through four dominant media frames globally: (1) security/threat frame ('invasion,' 'flood,' 'wave'); (2) economic burden frame ('welfare recipients,' 'job takers'); (3) humanitarian/victim frame; and (4) cultural threat frame ('clash of civilizations').
  • Agenda-setting: media determine not only how audiences think about migration but which aspects of it they think about at all — the aspects that receive coverage become 'migration,' while those omitted become invisible.
  • The visual dimension: photography and video carry powerful framing effects — images of crowds at borders, boats at sea, and barbed wire activate threat schemas that written neutrality cannot counteract.
  • Alternative frames: civic/cultural contribution frames, human interest frames, and structural frames (examining causes rather than consequences) exist and have been shown to produce different public attitudes.

Instructor notes

Framing theory is foundational for this module and students should understand it as a general analytical tool, not just something that applies to migration. Start with the four dominant migration frames and ask students to identify examples from recent news coverage in their own national context — this immediately grounds the abstract framework in concrete material. The visual framing point connects directly to Sakki and Martikainen's research coming up.

Narration transcript

To understand how migration is represented in public discourse, we need a clear framework. Media framing theory, developed by Erving Goffman and systematized by Robert Entman, argues that the way information is presented shapes interpretation at least as powerfully as the factual content itself. What is emphasized, what is left out, what metaphors are used, whose voices are given authority — these choices constitute a frame that tells audiences not just what happened but how to feel about it and what to do about it. Research on media coverage of migration across Europe and North America has identified four dominant frames that recur across national contexts. The security or threat frame uses spatial metaphors of invasion — floods, waves, hordes — to construct migration as an overwhelming, uncontrollable force threatening the host society.

(2021, Scientific Reports): experimental study showing that exposure to negative immigration rhetoric measurably increases in-group favoritism and hostile physiological and emotional responses to immigrants — effects were documented both through self-report and physiological measurement.
  • Skinner (2025, Political Research Quarterly): survey experiment showing that securitization rhetoric polarizes public attitudes on refugee admissions along partisan lines — while humanitarian frames have more cross-partisan appeal, suggesting framing choices shape political conflict as well as individual attitudes.

Instructor notes

The psychological research is powerful because it goes beyond 'this is wrong' to 'this is what happens in the brain and body.' Mosso et al.'s physiological measurement component is particularly striking for students — it makes media effects concrete and embodied, not merely ideational. The vicious cycle framework from Lueders et al. is important for understanding why media framing is so resistant to correction. Skinner's framing research on partisan polarization has direct policy implications: humanitarian frames have cross-partisan appeal that securitizing frames do not.

Narration transcript

Media framing is not just about aesthetics — it has documented psychological effects. Research by Adrian Lueders and colleagues, published in the European Journal of Social Psychology in 2019, showed through a laboratory experiment that frustrated psychological needs — particularly needs for security, belonging, and predictability — drive people to selectively expose themselves to negative media coverage of refugees, which then reinforces their hostile attitudes, which drives them to seek more negative coverage. This is a vicious cycle with important implications: media effects on migration attitudes are not simply about what people read once but about how repeated exposure to negative framing interacts with underlying psychological needs to create increasingly entrenched positions.

Key pointAlan Kurdi, September 2015: a single photograph of a drowned 3-year-old briefly shifted European public opinion and political rhetoric — showing the power of the humanizing image to interrupt dehumanizing narratives.

Full slide detail

  • Sakki and Martikainen (2021, Ethnic and Racial Studies): content analysis of 1,473 newspaper photographs from Finnish, German, and Hungarian newspapers during the 2015-2016 'crisis' — identified six visual rhetorical strategies used to construct refugees and migrants.
  • Six visual strategies: (1) massifying — showing crowds, erasing individuals; (2) criminalizing — showing confrontations with police, barbed wire; (3) demonizing — using dark imagery, aggressive poses; (4) spectacularizing — dramatic, crisis-imagery; (5) victimizing — showing passive, suffering individuals; and (6) normalizing — everyday, humanizing contexts.
  • Finding: massifying and spectacularizing were the most common visual strategies — constructing migration as an overwhelming, exceptional event rather than a normal human phenomenon.
  • The power of the single photograph: the image of 3-year-old Alan Kurdi, a Syrian boy whose body was photographed on a Turkish beach in September 2015, briefly shifted European public opinion and policy toward increased compassion — a single image challenging an entire dominant frame.
  • Counter-photography: documentary photography projects and photo-journalism focused on individual stories, family connections, and everyday life have been shown to produce more humanizing responses — but require active editorial choices to surface.

Instructor notes

Sakki and Martikainen's taxonomy of visual strategies is an excellent teaching tool — it gives students a vocabulary for analyzing specific images rather than making vague claims about 'negative coverage.' Ask students to bring in a migration-related photograph and analyze which of the six visual strategies it employs. The Alan Kurdi case is well-documented and powerful — the brief shift in European public and political discourse following the photograph's publication, and its subsequent erosion, illustrates both the power and the limits of single humanizing images.

Narration transcript

The visual dimension of migration coverage is particularly powerful because photographs operate on emotional and cognitive registers that text cannot fully reach. Inari Sakki and Jari Martikainen's 2021 content analysis of 1,473 newspaper photographs from Finnish, German, and Hungarian publications during the 2015-2016 European migration situation provides an exceptionally detailed taxonomy of how visual rhetoric works. They identified six strategies: massifying, which erases individual identity by showing undifferentiated crowds; criminalizing, which associates migrants with police confrontations and security infrastructure; demonizing, which uses visual cues — dark imagery, aggressive postures — to construct migrants as threatening; spectacularizing, which frames migration as an exceptional crisis; victimizing, which constructs migrants as passive, helpless sufferers dependent on rescue; and normalizing, which shows migrants in everyday, humanizing contexts.

  5. Zogata-Kusz et al.: Polish and Czech media constructed Ukrainian refugees as 'deserving' through cultural and racial proximity.2023, Journal of Refugee Studies
Key pointLanguage is policy: the choice between 'refugee,' 'migrant,' 'asylum seeker,' 'illegal,' and 'undocumented' in media coverage encodes moral judgments that directly shape public opinion and political action.

Full slide detail

  • Crawley and Skleparis (2018, JEMS): their 'categorical fetishism' argument is as much a media critique as a legal one — the refugee/migrant binary deployed in media and political discourse functioned to create hierarchies of worthiness that justified exclusion.
  • European 2015 media analysis: in the weeks after Alan Kurdi's photograph, coverage shifted from 'migrants' to 'refugees' — a linguistic change that corresponded to a policy shift toward greater reception. The category used was not purely descriptive but politically determined.
  • Racializing migration categories: 'refugee' has in practice been applied more readily to White Europeans (Ukrainians, Bosnians) than to Black Africans or Middle Easterners facing comparable or worse persecution — the category embeds racial judgment even when it appears legally neutral.
  • The 'economic migrant' as political construct: the label 'economic migrant' is deployed to delegitimize claims regardless of the actual content of those claims — obscuring the reality that survival migration from extreme poverty can be as coercive as flight from political violence.
  • Zogata-Kusz et al. (2023, Journal of Refugee Studies): Polish and Czech media constructed Ukrainian refugees as 'deserving' through cultural and racial proximity — illuminating by contrast the racialized deservingness hierarchies embedded in mainstream migration media coverage.

Instructor notes

This slide develops the categorical politics theme from Module 9 specifically in the media context. Crawley and Skleparis's argument operates at the level of media and political discourse, not just law. Zogata-Kusz et al.'s Ukrainian study is exceptionally useful for making the racial dimension of category deployment analytically clear — the differential between Ukrainian and Afghan or Syrian coverage has been noted by many commentators, and the scholarly analysis here provides rigorous backing. Ask students to analyze headlines from the same week from two different publications covering the same migration event: what language choices do they make?

Narration transcript

The political use of migration categories in media coverage is one of the clearest examples of how language functions as policy. When British tabloids shifted their coverage of people crossing the Mediterranean from calling them 'refugees' to 'migrants' in 2015, the linguistic change was not merely descriptive — it was a political act that shaped policy responses, changed public attitudes, and influenced legislative debate. Crawley and Skleparis's 'categorical fetishism' critique applies as much to media discourse as to legal categories: the refugee-migrant binary in European media coverage creates a hierarchy of worthiness in which 'genuine refugees' deserve protection while 'mere migrants' do not — a distinction that their interviews revealed was empirically incoherent when applied to actual people crossing the Mediterranean.

Key pointThe crime myth: research in the US, Europe, and Australia consistently finds that immigrants commit serious crimes at lower rates than native-born citizens — yet this myth is among the most persistent in migration discourse.

Full slide detail

  • Migration is among the most misinformation-prone topics in political discourse: false claims about crime rates, welfare use, disease, economic impact, and demographic 'replacement' circulate widely and are often shared more rapidly than corrections.
  • Common migration myths: immigrants commit more crime (false — research consistently shows immigrants commit crime at lower rates than native-born in most studied countries); immigrants take jobs from native workers (contested — evidence shows more complex labor market effects); immigration is historically unprecedented (false — migration is as old as humanity).
  • Social media amplification: inflammatory, emotionally activating migration misinformation spreads faster and farther on social media platforms than accurate, nuanced content — a structural feature of algorithmic content moderation.
  • Political misinformation actors: far-right political movements have developed sophisticated migration misinformation operations — using social media, pseudo-news sites, and coordinated messaging to spread false narratives while exploiting gaps in media literacy.
  • The correction dilemma: fact-checks can reduce belief in specific false claims but may inadvertently increase attention to and salience of the original claim — research on 'backfire effects' and correction effectiveness is mixed but suggests corrections alone are insufficient.

Instructor notes

Faculty should be prepared with specific, sourced debunking of common myths — the crime rate finding is particularly well-established and worth citing with specific studies. The correction dilemma is important: simply correcting falsehoods may not be sufficient, and there is research on whether repeated corrections can inadvertently amplify false claims. The social media structure point is important context: misinformation spread is not just about individual bad actors but about platform incentive structures.

Narration transcript

Migration is one of the most misinformation-prone subjects in contemporary political discourse. False claims about immigrants' criminal behavior, their use of welfare and public services, their health status, and their supposed plan to demographically 'replace' native populations circulate at scale on social media and through politically motivated pseudo-news operations. These are not minor inaccuracies — they shape public opinion and electoral outcomes. One of the most persistent migration myths is that immigrants commit crime at higher rates than native-born citizens. The research evidence across the United States, Germany, the United Kingdom, Australia, and other major host countries consistently shows the opposite: immigrants, including undocumented immigrants, commit serious crimes at lower rates than native-born citizens.

Key pointThe securitization move: once migration is framed as an existential security threat, normal policy deliberation is suspended — emergency measures become justified and democratic oversight weakens.

Full slide detail

  • The securitization of migration (Buzan, Waever, de Wilde, 1998): the process by which political actors frame an issue as an existential threat, moving it from normal policy debate to an emergency requiring exceptional measures — bypassing democratic deliberation.
  • Populist migration framing: 'the people' (ethnically and culturally defined) vs. 'the elite' who allow migration — positions migrants as a tool of corrupt elites rather than as people with rights and agency.
  • Skinner (2025): securitizing language in refugee policy debates polarizes attitudes along partisan lines — people who already support restrictions become more extreme when exposed to security framing, while people who support admission become more resistant.
  • Humanitarian framing as strategic response: research shows that humanitarian frames — presenting refugees as individual people with specific stories, families, and aspirations — produce more cross-partisan support for refugee admission than security frames.
  • The media-policy link: analysis of European immigration policy from 2015-2024 shows a strong correlation between peaks in negative, security-framed media coverage and subsequent policy restrictions — while the direction of causality is contested, the relationship is consistent.

Instructor notes

The securitization theory from the Copenhagen School is a foundational concept in critical security studies and very applicable to migration. Students should understand that securitization is a speech act that requires both a securitizing actor (who makes the claim) and an audience that accepts it — it is not automatic. The media-policy correlation is important but requires epistemological care: the direction of causality between media coverage and policy is hard to establish, and both may reflect underlying political dynamics rather than either causing the other.

Narration transcript

The concept of securitization, developed by Barry Buzan, Ole Waever, and Jaap de Wilde in their 1998 framework, describes the process by which political actors move an issue from the realm of ordinary politics — where competing interests are balanced through democratic deliberation — to the realm of existential security threat, where emergency measures are justified and normal rules are suspended. Migration has been extensively securitized in European and North American political discourse, particularly since 2001 and more intensively since 2015. The consequences are significant: once migration is framed as an existential threat rather than a policy challenge, exceptional enforcement measures — militarizing borders, suspending asylum procedures, detaining asylum seekers — become politically legitimized in ways that would otherwise be unacceptable.

Counter-Narratives: What Better Looks Like

  1. Solutions journalism a journalism framework that covers not just the problem but the response.
  2. Human interest and narrative journalism centering individual migrants' stories — their names, families, work, ambitions, cultural contributions — produces …
  3. Migrants-as-journalists supporting migration-affected communities to tell their own stories, through training programs, ethnic media, and …
  4. The contribution frame emphasizing what migrants contribute to host societies.
  5. Lived experience in the classroom inviting migrants and refugees to speak in academic settings.
Key pointThe contribution frame in practice: the US narrative about immigration ('nation of immigrants,' 'Emma Lazarus') has historically been more welcoming than the European civic-national frame — demonstrating that dominant narratives can be changed through sustained cultural work.

Full slide detail

  • Solutions journalism: a journalism framework that covers not just the problem but the response — documenting what is working in migration integration, refugee entrepreneurship, community welcome, and policy innovation.
  • Human interest and narrative journalism: centering individual migrants' stories — their names, families, work, ambitions, cultural contributions — produces humanizing effects that statistical data cannot achieve.
  • Migrants-as-journalists: supporting migration-affected communities to tell their own stories, through training programs, ethnic media, and digital platforms — shifting from 'coverage of' to 'coverage by.'
  • The contribution frame: emphasizing what migrants contribute to host societies — economically, culturally, demographically, socially — rather than framing them primarily as a problem to be managed.
  • Lived experience in the classroom: inviting migrants and refugees to speak in academic settings — when done ethically, with proper framing and consent — can be transformative, but requires care: migrants are not obligated to educate, and trauma should not be commodified for pedagogical purposes.

Instructor notes

This slide provides practical content for faculty who want to think about how to teach migration through better media. Solutions journalism is a real movement with training programs and a network — worth introducing to students in journalism or communication programs. The 'migrants-as-journalists' point connects to participatory media and community media traditions. The ethical caution about inviting lived experience into classrooms is important: there is a real risk of what scholars call 'trauma tourism' or treating migrants as objects of educational curiosity rather than as full persons.

Narration transcript

If dominant media framing is the problem, what does better coverage look like? Solutions journalism, a framework developed by the Solutions Journalism Network and practiced by a growing number of newsrooms, deliberately covers not just the problem but the response — documenting what is working, what is failing, and why. Applied to migration, solutions journalism might cover a successful integration program in a German city, a refugee-led cooperative business in Uganda, or a municipal government that found effective ways to extend services to undocumented residents — stories that exist but are systematically undercovered relative to conflict and crisis. Human interest and narrative journalism — covering individual people's lives, names, families, and stories — produces humanizing effects that statistical data alone cannot achieve.

Debate

Debate: Can Media Coverage of Migration Be 'Neutral'?

  1. The objectivity norm in journalism traditional journalistic standards require 'balance'
  2. Structural bias argument media organizations are embedded in commercial, national, and political structures that systematically skew coverage.
  3. The 'both sides' problem in migration coverage presenting a politician's false claim about migration crime rates alongside a researcher's empirical finding as if they …
  4. Journalist advocacy vs. engaged journalism: some media scholars argue journalists covering migration should adopt explicitly humanitarian frames.
  5. Platform governance debate should social media platforms actively remove or demote migration misinformation?
Key pointThe false equivalence trap: 'balanced' coverage that treats a peer-reviewed finding and a politician's false claim as equally valid opinions is not neutral — it actively misinforms. Objectivity requires evaluating evidence quality, not just presenting both sides.

Full slide detail

  • The objectivity norm in journalism: traditional journalistic standards require 'balance' — presenting 'both sides' — but in migration coverage, this can produce false equivalence between evidence-based claims (immigrants commit less crime) and false claims (immigrants bring crime).
  • Structural bias argument: media organizations are embedded in commercial, national, and political structures that systematically skew coverage — ownership concentration, advertiser pressure, audience demographics, and editorial hiring patterns all shape what is covered and how.
  • The 'both sides' problem in migration coverage: presenting a politician's false claim about migration crime rates alongside a researcher's empirical finding as if they were equally valid produces systematic misinformation through the form of balanced reporting.
  • Journalist advocacy vs. engaged journalism: some media scholars argue journalists covering migration should adopt explicitly humanitarian frames; others argue this undermines public trust in journalism as an independent institution — a genuine professional ethics debate.
  • Platform governance debate: should social media platforms actively remove or demote migration misinformation? Or does this constitute politically motivated censorship that suppresses legitimate political debate? Major platforms have taken different positions.

Instructor notes

This debate is important for faculty who teach journalism, communication, or policy students. The false equivalence point is well-established in science communication research and directly applies to migration coverage. The journalist advocacy debate is genuinely contested within the journalism profession — present both positions fairly. The platform governance debate is increasingly important as social media becomes the primary information environment for many students.

Narration transcript

One of the most important meta-questions in migration media analysis is whether neutral coverage is even possible, and whether it is always desirable. Traditional journalism ethics centers on objectivity and balance — presenting multiple perspectives without taking sides. But in migration coverage, strict 'balance' creates a specific problem: presenting empirical findings from peer-reviewed research alongside false claims from political actors as if they are equally valid produces misinformation through the form of journalistic neutrality. This is what philosophers of science and science communicators call false equivalence. When a study finds that immigrants commit serious crimes at lower rates than native-born citizens, presenting this alongside a politician's claim that 'immigrants bring crime' without qualification implies that these are equally supported positions — and systematically misleads audiences about the actual evidence.

Case study

Racialized Deservingness: The Ukrainian Case in European Media

  1. Zogata-Kusz, Öbrink Hobzová, and Cekiera2023, Journal of Refugee Studies qualitative content analysis of 6 months of Polish and Czech media coverage of Ukrainian refugee arrivals in 2022.
  2. Construction mechanisms cultural proximity ('just like us').
  3. The constitutive contrast the construction of Ukrainians as uniquely deserving implicitly constructs other refugee groups as less deserving.
  4. Media coverage matched policy the same media systems that constructed Ukrainians as deserving had, in 2015-2021, constructed Syrian, Afghan, and …
  5. Implications for refugee law the 1951 Convention's protection is based on individual persecution, not nationality or race.
Key pointThe same Polish media that had supported pushbacks against Afghan and Syrian refugees at the Belarus border in 2021 celebrated the welcome of Ukrainian refugees in 2022 — illustrating how racialized deservingness operates through framing, not legal principle.

Full slide detail

  • Zogata-Kusz, Öbrink Hobzová, and Cekiera (2023, Journal of Refugee Studies): qualitative content analysis of 6 months of Polish and Czech media coverage of Ukrainian refugee arrivals in 2022 — identifying how Ukrainian refugees were constructed as 'deserving' of protection.
  • Construction mechanisms: cultural proximity ('just like us'); Christian identity; whiteness (explicitly referenced in some coverage); economic profiles (nurses, teachers, engineers); active agency in resistance ('brave').
  • The constitutive contrast: the construction of Ukrainians as uniquely deserving implicitly constructs other refugee groups as less deserving — the hierarchy is established by the comparison, whether or not the contrast is explicitly named.
  • Media coverage matched policy: the same media systems that constructed Ukrainians as deserving had, in 2015-2021, constructed Syrian, Afghan, and African migrants and refugees as undeserving threats — and had supported the policies that excluded them.
  • Implications for refugee law: the 1951 Convention's protection is based on individual persecution, not nationality or race — yet media and public narratives create de facto racial hierarchies in its application, challenging the principle of non-discrimination that is fundamental to refugee law.

Instructor notes

This case study is particularly powerful because it is recent, well-documented, and forces students to examine comfortable assumptions. Some students may resist the racial analysis — be precise about the empirical evidence from Zogata-Kusz et al. and distinguish between individual intentions and structural/discursive patterns. The contrast with the Belarus pushbacks in 2021 is an important historical anchor. Ask: if the 1951 Convention prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, nationality, and religion, what does it mean that media and public narratives systematically construct racial hierarchies of refugee deservingness?

Narration transcript

The 2022 Ukrainian refugee response in Europe provided one of the most analytically clear demonstrations of racialized media framing in recent history. Zogata-Kusz, Öbrink Hobzová, and Cekiera's 2023 study in the Journal of Refugee Studies analyzed how Polish and Czech media covered Ukrainian refugees in the weeks after the February 2022 invasion, and identified a set of discursive mechanisms that constructed Ukrainians as uniquely deserving of protection. Cultural and civilizational proximity — 'these are people just like us' — was a prominent element. Christian religious identity provided another axis of similarity. Whiteness was in some cases explicitly invoked by journalists and commentators as a marker of similarity.

Full slide detail

  • The media comparison assignment: select three sources (one mainstream/centrist, one tabloid/populist, one community/ethnic/NGO) covering the same migration event in the same week — analyze frame, language, visual choices, voices cited, and claims made.
  • Analytical dimensions: (1) Who is speaking? Whose voices are quoted? (2) What frame is dominant? (3) What metaphors and descriptive language are used? (4) What images are selected? (5) What is the problem? What solution is implied? (6) What is omitted?
  • Sourcing analysis: who are the 'experts' cited on migration in mainstream media? Typically politicians and enforcement officials; rarely academic researchers, migrant community representatives, or service providers — a sourcing gap that shapes coverage systematically.
  • Historical comparison: compare coverage of a contemporary migration event with historical coverage of a past migration wave (e.g., Irish immigration to the US, Polish migration to the UK) — similarities in frames often shock students.
  • Social media analysis: examining how migration-related content circulates on Twitter/X, Facebook, TikTok, and YouTube using publicly available data — valuable for graduate seminars with appropriate methodological training.

Instructor notes

This framework directly prepares students for the module's signature experience — the media comparison analysis assignment. The sourcing gap point is often revelatory for students who have not analyzed sources systematically: when they actually count how often migrants' own voices appear in news coverage vs. government officials and pundits, the disparity is striking. The historical comparison exercise — noting that almost every immigrant group was described in the same threatening terms as contemporary immigrants — is powerful for disrupting the sense that current negative framing is uniquely justified.

Narration transcript

The module's signature experience is a media comparison analysis, and this slide provides the analytical framework for conducting it rigorously. The goal is to take the framing theory and empirical research from this module and apply them to specific, real media texts. The basic design involves selecting three sources covering the same migration event — a mainstream newspaper or broadcast outlet, a tabloid or populist outlet, and a community, ethnic, or NGO publication — and analyzing each along the same dimensions. Who is speaking in this story? Whose voices are quoted as authorities? What is the dominant frame — security threat, economic burden, humanitarian victim, cultural contribution? What specific language choices and metaphors are employed? What images are selected and what visual strategies do they use from the Sakki-Martikainen taxonomy?

  • Skinner (2025): survey experiment (N = 1,800+ US respondents) — securitization frame increased partisan polarization on refugee admissions by 15+ percentage points vs. a humanitarian frame that showed cross-partisan appeal.
  • Zogata-Kusz et al. (2023): six months of Polish and Czech print media analyzed — 'deserving' social construction of Ukrainian refugees appeared in 83 percent of positive coverage, with racial and cultural proximity as the primary framing mechanism.

Instructor notes

This data synthesis slide is useful for faculty who want to assign quantitative research to students — it provides specific sample sizes and effect magnitudes alongside the conceptual frameworks. The convergence across multiple research methods (content analysis, lab experiment, survey experiment, qualitative content analysis) strengthens the overall evidence base considerably. Students should note that experimental effects may be smaller in real-world conditions, but the direction is consistent.

Narration transcript

The research in this module draws on multiple methods and produces a convergent picture. Sakki and Martikainen's content analysis of nearly 1,500 newspaper photographs from Finnish, German, and Hungarian outlets found that massifying and spectacularizing visual strategies — those that show crowds and construct migration as a crisis event — appeared in more than 60 percent of the images sampled. Mosso and colleagues' 2021 experiment with 200 Italian participants found that exposure to negative immigration rhetoric in mass media measurably increased both self-reported in-group favoritism and physiological hostility measures, compared to a neutral control condition — demonstrating that media effects operate on the body as well as on stated opinions.

Instructor notes

Prompt 1 connects media theory to professional ethics and industry standards — there is active debate in journalism ethics about whether migration coverage requires specific guidelines. The Guardian, Deutsche Welle, and some national broadcasting authorities have adopted explicit migration language guidelines; others reject editorial standards as censorship. Prompt 3 on subject-centered journalism is theoretically sophisticated and connects to broader debates about the ethics of journalism about vulnerable populations.

Narration transcript

Media and narrative shape public perception and policy in ways that are well-documented and consequential. The research in this module — from Lueders's vicious cycle to Mosso's physiological effects to Skinner's partisan polarization to Sakki and Martikainen's visual analysis to Zogata-Kusz's racialized deservingness taxonomy — establishes with empirical rigor what critical theorists had long argued: migration coverage is not neutral documentation but a system of meaning-making with real political and human consequences. The discussion questions in this module ask students to take the next step: from analysis to responsibility.

Full slide detail

  • Media framing powerfully shapes migration attitudes: the security/threat frame, economic burden frame, cultural threat frame, and victimizing humanitarian frame all structure how audiences understand and respond to migration — with documented psychological effects.
  • Negative media exposure creates a vicious cycle: frustrated psychological needs drive selective exposure to hostile coverage, which reinforces hostile attitudes, which drives further selective exposure — facts alone are insufficient to break this cycle.
  • Language is policy: the choice of 'refugee' vs. 'migrant,' 'undocumented' vs. 'illegal,' and 'asylum seeker' vs. 'queue jumper' encodes moral judgments that directly shape political action — faculty must help students see this.
  • Racialized deservingness hierarchies operate in mainstream migration media, constructing some groups as more sympathetic than others through racial, cultural, and religious proximity markers — the Ukrainian case makes this unusually visible.
  • Better journalism exists — solutions-focused, human-interest, subject-centered, community-produced — but requires active editorial choices to surface; media literacy education is a necessary complement.

Instructor notes

Close by connecting media analysis to the broader course project of cultivating critical, evidence-based migration literacy. The final reflection — asking faculty and students to examine their own media consumption — is important for modeling reflexivity rather than positioning the analysis as purely about 'other people's biases.' Module 13 shifts to climate and the future — note that climate migration is itself a subject of significant media framing and misinformation.

Narration transcript

This module has demonstrated, with empirical rigor, that migration media coverage is not a neutral relay of facts but a powerful system of meaning-making that shapes public attitudes, political choices, and ultimately the lives of the people it covers. The vicious cycle of media effects — frustrated needs, selective exposure, reinforced hostility, more selective exposure — suggests that simple fact-checking and correction are insufficient responses to migration misinformation. The language analysis — refugee versus migrant, undocumented versus illegal — reminds us that words are not merely descriptions but interventions in political reality. The visual analysis from Sakki and Martikainen gives us a vocabulary for examining photographs with the same analytical rigor we apply to texts.
