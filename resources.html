1 Foundations of Global Migration

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOITheories of International Migration: A Review and Appraisal
    Massey, D. S., Arango, J., Hugo, G., Kouaouci, A., Pellegrino, A., & Taylor, J. E. · 1993 · Population and Development Review, 19(3), 431–466 · 10.2307/2938462

    The foundational canonical review article that systematically evaluates neoclassical economics, new economics of labor migration, dual labor market theory, world systems theory, network theory, and institutional theory. Indispensable as a starting point for any migration theory unit because it maps the entire theoretical landscape in one place.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration and Development: A Theoretical Perspective
    de Haas, Hein · 2010 · International Migration Review, 44(1), 227–264 · 10.1111/j.1747-7379.2009.00804.x

    De Haas synthesizes optimistic and pessimistic views of migration's development impact by integrating the new economics of labor migration, livelihood approaches, and transnational perspectives. A key theoretical piece that shows why migration outcomes are heterogeneous and context-dependent, and demonstrates how paradigm shifts in social theory shape migration debates.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration in the Age of Involuntary Immobility: Theoretical Reflections and Cape Verdean Experiences
    Carling, Jørgen · 2002 · Journal of Ethnic and Migration Studies, 28(1), 5–42 · 10.1080/13691830120103912

    Carling introduces the aspiration/ability framework, distinguishing between migrants' desire to move and their capacity to do so. This article is essential for teaching the voluntary–forced migration continuum and the structural constraints that produce 'involuntary immobility', a concept now central to migration theory.

    Access article →
  • DOIWho is a Migrant? Abandoning the Nation-state Point of View in the Study of Migration
    Tazzioli, Martina & Scheel, Stephan · 2022 · Migration Politics, 1(1), 002 · 10.21468/migpol.1.1.002

    Critically examines how nation-state-centred definitions of 'migrant' embed methodological nationalism and create stigmatizing labels. Faculty teaching definitional debates (refugee vs. migrant vs. IDP) will find this article provocative and useful for introducing students to the politics of categorization.

    Access article →
  • DOIWhy Do People Migrate? Fresh Takes on the Foundational Question of Migration Studies
    Carling, Jørgen · 2024 · International Migration Review, 58(4), 1639–1662 · 10.1177/01979183241269445

    Carling revisits migration theory's central question from multiple angles including push–pull, capabilities, volition, and opportunity frameworks. This recent article provides an accessible yet theoretically rigorous entry point that debunks simplistic explanations while introducing students to multi-causal thinking.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigration Policies Are Problematic – Because They Are About Migration
    Castles, Stephen · 2017 · Ethnic and Racial Studies, 40(9), 1538–1542 · 10.1080/01419870.2017.1308532

    Castles argues that migration policies fail because states try to manage what are inherently social transformation processes, not controllable flows. This piece is ideal for introducing the 'myths vs. realities' theme and for contextualizing why restrictive policies rarely achieve their stated goals.

    Access article →

2 Migration Data and Global Trends

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOIAnatomy of a Misfit: International Migration Statistics
    Ahmad-yar, Ahmad Wali & Bircan, Tuba · 2021 · Sustainability, 13(7), 4032 · 10.3390/SU13074032

    A systematic review categorizing the gaps in international migration statistics across UN DESA, IOM, OECD, Eurostat, and UNHCR. Directly useful for teaching what data sources exist, what they measure well or poorly, and the five categories of gap: definitions, drivers, geography, demographics, and time lags.

    Access article →
  • DOIMeasuring Global Migration Flows Using Online Data
    Johnston, Drew; Giraudy, Eugenia; Chi, Guanghua; Abel, Guy J.; & Bailey, Mike · 2025 · Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 122(16), e2409418122 · 10.1073/pnas.2409418122

    Uses privacy-protected Facebook data from three billion users to estimate monthly country-to-country migration flows for 181 countries, closely matching official statistics. Excellent for teaching data innovation, the promises and limitations of big data in migration research, and the UN call for improved data collection.

    Access article →
  • DOIInternational Migration Review at 60: Evolving and Emerging Models of International Migration Research
    Menjívar, Cecilia; Kraly, Ellen; & Reed, Holly E. · 2024 · International Migration Review, 58(4), 1–28 · 10.1177/01979183241274751

    Reviews 60 years of migration scholarship, identifying seven broad trends including mixed migration, climate migration, and South-South flows. Ideal for orienting faculty and students to the evolving landscape of migration research and how data priorities have shifted over time.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Globalization of International Migration? A Conceptual and Data-Driven Synthesis
    Ha, Jiyoung; Dewaard, Jack; Abel, Guy J.; et al. · 2024 · Population and Development Review, 50(4), 1089–1117 · 10.1111/padr.12686

    Uses country-to-country flow estimates from 1990–2020 to examine whether migration is truly 'globalizing', distinguishing intensity from connectivity. Faculty can use this to challenge students to engage critically with commonly cited claims about the growth of global migration.

    Access article →
  • DOIEstimating Global Bilateral Refugee Migration Flows from 1990 to 2015
    Schellerer, Stefan · 2023 · International Migration Review, 57(3), 1207–1234 · 10.1177/01979183231187621

    Generates the first systematic bilateral refugee flow estimates at global scale, revealing a post-1990s decline and a geographic focus shift from Africa to Asia. Demonstrates methodological innovation for filling data gaps in forced-migration statistics and illustrates the difference between stock and flow data.

    Access article →
  • DOIMisinformation and Its Impact on Contested Policy Issues: The Example of Migration Discourses
    Erokhin, Denis; Komendantova, Nadejda; & Albano, Teresa · 2023 · Societies, 13(7), 168 · 10.3390/soc13070168

    Analyzes how misinformation about migration spreads through social media and shapes public attitudes, drawing on 45,000 tweets. Essential for the 'political use of statistics' theme and for discussing why accurate data communication matters in migration policy debates.

    Access article →

3 Drivers of Migration

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOIDrivers of Migration: Why Do People Move?
    Castelli, Francesco · 2018 · Journal of Travel Medicine, 25(1), tay040 · 10.1093/jtm/tay040

    An accessible, peer-reviewed synthesis of the main drivers of migration across economic, political, environmental, and conflict dimensions, explicitly covering push-pull dynamics and multi-causality. Well-suited as an introductory reading for students new to the field due to its clarity and breadth.

    Access article →
  • DOIDeciphering Interwoven Drivers of Environment-Related Migration: A Multisite Case Study from the Ethiopian Highlands
    Groth, Johanna; Ide, Tobias; Sakdapolrak, Patrick; Kassa, Endeshaw; & Hermans, Kathleen · 2020 · Global Environmental Change, 63, 102094 · 10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2020.102094

    Using Qualitative Comparative Analysis in Ethiopia, this study shows that capabilities and migrant networks—not environmental push factors alone—drive environment-related migration. Directly demonstrates the aspirations-capabilities framework in an empirical setting and underscores multi-causality over simplistic environmental determinism.

    Access article →
  • DOICOVID-19 Responses Restricted Abilities and Aspirations for Mobility and Migration: Insights from Diverse Cities in Four Continents
    Jolivet, Delphine; Fransen, Sonja; Adger, W. Neil; et al. · 2023 · Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, 10(1), 191 · 10.1057/s41599-023-01721-y

    Drawing on in-depth qualitative data from six cities across four continents, this study applies Carling's aspiration/ability model to COVID-19 disruptions of migration. It vividly shows how macro shocks simultaneously reshape both aspirations and capabilities to migrate, making it ideal for teaching migration decision-making frameworks.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Perils of Seeing Twenty-First Century Migration Through a Twentieth-Century Lens
    Massey, Douglas S. · 2018 · International Social Science Journal, 68(227–228), 51–61 · 10.1111/ISSJ.12173

    Massey argues that conventional theories fail to account for twenty-first-century drivers including climate change, forced displacement, and South–South flows. An authoritative critique that pushes students beyond classic economic models toward understanding the contemporary complexity of migration drivers.

    Access article →
  • DOIHumanising Research on Migration Decision-Making: A Situated Framework
    Fresnoza-Flot, Asuncion · 2024 · Open Research Europe, 3(142), v2 · 10.12688/openreseurope.16483.2

    Proposes a 'situated' framework that centers human agency and emotional dimensions in migration decision-making, challenging mainstream theories that overemphasize structural factors. Useful for introducing the micro-level aspiration side of the aspirations-capabilities framework and for raising questions about migrant agency.

    Access article →
  • DOIInvoluntary Immobility and Well-Being: A Scoping Review of Existing Literature and Future Research Directions
    Vanden Hautte, Lise; Scheerens, Charlotte; Ruyssen, Ilse; & Toma, Sorana · 2025 · Globalization and Health, 21(1), 57 · 10.1186/s12992-025-01116-5

    Scoping review of 13 studies on the mental health and well-being impacts of involuntary immobility—the condition of wanting but being unable to migrate. Bridges the Module 1 theoretical concept of involuntary immobility with Module 3's discussion of what prevents migration and with Module 5's mental health themes.

    Access article →

4 Migration Through History

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOIMigration History and Historiography
    Rossi, Benedetta · 2018 · Oxford Research Encyclopedia of African History. Oxford University Press · 10.1093/acrefore/9780190277734.013.325

    A comprehensive survey of African migration history from deep pre-history through the transatlantic slave trade, colonial forced labour, and contemporary migration patterns. Essential for a Global South perspective on historical migration and for grounding discussions of colonialism's role in shaping current migration systems.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Age of Intra-African Migration: Shifting Patterns of Regional Mobility Between Two Global Diasporas, 1850–1960
    Frankema, Ewout & de Haas, Michiel Bart · 2025 · Comparative Migration Studies, 13(1), 19 · 10.1186/s40878-025-00448-w

    Analyses the understudied era of intra-African regional mobility between 1850 and 1960, challenging the notion that contemporary African mobility is unprecedented. Directly relevant to debunking myths about African migration and situating current flows in a longer historical arc of slavery, colonial labour, and post-independence patterns.

    Access article →
  • DOIGlobal Spatio-Temporal Patterns in Human Migration: A Complex Network Perspective
    Laio, Francesco; Davis, Kyle K.; Ridolfi, Luca; & D'Odorico, Paolo · 2013 · PLOS ONE, 8(1), e53723 · 10.1371/journal.pone.0053723

    Examines how the global migration network became more interconnected from 1960 to 2000, and shows that destination choices reflect colonial histories, language, and religious ties. This data-driven piece vividly illustrates how colonial legacies continue to structure modern migration systems.

  • DOIRefugee Policy Amidst Global Shocks: Encampment, Resettlement Barriers and the Search for 'Durable Solutions'

  • DOIDurable Displacement and the Protracted Search for Solutions: Promising Programs and Strategies

    Introduces a special issue covering 10 cases of protracted displacement across three continents, synthesizing what durable solutions have worked and why they remain elusive. Historically situates post-WWII displacement solutions within contemporary crises, illustrating continuity in the challenges facing displaced populations.

    Access article →
  • DOIA European Narrative of Border Externalisation: The European Trust Fund for Africa Story
    Pacciardi, Agnese · 2024 · European Security, 33(1), 91–110 · 10.1080/09662839.2024.2304723

5 Migration Journeys and Routes

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOITorture, Trauma and Posttraumatic Symptoms in Syrian Women Asylum Seekers in the Greek Border Camp of Idomeni
    Guarch-Rubio, Marta; Manzanero, Antonio L.; Roy Delgado, Francisco; Minescu, Anca; & Mohamed, D. · 2025 · Torture Journal, 35(1), 1–17 · 10.7146/torture.v35i1.140862

    Documents high rates of PTSD (78–91%) among Syrian and Kurdish women blocked at the Idomeni camp, linking trauma to both pre-migration violence and conditions in transit camps. Provides concrete evidence for the gendered and cumulative nature of journey-related trauma and the concept of 'torturing environments'.

    Access article →
  • DOI'I Feel Like I Am Less Than Other People': Health-Related Vulnerabilities of Male Migrants Travelling Alone on Their Journey to Europe
    Arsenijević, Jelena; Burtscher, Dejan; Ponthieu, Annick; et al. · 2018 · Social Science & Medicine, 209, 86–94 · 10.1016/j.socscimed.2018.05.038

    Qualitative study with male migrants along the Balkan route reveals how deterrence measures (pushbacks, detention) amplify psychological distress and create 'traditionally invisible' vulnerability. Challenges gender assumptions in humanitarian response and demonstrates how border enforcement policies deepen journey-related harm.

    Access article →
  • DOIBeyond the Border: Clandestine Migration Journeys
    Mainwaring, Ċetta & Brigden, Noelle K. · 2016 · Geopolitics, 21(2), 243–262 · 10.1080/14650045.2016.1165575

    Conceptualizes clandestine migration journeys as a series of cumulatively dangerous passages shaped by geography, state enforcement, and smuggling networks. A key theoretical framework for teaching routes, transit, and the spatial dimensions of irregularized migration that bridge smuggling, risk, and resilience.

    Access article →
  • DOIBad Deals and Tragic Pacts: The Impact of the EU's Management of Migration Through the Externalisation of its Borders on 'Women on the Move'
    Vella, Mary Grace · 2024 · Open Research Europe, 4(188), 1 · 10.12688/openreseurope.18388.1

    Critically examines how the EU–Turkey Deal and subsequent agreements expose women and girls to heightened risks through securitized border management. Directly addresses gender-specific vulnerabilities in migration journeys, border externalization, and the protection gaps created by containment-focused policies.

    Access article →
  • DOIFrom Forced Migration to Forced Arrival: The Campization of Refugee Accommodation in European Cities
    Kreichauf, René · 2018 · Comparative Migration Studies, 6(1), 7 · 10.1186/s40878-017-0069-8

    Introduces the concept of 'campization'—the extension of camp logics into urban arrival spaces—analyzing how containment, exclusion, and temporality are reproduced in cities. Bridges the journey–destination gap and is essential for teaching how route-end conditions continue to shape migrant experiences and resilience.

    Access article →
  • DOIMigrant Deaths in California's Borderlands, 2018–2023
    LaFleur, Marni; On, Kevin; Ceja, Ligia; & Martínez, Daniel E. · 2024 · Journal on Migration and Human Security, 12(4), 234–259 · 10.1177/23315024241291825

    Documents 314 migrant deaths in southern California over five years, linking spikes in mortality to border wall construction and COVID-era deterrence policies. Provides stark empirical data on the lethal consequences of border enforcement, making it highly impactful for teaching about route risks and the ethics of deterrence.

    Access article →

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOIRefugee Policy Amidst Global Shocks: Encampment, Resettlement Barriers and the Search for 'Durable Solutions'
    Spiegel, Paul B. & Mhlanga, Jabulani · 2022 · Global Policy, 13(3), 386–399 · 10.1111/1758-5899.13084

    Analyzes the tensions between encampment, resettlement barriers, and local integration in light of the 1951 Convention and Global Compact on Refugees.   • DOIInternal Displacement (Chapter 47 in Oxford Handbook of International Refugee Law)

Essential for teaching the IDP protection gap.

    Access article →
  • DOIUnderstanding the Crisis of Refugee Law: Legal Scholarship and the EU Asylum System
    Noll, Gregor; Vedsted-Hansen, Jens; & Byrne, Rosemary · 2020 · Leiden Journal of International Law, 33(4), 871–891 · 10.1017/S0922156520000382

  • DOIThe Right to the City for Urban Refugees? Living in the Shadow of the Camp in Nairobi, Amman and Addis Ababa
    Earle, Lucy · 2024 · Urban Studies, 61(14), 2831–2847 · 10.1177/09562478241277077

    Compares urban refugee experiences in three major Global South cities, showing how refugees face the choice between humanitarian assistance in camps and informal self-reliance in cities.   • DOIDurable Displacement and the Protracted Search for Solutions: Promising Programs and Strategies
    Ferris, Elizabeth & Kerwin, Donald M. · 2023 · Journal on Migration and Human Security, 11(1), 1–25 · 10.1177/23315024231160454

    Synthesizes 10 cases of protracted forced displacement and identifies cross-cutting barriers to durable solutions (resettlement, local integration, voluntary return). Provides a comprehensive, empirically grounded framework for teaching why durable solutions remain elusive for the world's 100+ million displaced people.

    Access article →
  • DOIPalestinian Refugees and the Syrian Uprising: Filling the Protection Gap During Secondary Forced Displacement
    Erakat, Noura · 2014 · International Journal of Refugee Law, 26(4), 581–621 · 10.1093/IJRL/EEU047

    Access article →

7 Borders, Sovereignty, and Migration Policy

Peer-reviewed readings

  • DOIExternal Border Control Techniques in the EU as a Challenge to the Principle of Non-Refoulement
    Nagy, Boldizsár & Goldner Lang, Iris · 2021 · European Constitutional Law Review, 17(3), 476–509 · 10.1017/S1574019621000249

    Systematically analyzes how EU border practices—pushbacks, 'safe country' designations, deterrence policies—undermine non-refoulement under EU and international law. Essential for teaching the tension between state sovereignty, domestic law, and international refugee law obligations in a comparative EU context.

    Access article →
  • DOINon-Refoulement in a World of Cooperative Deterrence
    Hathaway, James C. & Gammeltoft-Hansen, Thomas · 2015 · Columbia Journal of Transnational Law, 53(2), 235–284 · 10.2139/SSRN.2479511

    Hathaway and Gammeltoft-Hansen argue that cooperative interdiction schemes between states allow systematic non-refoulement violations by diffusing legal responsibility. A landmark article in international refugee law that illustrates how states use inter-state cooperation to evade obligations, fundamental for teaching deportation and detention policy.

    Access article →
  • DOIThe Emerging Migration State
    Hollifield, James F. · 2004 · International Migration Review, 38(3), 885–912 · 10.1111/j.1747-7379.2004.tb00223.x

    Hollifield introduces the concept of the 'liberal paradox'—the tension between states' desires to control immigration and the rights-based constraints that liberal democracies impose on themselves.
